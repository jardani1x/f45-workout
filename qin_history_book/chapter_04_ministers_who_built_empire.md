# Chapter 4: The Ministers Who Built an Empire

The creation of the first Chinese empire was not the work of a single man, however great his ambitions or formidable his will. The unification of China under Qin Shi Huang (Ying Zheng) was the product of a remarkable convergence of talent at the Qin court—a constellation of ministers, generals, and administrators who transformed a powerful state into an unprecedented imperial system. To understand how Ying Zheng accomplished what no ruler before him had achieved, one must understand the men who served him: their backgrounds, their ideas, and the complex relationships that bound them together in service to a vision that exceeded any one individual's capacity.

The Qin court under Ying Zheng was not a place of consensus or democratic deliberation. It was a hierarchy in which the king's will supreme, and in which success depended on the ability to interpret and execute that will with precision. Yet within this framework, individual ministers exercised enormous influence, shaping policy in areas ranging from law and administration to military strategy and imperial ideology. The partnership between Ying Zheng and his ministers was one of mutual dependency: the king provided the vision and the authority, while his subordinates provided the expertise, the energy, and the organizational capacity to make that vision a reality.

## Li Si: The Legalist Architect of Empire

Among all the figures who surrounded Ying Zheng, none was more important than Li Si. A native of the state of Chu who had studied under the famous Legalist philosopher Xun Kuang, Li Si arrived at the Qin court with a clear vision of how a state should be governed—and with the intellectual tools to implement that vision. His rise to power, which began in the years following Ying Zheng's assumption of personal rule, would parallel the transformation of Qin from a powerful kingdom into the first empire in Chinese history.

Li Si's intellectual background was significant. Xun Kuang, better known as Xunzi, was one of the most influential philosophers of the Warring States period—a thinker who combined Confucian learning with a hard-headed realism about human nature. Xunzi taught that humans were inherently selfish and disorderly, and that only through rigorous law and the strict enforcement of social norms could society achieve stability. This philosophy, which differed from the more optimistic views of other Confucian thinkers who believed in the inherent goodness of humanity, provided a theoretical foundation for the Legalist policies that had guided Qin for generations.

According to Sima Qian's account, Li Si had initially hesitated before committing himself to Qin's service. Recognizing that Qin was the one state most likely to achieve unification, he had sought a position at the Qin court, but his initial efforts to gain an audience with the king had been unsuccessful. Only after the intervention of a friend who recommended him to the court did Li Si secure a position as a minor official—a beginning that belied the extraordinary influence he would eventually achieve.

The traditional narrative presents Li Si as the architect of many of the policies that defined the Qin Empire: the standardization of weights and measures, the unification of script, the creation of a centralized administrative system, and the harsh legal codes that governed the empire's subjects. Yet his role was not merely that of a technocratic administrator. Li Si was also a political operator of considerable skill, navigating the complex currents of Qin court politics and maintaining his position even as other ministers rose and fell around him.

One of Li Si's most significant contributions was his role in persuading Ying Zheng to adopt the title of "First Emperor" (始皇帝, Shi Huangdi) following the unification of China in 221 BCE. According to the *Records of the Grand Historian*, Li Si proposed the new title as a way of signifying the unprecedented nature of Ying Zheng's achievement. The title combined the ancient Zhou term for sovereign (帝) with the more recent title of "king" (王), creating a designation that suggested both ancient legitimacy and new authority. Moreover, Li Si proposed that the title should be hereditary, passed to the emperor's descendants as "Second Emperor" (二皇帝), "Third Emperor" (三皇帝), and so on—an arrangement that would have established a permanent imperial dynasty.

The proposal to adopt the title of "First Emperor" was more than a mere formality; it was a statement of political philosophy. By creating a title that was explicitly new and unprecedented, Li Si was acknowledging that Ying Zheng had accomplished something that exceeded the achievements of all previous rulers. At the same time, the hereditary arrangement reflected a desire to establish permanence and continuity—a dynasty that would last forever, in theory if not in practice.

Li Si's influence extended to the darker aspects of Qin governance as well. He was a strong advocate of the policy known as "burning the books and burying the scholars" (焚书坑儒), which in 213 BCE ordered the destruction of all philosophical texts except those dealing with medicine, agriculture, and divination, and which resulted in the execution of some four hundred scholars who had criticized the government. Sima Qian, writing from a Confucian perspective that viewed this policy as one of the great crimes of the Qin dynasty, presents Li Si as the driving force behind the edict. Modern scholars have debated whether Li Si was truly the author of this policy or whether it was driven by the emperor's own paranoia, but there can be little doubt that Li Si, as chief minister, bore significant responsibility for its implementation.

The relationship between Li Si and Ying Zheng was one of the closest in the emperor's court. Unlike Lü Buwei, who had dominated the young king during the regency, Li Si always maintained the appearance of subordinate loyalty while exercising enormous practical influence. This delicate balance was maintained until the end of the First Emperor's reign, when Li Si became entangled in the political struggles that followed the emperor's death—a demise that would ultimately lead to Li Si's own execution in 208 BCE.

## Wang Jian: The General Who Conquered the South

If Li Si was the architect of the Qin Empire's domestic policies, Wang Jian was the general who extended its boundaries to their furthest extent. A career military officer who had risen through the ranks of the Qin army, Wang Jian commanded the campaigns that destroyed the state of Chu and brought the southern regions of what is now China under imperial control.

The conquest of Chu was the largest military operation of the unification wars. The state of Chu occupied a vast territory in the Yangtze River basin, stretching from the central plains to the coast of what is now Jiangsu Province. It was one of the oldest and most culturally sophisticated of the Warring States, and its military forces were substantial. To defeat Chu would require not merely numerical superiority but also careful planning, logistical expertise, and the ability to adapt to unfamiliar terrain and climate.

According to Sima Qian's account, there was significant debate in the Qin court over how to approach the conquest of Chu. Wang Jian initially proposed a massive commitment of resources—sixty thousand troops and years of preparation—but other generals, including Li You, argued for a smaller, more cautious approach. Ying Zheng ultimately sided with Wang Jian, appointing him to command the major campaign that began in 224 BCE.

The campaign against Chu was a masterpiece of military organization. Wang Jian's forces advanced along multiple axes, coordinating the movements of different armies to divide and confuse the Chu defenders. The Qin troops, trained in the harsh discipline of the Qin military system, proved far superior to the Chu forces in both organization and morale. By 223 BCE, the Chu capital of Jiangling had fallen, and the state of Chu had ceased to exist as an independent political entity.

Following the conquest of Chu, Wang Jian turned his attention to the southern regions that had previously been outside the Zhou world-system. In 222 BCE, he led expeditions into what is now Fujian and Zhejiang Provinces, conquering the ancient kingdom of Minyue and extending imperial authority to the coast of the East China Sea. These campaigns, which brought the First Emperor's realm to its maximum extent, were among the most geographically ambitious of the unification wars.

Wang Jian's success was not merely a matter of military skill; it also reflected the adaptability of the Qin system. The general was able to integrate conquered populations into the imperial structure, establishing administrative centers and appointing officials to govern the new territories. This capacity for absorption and administration distinguished Qin from earlier conquerors who had been content to exact tribute without establishing direct control.

## Meng Tian: The General Who Unified the North

While Wang Jian was conquering the south, another general—Meng Tian—was carrying out an equally ambitious campaign in the north. Meng Tian's primary responsibility was the defense of the empire against the nomadic peoples of the Mongolian steppe, known in Chinese sources as the Xiongnu. His campaigns, which extended the empire's northern frontier to the line of the Great Wall, were among the most strategically significant of the Qin period.

The Great Wall itself was one of the most enormous construction projects in ancient history. Begun under the First Emperor's orders in the 210s BCE, the wall stretched over thousands of miles, connecting and extending earlier fortifications built by the various Warring States to create a unified defensive line against northern incursion. The project required the mobilization of hundreds of thousands of workers, including soldiers, peasants, and convicts, and it entailed enormous human suffering. Traditional sources speak of hundreds of thousands of workers dying during the construction, their bodies incorporated into the wall itself—a graphic symbol of the human cost of imperial ambition.

Meng Tian commanded the forces that conducted the initial campaigns against the Xiongnu and oversaw the construction of the northern fortifications. According to the *Records of the Grand Historian*, he was appointed to his command because of his skill in dealing with nomadic adversaries—a skill that derived from his family's long experience with frontier warfare. The Meng family had served the Qin state for generations as military commanders, and they possessed the expertise necessary to organize campaigns against enemies who fought with cavalry tactics quite different from those of the settled Chinese states.

The northern campaigns were largely successful. By the end of the First Emperor's reign, the Xiongnu had been pushed back beyond the Great Wall, and the northern frontier had been stabilized. Yet this success was achieved at enormous cost—not merely in the lives of soldiers and workers, but also in the diversion of resources that might have been used for other purposes. The Great Wall would remain a defining feature of the Chinese frontier for millennia, but its construction was also a symptom of the imperial system's inability to permanently subdue the nomadic peoples of the steppe.

## Li You and the Transformation of the Military

While Li Si, Wang Jian, and Meng Tian were the most prominent figures in the First Emperor's court, they were by no means the only important ministers. The Qin government was staffed by hundreds of officials, many of whom played crucial roles in specific areas of policy. Among these, Li You stands out as a figure who combined military command with administrative responsibility in ways that shaped the development of the empire.

Li You was appointed as chief minister (丞相) following the death of Li Si's rival, Wei Gao, in 208 BCE—a position that made him one of the two highest officials in the empire. Unlike Li Si, who was primarily an administrator and policy thinker, Li You had significant military experience, having commanded campaigns against the state of Zhao in the 230s BCE.

The relationship between Li You and Li Si was complex. Traditional sources present them as rivals, competing for influence with the emperor and pursuing different visions of imperial policy. According to Sima Qian, Li You opposed some of Li Si's proposals, including the harsh policies toward scholars that culminated in the burning of the books. This opposition, however, did not prevent Li You from serving the emperor loyally or from participating in the implementation of policies with which he might have disagreed.

Li You's importance in the Qin system reflected a broader pattern: the integration of military and administrative functions under the control of the central government. Unlike in some Warring States, where military commanders often operated with considerable autonomy, the Qin system ensured that generals were subordinate to civil authority. Even the most successful commanders served at the pleasure of the emperor and could be recalled or replaced at any time. This centralization of control was essential to the unification wars, as it allowed the king to coordinate campaigns across multiple fronts and to ensure that military resources were allocated efficiently.

## The Meritocratic System and the Transformation of Governance

The ministers who served Ying Zheng shared certain characteristics that distinguished them from the aristocratic officials who dominated other Warring States. Most were men of relatively humble origins who had risen through the Qin system of meritocratic selection—a system that evaluated candidates based on their abilities rather than their birth. This commitment to meritocracy was a hallmark of Qin governance, and it helps explain why the Qin court was able to attract talent from across the Warring States.

The Qin system of official selection was based on a combination of recommendations, examinations, and practical experience. Candidates for office were evaluated by their local officials and by senior ministers at the court, and they were promoted based on their performance in specific tasks. This system, which had been developed over generations of Qin rulers, was fundamentally different from the hereditary aristocratic systems that prevailed in other states, where positions of authority were passed down within noble families.

The meritocratic principle was not entirely without limits. The Qin system still favored members of the royal family and established aristocratic families in certain contexts, and it was not a true system of open competition in the modern sense. Yet it was far more open than the aristocratic systems of the central plains, and it provided a pathway for talented individuals of humble origins to rise to positions of great influence.

This openness was essential to Qin's success. By drawing talent from across the Warring States, the Qin court was able to benefit from a wide range of perspectives and expertise. Li Si came from Chu; Wang Jian may have had origins in other states; Meng Tian's family had frontier connections that gave them unique expertise. Each brought different experiences and skills to the court, enriching the government's capacity for both administration and warfare.

The partnership between Ying Zheng and his ministers was also shaped by the Legalist philosophy that dominated Qin political thought. Legalism taught that good governance required clear laws, harsh punishments, and the absolute authority of the ruler. Ministers were not counselors who advised the king on the basis of moral principle; they were administrators who implemented the king's will through the machinery of the state. This framework created a relationship that was both hierarchical and functional: the king gave orders, and the ministers executed them.

Yet this relationship was more complex than simple obedience. Within the framework of the king's authority, ministers exercised considerable discretion in how they implemented policy. Li Si, in particular, was able to shape the development of imperial policy in ways that went beyond mere execution of the king's orders. The First Emperor was not a puppet; he was an active ruler who made fundamental decisions about the direction of the empire. But he was also dependent on his ministers for the expertise and energy necessary to govern a vast and diverse realm.

## The Limits of Ministerial Influence

Despite their importance, the ministers of the First Emperor's court operated within strict limits. The king remained the ultimate source of authority, and any minister who was perceived as threatening the ruler's supremacy was vulnerable to removal. This pattern was established early in Ying Zheng's reign, with the fall of Lü Buwei, and it continued throughout the First Emperor's rule.

The fate of individual ministers often depended on their ability to maintain the king's confidence. Li Si, who served for nearly three decades, was the outstanding example of ministerial longevity—yet even he was not immune to the periodic fluctuations in royal favor that characterized the Qin court. Other ministers rose and fell with greater frequency, their careers dependent on their performance in specific tasks and their ability to navigate the political currents of the court.

This pattern reflected the fundamentally personal nature of Qin governance. Unlike modern bureaucratic systems, which operate through institutional structures that persist regardless of individual officeholders, the Qin system depended heavily on the relationship between the ruler and individual ministers. The king's will was the driving force of government, and the ministers were instruments of that will. When the king was strong and decisive, the system worked effectively; when the king was weak or indecisive, the system was vulnerable to fragmentation.

The unification of China in 221 BCE created new challenges for this system. The empire was far larger and more complex than the kingdom had ever been, and the demands on the government increased correspondingly. The ministers who had served Ying Zheng well in the context of a single state now faced the task of governing a vast territory that encompassed former enemies and culturally diverse populations. Their success in meeting this challenge would determine whether the Qin Empire would survive beyond the lifetime of its founder.

## The Legacy of the First Emperor's Ministers

The ministers of the First Emperor's court left a legacy that extended far beyond their own lifetimes. The administrative systems they created—the standardized weights and measures, the unified script, the network of roads and administrative centers—became the foundations of Chinese imperial governance for millennia. The Great Wall they constructed became a symbol of Chinese civilization's boundary with the nomadic world. The legal codes they developed, despite their harshness, established precedents that would influence Chinese law for centuries.

Yet this legacy was complicated by the eventual collapse of the Qin dynasty. Within four years of the First Emperor's death in 210 BCE, the empire had fractured into rebellion, and the dynasty that was supposed to last forever had been replaced by the Han. The ministers who had served the First Emperor were blamed by later historians for the dynasty's failures, accused of having led the emperor astray and having implemented policies that were too harsh to maintain order.

This posthumous condemnation was not entirely fair. The First Emperor was the ultimate decision-maker, and the ministers served at his pleasure. Yet the traditional narrative, shaped by Confucian historians who viewed Qin as a morally bankrupt tyranny, tended to focus on the ministers rather than on the emperor himself. Li Si, in particular, became a symbol of the dangers of Legalist philosophy—a warning about what could happen when power fell into the hands of clever but unscrupulous advisors.

Modern scholarship has begun to revise this picture. By examining archaeological evidence, including the bamboo slips discovered at Shuihudi and other sites, scholars have gained new insights into the actual practices of Qin governance. These sources suggest that the Qin system was more complex and nuanced than the traditional account allows—that the harsh laws were not applied uniformly, and that local officials often exercised discretion in ways that moderated the system's worst excesses.

The ministers who served Qin Shi Huang were neither saints nor demons. They were human beings who served a ruler of extraordinary ambition and will, doing their best to implement his vision while navigating the complexities of court politics and the challenges of governing an empire. Their achievements were immense: the unification of China, the creation of administrative systems that would endure for millennia, and the transformation of the Chinese world in ways that continue to shape it today. Their failures were also significant: the policies that alienated populations, the repression that generated resentment, and the inability to ensure a smooth succession that might have preserved the dynasty. In the end, the story of the First Emperor's ministers is the story of the Qin Empire itself—ambitious, brilliant, flawed, and monumental.

### Chapter Notes

1. **Li Si's Origins**: There has been scholarly debate about Li Si's birthplace. Some sources suggest he was from Chu; others indicate he may have been from Cai. The traditional account in Sima Qian identifies him as a native of Chu, which would explain his initial hesitation to serve Qin—a state that had fought numerous wars with Chu.

2. **The Title of "First Emperor"**: The proposal to adopt the title of "First Emperor" is attributed to Li Si in Sima Qian's account, but some scholars have suggested that the idea may have originated with the king himself. The title represented a deliberate break with the past while also invoking ancient legitimacy.

3. **Wang Jian's Campaigns**: The conquest of Chu was not the straightforward victory that the traditional narrative suggests. Archaeological evidence indicates that resistance continued for several years after the fall of the Chu capital, and that the Qin authorities faced significant challenges in administering the conquered territory.

4. **Meng Tian and the Great Wall**: The traditional account states that Meng Tian committed suicide after being ordered to abandon his command, but some scholars have suggested that he may have been executed as part of the political purges that followed the First Emperor's death.

5. **The Meritocratic System**: The extent of Qin's meritocracy has been debated by modern scholars. While the Qin system was more open than hereditary aristocratic systems, it was not a true system of equal opportunity. Family connections and royal favor still played important roles in determining who rose to positions of influence.
