# Chapter 17: The Six States Fall — A Chronology of Conquest

The unification of China under Qin in 221 BCE was the culmination of a series of military campaigns that had systematically destroyed the great powers of the Warring States era. Between 230 and 221 BCE, Qin waged a coordinated campaign of conquest that eliminated Han, Zhao, Wei, Chu, Yan, and Qi from the political map of China. This chapter provides a systematic overview of these campaigns, examining the timeline, sequence, and methods of conquest. By tracing the fall of each state in chronological order, we can begin to identify the patterns that characterized Qin's approach to unification and understand why the conquest proceeded with such remarkable speed.

The story of Qin's conquests is not merely a military narrative; it is also a story of political strategy, diplomatic manipulation, and the calculated exploitation of enemy weaknesses. The Qin rulers and their generals did not simply rely on brute force; they employed a sophisticated combination of military pressure, economic coercion, and diplomatic subversion to achieve their objectives. Understanding these methods is essential to grasping how a single state could overcome six major rivals and create the first unified empire in Chinese history.

## The Strategic Context: Qin at the Dawn of Conquest

Before examining the individual campaigns, it is necessary to understand the strategic context in which they took place. By the late third century BCE, Qin had established clear military superiority over all of its rivals. This superiority was the product of decades of reform, economic development, and military modernization under a series of capable rulers. The Legalist reforms of Shang Yang, the administrative innovations of Li Si, and the military transformations of successive Qin kings had created a war machine that was unmatched in the Warring States world.

The strategic situation in 230 BCE, when the conquests began, was favorable to Qin. The major states had been weakened by decades of intermittent warfare, and none possessed the resources or the political cohesion to mount a coordinated resistance. The coalition diplomacy that had occasionally threatened Qin in the past had collapsed, leaving each state to face Qin's armies alone. This isolation of the potential targets was itself a Qin achievement, the result of careful diplomatic maneuvering that had driven wedges between the states.

Qin's position was also strengthened by its geographic advantages. The Qin heartland in the Wei River Valley was naturally defensible, protected by mountains and the Yellow River. From this secure base, Qin could launch campaigns in any direction, projecting power into the Central Plains while maintaining its lines of communication and supply. This geographic advantage allowed Qin to fight prolonged campaigns without the risks that would have attended a less secure position.

## The Fall of Han (230 BCE)

The conquest of Han in 230 BCE marked the beginning of the final phase of Warring States warfare. Han was the smallest of the major states, and its strategic position— sandwiched between Qin, Zhao, Wei, and Chu—made it particularly vulnerable to pressure from all directions. By the late third century BCE, Han had been significantly weakened by territorial losses to its more powerful neighbors, and it lacked the resources to mount effective resistance.

The Qin campaign against Han was led by the general Xin (恆), whose full name and background are unclear in the historical record. The campaign was relatively straightforward: Qin forces advanced into Han territory from the west, overwhelming the limited Han defenses and capturing the capital of Xianyang (陽翟). The king of Han was captured and executed, and the state was annexed into Qin. According to the *Shiji*, the conquest of Han required only about a year, reflecting the state's extreme weakness (Sima Qian, *Shiji*).

The fall of Han had important strategic implications. It eliminated the state that had long served as a buffer between Qin and the other major powers of the Central Plains. With Han eliminated, Qin now shared borders with Zhao, Wei, and Chu, putting it in a position to apply direct pressure on each of these states. The conquest of Han also provided Qin with valuable territorial gains and resources that could be used to support further campaigns.

## The Destruction of Zhao (228-222 BCE)

The conquest of Zhao was one of the longest and most difficult campaigns of the unification wars. Zhao was a major military power, famous for its cavalry and its able commanders, and it had resisted Qin aggression for decades. The campaign against Zhao would require sustained effort and would involve some of the bloodiest battles of the entire unification period.

The first major phase of the campaign began in 228 BCE, following the conquest of Han. Qin forces, commanded by Wang Jian and later by his son Wang Ben, invaded Zhao from the west and south. The initial campaign was successful, and the Qin forces advanced to the Zhao capital of Handan (邯鄲), which fell in 228 BCE. The Zhao king was captured, and the main Zhao state was annexed.

However, the conquest of the Zhao capital did not end Zhao resistance. A remnant of the Zhao royal family, led by a prince who escaped the initial conquest, established a rump state in the northern region of Dai (代). This remnant continued to resist Qin rule for several years, supported by the rugged terrain of the north and by sympathetic nobles. The Qin forces were forced to conduct a prolonged counter-insurgency campaign to eliminate this resistance.

The final elimination of Zhao resistance came in 222 BCE, when Qin forces under Wang Ben hunted down and captured the last Zhao claimants. The prince of Dai was captured and executed, and the last remnants of Zhao resistance were crushed. The complete destruction of Zhao was achieved, and its territories were incorporated into the Qin commandery system.

The conquest of Zhao was notable for its brutality. The *Shiji* records that the Qin forces engaged in widespread destruction and杀戮 in the conquered territories, a pattern that would characterize many of the later conquests. This brutality served both practical and strategic purposes: it weakened the ability of the conquered population to resist, and it served as a warning to other states about the consequences of defying Qin.

## The Elimination of Wei (225 BCE)

The conquest of Wei in 225 BCE was one of the shortest campaigns of the unification wars. Wei had been a major power in the fourth century BCE, but it had been significantly weakened by defeats at the hands of Qin and Qi. By the time of the unification campaigns, Wei was a shadow of its former greatness, lacking the military capacity to resist the Qin advance.

The campaign against Wei was led by the general Wang Ben (王賁), the son of Wang Jian. Wang Ben's forces invaded Wei territory from the west, advancing rapidly toward the Wei capital of Daliang (大梁). The Wei forces offered limited resistance before collapsing, and the Qin army laid siege to Daliang.

The siege of Daliang was a notable military operation. The city was well-fortified and had previously withstood long sieges, but Wang Ben employed a innovative tactic: he diverted the Yellow River to flood the city. The flooding destroyed the city's defenses and forced its surrender. According to the *Shiji*, the waters of the Yellow River drowned the city and its surroundings, creating a vast lake where the city had stood (Sima Qian, *Shiji*).

The fall of Daliang marked the end of Wei as an independent state. The Wei king was captured and executed, and the Wei territories were annexed into Qin. The conquest was remarkably quick, achieved in a single campaign season. The speed of the victory reflected both the weakness of Wei and the effectiveness of Wang Ben's innovative approach.

## The Campaign Against Chu (228-223 BCE)

The conquest of Chu was the largest and most challenging campaign of the unification wars. Chu was not merely the largest of the Warring States in terms of territory; it was also a powerful state with significant military resources and a sophisticated cultural tradition. The campaign would require the largest army Qin had ever fielded and would test the limits of Qin's military capacity.

The initial phase of the campaign began in 228 BCE, following the fall of Zhao. Wang Jian was given command of an enormous force, reported in the *Shiji* as 600,000 men, though modern scholars debate whether this number should be taken literally. The army was tasked with conquering the vast Chu territories in the south.

The campaign proceeded in stages. The first major engagement was the Battle of Chen (陳) in 224 BCE, where the Chu forces under the general Xiang Yan (項燕) were defeated. Xiang Yan was killed in the battle, and the main Chu field army was destroyed. Following this victory, the Qin forces advanced on the Chu capital of Ying (郢), which fell in 223 BCE. The king of Chu was captured, and the state was annexed.

However, the conquest of Chu was not complete with the fall of the capital. Significant resistance persisted in the southern and eastern regions of the former Chu territories, and Qin forces conducted a prolonged campaign of pacification over the following years. This resistance was eventually overcome, but it demonstrated the difficulties of conquering a state as large and diverse as Chu.

The conquest of Chu had important implications for the unification of China. With Chu eliminated, Qin controlled the vast southern territories that had previously been beyond its reach. The incorporation of Chu's population and resources significantly increased the scale of the Qin empire, and the conquered territories would become some of the most important regions in subsequent Chinese history.

## The Eastern Campaigns: Yan (226-222 BCE)

The campaigns against Yan and Qi in the northeast and east represented the final phase of the unification wars. With Chu, Zhao, Wei, and Han eliminated, only two major states remained: Yan in the northeast and Qi in the east. The conquest of these states would complete the unification of China.

The campaign against Yan began in 226 BCE, following the completion of the Chu conquest. The Qin forces were commanded by Wang Ben, who led his army into Yan territory from the west. The initial stages of the campaign were successful, with Qin forces achieving several victories and advancing toward the Yan capital of Ji (薊).

The Yan king Xi (喜) recognized that the capital could not be held and fled to the northeastern region of Liaodong (遼東). Wang Ben pursued the fleeing king across the mountains, eventually capturing and executing him in 226 BCE. However, resistance continued under the Prince of Yan, who organized a guerrilla campaign against Qin rule. This resistance was eventually eliminated, and the last major Yan forces were defeated in 222 BCE.

The conquest of Yan brought Qin to the northeastern frontier, extending its territories to the borders of the Korean peninsula and the Manchurian steppe. The strategic position gained from the Yan conquest would prove important for the future development of the Chinese empire.

## The Final Campaign: Qi (221 BCE)

The conquest of Qi in 221 BCE was the easiest and quickest of all the major campaigns. Qi had been in decline for decades, and its military was no match for the professional forces of Qin. The Qi court had lost the will to resist, and many influential figures advocated surrender as the only realistic option.

The campaign began in 221 BCE, when Qin forces invaded Qi from the west. The Qi forces offered minimal resistance, and the capital of Linzi (臨淄) fell without a significant battle. The Qi king, whose title was Jian (建), was captured and executed, and the state was annexed into the Qin empire.

The ease of the Qi conquest was not merely a matter of military superiority; it also reflected a deliberate strategy of diplomatic pressure. In the years preceding the final campaign, Qin had cultivated relationships with influential figures in the Qi court, encouraging them to advocate surrender rather than resistance. This diplomatic offensive weakened Qi's will to fight and facilitated the rapid conquest.

## Patterns in the Conquest

The campaigns of unification reveal several clear patterns that characterized Qin's approach to conquest. First, Qin consistently exploited the isolation of its targets, taking advantage of the divisions between the states to prevent coalition resistance. Each state was conquered individually, without the support of its neighbors.

Second, Qin employed a combination of military force and diplomatic manipulation. The campaigns were not simply wars of attrition; they were accompanied by efforts to weaken enemy morale, corrupt enemy officials, and encourage surrender. This multi-faceted approach reduced the costs of conquest and minimized the risks of prolonged warfare.

Third, Qin demonstrated remarkable adaptability in its military methods. Different campaigns required different approaches, and the Qin commanders proved capable of adjusting their tactics to the specific circumstances of each enemy. The flooding of Daliang, the mountain warfare in Liaodong, and the massive army deployed against Chu all demonstrated this adaptability.

Fourth, the conquests were characterized by a ruthless policy toward defeated enemies. The rulers of the conquered states were executed, their royal families eliminated or scattered, and their territories placed under direct Qin administration. This policy of elimination prevented the emergence of restoration movements and ensured the permanent incorporation of the conquered territories.

## Why No Coalition Successfully Resisted Qin

A fundamental question about the unification campaigns is why the six states failed to mount a coordinated resistance. Throughout the Warring States period, coalitions of states had occasionally threatened Qin, and these coalitions had sometimes achieved significant successes. Yet in the final campaigns, each state fell in isolation, without meaningful support from its neighbors.

The answer lies in a combination of factors that worked together to prevent coalition formation. First, Qin's diplomatic efforts had successfully driven wedges between the states, creating mutual suspicions that prevented cooperation. The states had fought each other for centuries, and old enmities were not easily overcome even in the face of a common threat.

Second, the strategic situation had changed dramatically by the time of the unification campaigns. Earlier coalitions had formed when multiple states still possessed significant military capacity and when the stakes of cooperation were lower. By 230 BCE, the states that might have formed a coalition had been weakened by decades of warfare, and the only state with sufficient power to lead such a coalition—Chu—had been alienated by previous conflicts.

Third, the states simply lacked the political and military infrastructure to coordinate effective resistance. Each state had its own interests, its own courts, and its own armies. The costs of cooperation were high, and the benefits were uncertain. It was easier to hope that Qin would target a neighbor rather than oneself, or to negotiate a separate peace.

Finally, the states were paralyzed by their own internal divisions. Factional disputes, court intrigues, and questions of succession consumed their attention, leaving them unable to respond effectively to the Qin threat. By the time they recognized the danger, it was too late.

## The Rapidity of Victory

The speed of the unification campaigns is striking. In just nine years, Qin destroyed all six major states and created the first unified empire in Chinese history. This rapidity raises questions about the nature of the conquest: was it the inevitable result of Qin's overwhelming superiority, or did contingent factors play a significant role?

Modern scholars have emphasized both factors. Qin's superiority was real and based on genuine advantages in military organization, economic capacity, and political coherence. These advantages had been developing for decades and would have eventually produced a Qin victory even under less favorable circumstances.

However, contingent factors also mattered. The timing of the campaigns—the relative strengths and weaknesses of the target states at particular moments—affected the pace of conquest. The collapse of Chu earlier than expected, the internal troubles of Qi, and the death of key enemy commanders all accelerated the process.

The result was a conquest that proceeded with remarkable speed, surprising even the Qin court. The unification of China, which many had thought would require generations of warfare, was achieved in less than a decade. The implications of this rapid transformation would shape Chinese history for millennia.

## Conclusion

The campaigns of unification between 230 and 221 BCE transformed the political map of China. One by one, the great states of the Warring States era fell before the Qin armies: Han in 230 BCE, Zhao between 228 and 222 BCE, Wei in 225 BCE, Chu between 228 and 223 BCE, Yan between 226 and 222 BCE, and finally Qi in 221 BCE. Each conquest had its own characteristics, its own challenges, and its own outcomes. Yet together, they formed a coherent pattern of expansion that reflected Qin's strategic approach and its overwhelming military superiority.

The fall of the six states was not inevitable, but it was also not accidental. It was the product of decades of preparation, of political and economic development, and of strategic calculation. The states that fell were not passive victims; they were actors in their own downfall, paralyzed by internal divisions and unable to overcome their mutual suspicions. In the end, the unification of China was the result of both Qin's strength and its rivals' weaknesses.

The creation of the Qin empire marked a turning point in Chinese history, but it was also the culmination of a particular historical process. Understanding the conquests—their timing, their methods, and their patterns—is essential to understanding how the empire came into being and why it took the form that it did. The next chapter will explore the deeper causes of Qin's success, examining the structural advantages, political decisions, and contingent factors that made unification possible.

### Chapter Notes

The chronology of the unification campaigns is based primarily on Sima Qian's *Shiji*, which provides the basic narrative for each campaign. However, the *Shiji* accounts are sometimes inconsistent, and scholars have debated the precise dates and sequence of events.

The numbers of troops involved in the campaigns, particularly the 600,000 men attributed to the Chu campaign, have been the subject of scholarly debate. Some historians argue that these numbers were exaggerated for propaganda purposes, while others suggest that they reflect the genuine scale of Qin military mobilization.

The question of why the states failed to form effective coalitions has been discussed by numerous scholars. Modern interpretations emphasize the role of Qin's diplomatic manipulations, the internal weaknesses of the target states, and the structural constraints on coalition warfare in the Warring States system.
