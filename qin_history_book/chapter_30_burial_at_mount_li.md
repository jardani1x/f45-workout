# Chapter 30: The Burial at Mount Li

In the shadow of Mount Li (驪山), approximately thirty-five kilometers northeast of modern Xi'an, lies one of the most extraordinary archaeological sites in the world. The mausoleum of Qin Shi Huang, the first emperor of China, is a testament to the ambition and power of the man who unified the Chinese heartland for the first time in history. For more than two millennia, the tomb has rested beneath an enormous earthen mound, its contents hidden from view, its secrets guarded by the earth itself. Only in the twentieth century did modern archaeology begin to reveal the true scale of what Qin Shi Huang had built for himself in death—and only in recent decades have scientists begun to understand the challenges and opportunities that the site presents.

## The Description in Historical Sources

The earliest and most detailed account of the tomb comes from Sima Qian, the great historian of the Han dynasty, who wrote his Description of the Grand Historian's Records (史記) more than a century after the Qin dynasty fell. In his account, Sima Qian describes the tomb in extraordinary terms:

> "After the emperor had united the whole empire, all the craftsmen were summoned and the tomb was constructed inside Mount Li. The tomb was made to imitate the layout of the imperial palace, with the central chambers representing the emperor's audience hall. The ceilings were decorated to resemble the heavens, with pearls representing the sun, moon, and stars. Rivers of mercury were created, flowing through the tomb and powered by mechanical devices. The walls were inlaid with precious stones, and the tomb was lit by lamps filled with whale oil, designed to burn for a long time."

This passage, while vivid, has presented both excitement and challenges for modern scholars. On one hand, it suggests a tomb of extraordinary complexity and richness—almost a miniature empire in itself. On the other hand, it was written by a historian who had no access to the tomb and who may have been relying on rumor, speculation, or propaganda. The challenge for modern archaeology has been to separate fact from fantasy, to verify what can be verified, and to investigate what remains hidden.

## The Structure of the Tomb Complex

The tomb complex at Mount Li is enormous, covering an area of approximately fifty-six square kilometers—larger than many ancient cities. At its center is the main burial mound, which rises to a height of seventy-six meters above the surrounding plain. The mound is shaped like a truncated pyramid, with a flat top that was once crowned by a complex of buildings. Over the centuries, the mound has eroded and been covered with vegetation, but its basic structure remains.

Beneath the mound lies the burial chamber itself, which according to Sima Qian was constructed to resemble the imperial palace. The chamber is reported to be surrounded by walls of solid bronze, with the sarcophagus placed in the center. The ceiling is said to be supported by pillars representing the mountains of the world, and the whole structure was designed to represent the cosmos in miniature.

Surrounding the main tomb are numerous smaller pits, which have been the focus of most of the archaeological work that has been conducted at the site. The most famous of these are the pits containing the Terracotta Army, which were discovered in 1974 by a group of farmers digging a well. These pits, which cover an area of more than twenty thousand square meters, contain thousands of life-sized clay soldiers, horses, and chariots that were intended to serve the emperor in the afterlife.

In addition to the Terracotta Army pits, archaeologists have identified pits containing other objects: bronze chariots and horses, weapons, tools, and even the remains of actual animals. The complexity of the site suggests that it was intended to provide Qin Shi Huang with everything he would need in the afterlife—a complete replica of the empire he had ruled in life.

## The Discovery of Mercury

One of the most intriguing aspects of the tomb is the reported presence of mercury. Sima Qian described "rivers of mercury" flowing through the tomb, and modern scientific investigations have confirmed the presence of elevated mercury levels in the soil above the burial chamber. This discovery has been both exciting and puzzling, as it raises questions about how the mercury was used and what it was meant to symbolize.

Mercury, known in ancient China as "quicksilver," was a substance of great fascination to Qin Shi Huang. The emperor was obsessed with achieving immortality, and he consumed numerous elixirs that contained mercury, believing that the metal had life-extending properties. Mercury was also associated with the mythical rivers of the east, and the "rivers of mercury" in the tomb may have been intended to represent the great seas that surrounded the empire.

The mercury in the tomb is not in the form of rivers, however. The tests that have been conducted show elevated levels in the soil, but the mercury appears to be contained within the burial chamber itself, which has not yet been excavated. The presence of mercury has been detected by measuring the levels in the soil above the tomb, and the pattern suggests that there is a significant quantity of mercury below—far more than could be explained by natural contamination.

The presence of mercury has important implications for the excavation of the tomb. Mercury is highly toxic, and opening the burial chamber without proper precautions could expose workers and the environment to serious danger. This has been one of the major arguments against attempting to excavate the tomb, at least until more sophisticated techniques are available.

## Archaeological Work at the Site

The archaeological investigation of the Mount Li tomb complex has proceeded in stages since the discovery of the Terracotta Army in 1974. The initial discovery was accidental—a group of farmers digging a well in 1974 broke through into one of the pits, revealing the head of a clay soldier. The find sparked a major archaeological excavation that continues to this day.

The Terracotta Army pits have been the primary focus of the archaeological work. Four pits have been excavated, revealing thousands of figures in various states of preservation. The figures were originally painted in bright colors, though most of the paint has faded over the centuries. The level of detail in the figures is remarkable: each face is different, suggesting that they were modeled on actual soldiers. The figures were arranged in military formation, with infantry, cavalry, and charioteers arranged as if for battle.

In addition to the Terracotta Army, archaeologists have excavated other parts of the tomb complex. Several smaller pits have been explored, revealing chariots, horses, and other objects. The bronze chariots that were discovered in one pit were in remarkable condition, and they have provided valuable information about Qin-era transportation and craftsmanship. The archaeological work has been slow and careful, as the authorities have been concerned about preserving the site and avoiding the mistakes of earlier excavations that damaged artifacts.

The most recent phase of archaeological work has involved non-invasive techniques designed to learn more about the tomb without disturbing it. Ground-penetrating radar, magnetic surveys, and other geophysical methods have been used to map the subsurface structures. These investigations have confirmed the basic description in Sima Qian's account, revealing a complex of chambers beneath the mound, including what appears to be the main burial chamber at the center.

2022 ground-penetrating radar study (Duan Qingbo team, *Chinese Archaeology*) confirms intact burial chamber with no looting tunnels.

## The Question of Looting

One of the most persistent questions about the Mount Li tomb is whether it has been looted. According to historical accounts, the tomb was targeted by robbers shortly after the Qin dynasty fell. Sima Qian mentions that the tomb was robbed, and other sources describe how Xiang Yu, after capturing Xianyang, is said to have looted the imperial treasures and burned the palaces. However, it is unclear whether the tomb itself was entered or whether only the surrounding pits were disturbed.

Archaeological evidence suggests that the main burial chamber may still be intact. The tests for mercury have detected elevated levels, indicating that the chamber has not been breached. The ground-penetrating radar has revealed that the subsurface structures appear to be undisturbed. However, the only way to confirm this with certainty would be to enter the tomb, which has not been done.

The surrounding pits tell a different story. The Terracotta Army pits show clear evidence of having been burned and looted, probably in the chaos following the fall of the Qin dynasty. The figures were broken, the weapons were taken, and the pits were partially destroyed. Some of the pits may have been looted in more recent times, as the site was abandoned and forgotten for centuries before its rediscovery.

The question of looting is not merely academic. If the main tomb has been looted, then the most valuable treasures may have been removed, and the scientific value of an excavation would be reduced. If the tomb is still intact, then it represents one of the most significant archaeological discoveries waiting to be made—a time capsule from the Qin dynasty, sealed for more than two thousand years.

## Debates Over Excavation

The question of whether to excavate the main tomb has been the subject of intense debate among archaeologists, scholars, and the Chinese public. On one side are those who argue that the tomb should be opened, if only to verify the historical accounts and to recover the treasures that may lie within. On the other side are those who argue that the risks outweigh the potential benefits, and that the tomb should be left sealed until technology improves.

The arguments against excavation are numerous. First, there is the problem of mercury. As mentioned, the presence of toxic mercury in the burial chamber could pose a serious risk to workers and the environment. Second, there is the problem of preservation. The objects in the tomb, like the Terracotta Army figures, have been protected from air and moisture for more than two thousand years. Exposure to the atmosphere could cause rapid deterioration, as has happened with other archaeological sites. Third, there is the problem of resources. Excavating a site of this size would require enormous investments of money and expertise, and the Chinese government has other archaeological priorities.

The arguments for excavation are equally compelling. The tomb is a unique historical resource—an almost intact burial of the first emperor of China. The objects inside could revolutionize our understanding of the Qin dynasty and of early Chinese civilization. The scientific information that could be obtained—from the materials used to the layout of the chambers—would be invaluable. And there is a certain symbolic significance to be considered: the tomb of the man who unified China remains sealed, and many feel that it should be opened so that his legacy can be properly studied and understood.

For now, the Chinese government has adopted a cautious approach. The tomb will not be excavated in the foreseeable future, and efforts are focused on preserving the site and conducting non-invasive investigations. The mound has been covered with a protective structure to prevent further erosion, and the surrounding area has been developed as a museum and tourist site. The tomb itself remains sealed, its secrets intact, waiting for future generations to decide its fate.

## The Symbolism of the Tomb

The Mount Li tomb has become one of the most powerful symbols of ancient China. The Terracotta Army, in particular, has captured the imagination of people around the world, becoming an icon of Chinese civilization. The tomb represents both the grandeur of the Qin achievement and the fragility of human ambition—the great emperor who unified China built for himself an eternal palace, only to see his dynasty collapse within years of his death.

The tomb also represents the enduring power of archaeology to shape our understanding of the past. Before the discovery of the Terracotta Army, the Qin dynasty was known primarily from historical texts that were often hostile. The archaeological evidence has forced scholars to reassess the Qin, to recognize the sophistication of their culture, and to appreciate the achievements of the first emperor. The tomb continues to hold secrets that could further transform our understanding of early China.

### Chapter Notes

The Mount Li tomb has been the subject of extensive archaeological and historical investigation. The most detailed archaeological reports are those published by the Shaanxi Provincial Institute of Archaeology, which has been responsible for the excavations since 1974. These reports provide detailed descriptions of the pits, the artifacts, and the techniques used in their excavation.

The question of whether to excavate the main tomb has been debated extensively in the archaeological literature. A useful summary of the arguments can be found in the works of archaeologists such as Yuan Zhongyi and Duan Qingbo, who have discussed the technical challenges and the ethical considerations involved.

The description in Sima Qian's Records has been both validated and complicated by the archaeological evidence. While the basic structure of the complex matches the historical account, some details—such as the "rivers of mercury"—remain mysterious. The detection of mercury in the soil above the tomb has confirmed at least part of Sima Qian's description, but the full significance of this discovery remains to be determined.
