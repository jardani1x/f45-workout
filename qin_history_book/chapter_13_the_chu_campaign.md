# Chapter 13: The Chu Campaign (224-223 BCE)

The conquest of Chu represented the most ambitious military undertaking in Qin's campaign of unification. Chu was not merely another Warring State; it was the largest and perhaps the most culturally sophisticated of all the rival kingdoms, a southern power that had long viewed itself as the rightful heir to the Zhou imperial tradition. With its vast territories spanning the Yangtze River basin and its capital at Shouchun, Chu commanded resources and manpower that dwarfed the other states of the Warring States system. The campaigns against Chu, which unfolded between 224 and 223 BCE, would test the limits of Qin's military capacity and reveal both the strengths and the vulnerabilities of the Qin war machine. The story of Chu's conquest is one of initial failure, strategic reassessment, and ultimately total triumph—a narrative that illuminates the character of the Qin generals and the determination of the king who led them. The conquest would also bring Qin into contact with the diverse peoples of the south and southwest, extending the boundaries of the Chinese world to encompass regions that had previously existed beyond the reach of the central平原 states.

## The Challenge of Chu

Chu occupied a vast territory stretching from the Yangtze River basin in the south to the Huang River region in the north, encompassing an area that covered much of modern-day Hubei, Hunan, Jiangxi, Anhui, and parts of Jiangsu and Sichuan provinces. The state had been a major power since the Spring and Autumn period, when it had emerged as one of the dominant states in the southern region. During the Warring States era, it had consistently ranked among the most formidable of the rival kingdoms, competing with Qin, Qi, and Zhao for hegemony over the Chinese world. At its height, Chu had controlled more territory than any other Warring State, encompassing regions that would later become some of the most prosperous and densely populated areas of imperial China. The Chu capital of Shouchun (壽春), to which the court had relocated in the fourth century BCE, was a major metropolitan center that could sustain prolonged resistance. The city's location on the upper reaches of the Yangtze River provided both strategic depth and economic vitality, connecting Chu to the valuable trade networks of the south.

The strategic challenges posed by Chu were immense. The state's geographic position meant that any Qin invasion would have to traverse difficult terrain, including mountainous regions, dense forests, and extensive wetland areas. The Chu military, while not as professionally organized as the Qin forces, was numerically substantial and fought with a distinctive style that emphasized mobility and the use of infantry in varied terrain. The Chu navy, which operated on the Yangtze River and its tributaries, gave the state additional strategic depth, allowing it to shift forces quickly and to threaten Qin flanks along the river systems.

Moreover, Chu possessed substantial economic resources. The fertile lands of the Yangtze valley produced abundant harvests, and the state's position on major trade routes gave it access to valuable resources from the south and southwest. The Chu capital of Shouchun (寿春), to which the court had relocated in the fourth century BCE, was a major metropolitan center that could sustain prolonged resistance.

Qin's previous encounters with Chu had been mixed. In the fourth century BCE, Qin had suffered significant defeats in its attempts to expand southward, and the memory of these failures had contributed to a certain caution in Qin's approach to Chu. However, by the third century BCE, the balance of power had shifted dramatically in Qin's favor. The reforms of Shang Yang and the subsequent militarization of Qin had created a war machine that had proven decisive against every state it had faced. Yet even the most confident Qin planners recognized that Chu would present unique challenges that would require careful strategy and substantial resources.

## The First Campaign: Overconfidence and Defeat

The first Qin campaign against Chu, which began in 224 BCE, reflected a dangerous degree of overconfidence. According to the historical accounts, Ying Zheng and his advisors underestimated the difficulty of the campaign and dispatched a force that proved inadequate to the task. The general chosen to lead this initial campaign was Li Xin (李信), a distinguished general unrelated to Li Si. Li You (李由), Li Si's son, appears later in history during the Chen Sheng rebellion of 209 BCE. This choice has been interpreted by scholars as reflecting a combination of political considerations and strategic miscalculation.

Li Xiang was not without military experience, but he lacked the extensive record of success that characterized veteran Qin commanders like Wang Jian. The decision to appoint him may have been influenced by political dynamics at the Qin court, where Li Si wielded considerable influence. However, the campaign's failure would demonstrate the dangers of allowing political considerations to override military judgment.

The campaign began with Qin forces advancing into Chu territory along the northern frontier. Initial progress appeared promising, and the Qin forces achieved some early successes against Chu border garrisons. However, as the army pushed deeper into Chu territory, it encountered increasingly stiff resistance. The Chu forces, commanded by Xiang (項) — likely Xiang Ya (項牙) or another member of the powerful Xiang clan — adopted a defensive strategy, withdrawing before the Qin advance while harassing its supply lines.

The terrain worked to Chu's advantage. The forests and wetlands of the south slowed the Qin advance and made it difficult to maintain lines of communication and supply. The Chu forces, familiar with the local geography, were able to operate effectively in this environment, while the Qin troops, trained primarily for warfare on the more open terrain of the central plains, found themselves at a disadvantage.

The campaign ended in failure when the Qin forces were decisively defeated at the Battle of Suqi (蘇醜). The details of the battle are not fully preserved in the historical sources, but it is clear that the Chu army achieved a significant victory, inflicting heavy casualties on the Qin forces and forcing them to retreat. The defeat was a shock to the Qin court and demonstrated that the seemingly invincible Qin war machine could be beaten when properly confronted.

## Wang Jian's Intervention and the Second Campaign

The failure of the first Chu campaign forced Ying Zheng to confront a difficult reality. The campaign had exposed fundamental weaknesses in Qin's approach: inadequate intelligence about Chu's military capabilities, insufficient forces for the scale of the operation, and a tendency to underestimate the challenges posed by the southern terrain. Most importantly, the failure had demonstrated the necessity of employing Qin's most capable military leadership.

Ying Zheng turned to Wang Jian, the veteran general who had already proven himself in campaigns against Zhao and other states. Wang Jian was by now an elderly man, but his reputation for careful planning and tactical brilliance made him the obvious choice for this crucial campaign. According to Sima Qian's account, Wang Jian initially demanded an enormous army for the campaign, requesting sixty thousand troops—virtually the entire available force of the Qin state (Sima Qian, *Shiji*).

The size of Wang Jian's request reflected his understanding of the challenge ahead. Unlike the first campaign, which had proceeded on the assumption that Chu could be defeated with a relatively small force, Wang Jian recognized that overwhelming numerical superiority would be necessary to overcome Chu's advantages in terrain and manpower. His request was also a political statement: by demanding such a large army, Wang Jian was signaling that he would not accept half-measures or political interference in his strategic planning.

Ying Zheng, apparently convinced by Wang Jian's analysis, granted the requested forces. The decision to commit such substantial resources to a single campaign demonstrated the king's determination to eliminate Chu as a factor in the unification struggle. With sixty thousand troops at his disposal, Wang Jian now had the means to pursue a more comprehensive strategy.

## The Conquest of Chu

The second campaign against Chu, which began in 223 BCE, followed a fundamentally different approach from the first. Wang Jian's strategy combined careful preparation with decisive action, avoiding the overextension that had undone the earlier expedition.

Wang Jian began by assembling his forces at the border and conducting a thorough assessment of the Chu defensive布局. Rather than rushing into battle, he spent time building up supplies and establishing secure lines of communication. This methodical approach reflected Wang Jian's characteristic caution and his understanding that a prolonged campaign would require a solid logistical foundation.

Once the campaign commenced, the Qin forces advanced along multiple axes, coordinated to prevent Chu from concentrating its defenses in any single area. The Chu army, under the command of Xiang (項), attempted to mount a defense but found itself overwhelmed by the scale and coordination of the Qin assault. The two armies met in a decisive engagement at Suqi, where Wang Jian achieved the victory that had eluded his predecessor.

The Battle of Suqi was the turning point of the war. In a reversal of the previous year's outcome, the Qin forces now triumphed over the Chu army. The Chu forces were shattered, with casualties reported in the hundreds of thousands. Xiang (項) was killed in the battle, eliminating the commander who had led Chu's successful defense the previous year. With the destruction of the main Chu field army, the path to the Chu capital of Shouchun lay open.

Following the victory at Suqi, Wang Jian's forces advanced rapidly toward the Chu capital. The fall of Shouchun marked the end of Chu as an independent state. The Chu king, whose title was Fu (負), was captured and executed—a fate that awaited the rulers of all conquered states. The royal family of Chu was scattered, with some members executed and others exiled or integrated into the Qin system.

The conquest of Chu was comprehensive. Wang Jian's forces swept across the former Chu territories, eliminating resistance and establishing Qin administrative control. The vast southern lands, from the Yangtze valley to the borders of the Wu and Yue regions, were now incorporated into the Qin state. The speed and completeness of the conquest demonstrated the effectiveness of Wang Jian's strategy and the overwhelming power of the Qin military when properly deployed.

## The Fate of the Chu Royal Family and Nobility

The treatment of the Chu royal family and nobility followed the pattern established in earlier Qin conquests. The Chu king was executed, eliminating the possibility of a restoration movement. The aristocratic families of Chu were disbanded, their estates confiscated, and their members either executed, exiled, or absorbed into the Qin administrative system.

However, the conquest of Chu also revealed some of the challenges that Qin would face in administering its newly acquired territories. The Chu nobility, though stripped of their political power, retained a strong sense of regional identity and cultural distinctiveness. The differences between Chu and Qin in language, customs, and social organization would create lasting tensions in the post-conquest period. These cultural differences would contribute to the rebellions that would erupt following the collapse of the Qin empire, as former Chu nobles would play prominent roles in the movements that overthrew Qin rule.

The conquest of Chu also brought Qin into contact with the peoples of the south and southwest, including the cultures of Wu, Yue, and the various tribal groups of the region. These territories would present their own administrative challenges, and the expansion of Qin influence into these areas would be a gradual process that would extend beyond the unification of 221 BCE.

## The Administration of Conquered Chu

Following the military conquest, Qin faced the enormous challenge of integrating the vast Chu territories into its administrative system. The territories were organized into commanderies and counties, with Qin officials replacing the old Chu administrators. This process was complicated by the cultural differences between Qin and Chu, as well as by the resistance of local populations to foreign rule.

The Qin government implemented policies designed to assimilate the Chu population into the Qin system. These included the promotion of Qin laws and customs, the relocation of Qin settlers to key areas, and the suppression of Chu cultural practices. However, these policies were only partially successful, and Chu cultural traditions would persist for generations, eventually resurfacing in the rebellions that followed the fall of Qin.

## Strategic Consequences of Chu's Fall

The fall of Chu had profound strategic implications for the unification campaign. With Chu eliminated, Qin had removed the largest and most territorially extensive of the rival states. The southern frontier was now secure, and Qin could turn its attention to the remaining states without fear of a major threat emerging from the south.

The conquest also freed up substantial military resources. The forces that had been committed to the Chu campaign could now be redeployed against the remaining states: Zhao to the north, Yan to the northeast, and Qi to the east. The elimination of Chu also changed the strategic geometry of the Warring States system, leaving the other states increasingly isolated and vulnerable to Qin's combined pressure.

For Ying Zheng, the conquest of Chu was a vindication of his determination to pursue unification. The initial setback in 224 BCE had been painful, but the ultimate victory in 223 BCE had secured the largest prize of the entire campaign. The king who would soon declare himself the first emperor of a unified China could now look forward to the final phase of the conquest with confidence.

## Historical Perspectives on the Chu Campaign

The conquest of Chu has been the subject of considerable historical analysis, with scholars focusing on the reasons for the initial failure and the factors that contributed to eventual success. The traditional Chinese historiography, following Sima Qian's account, emphasized the importance of proper military leadership and the dangers of overconfidence. The first campaign was attributed to Li Xiang's inadequacy, while the success of the second campaign was credited to Wang Jian's skill and experience.

Modern scholars have expanded on this analysis, examining the campaign within the broader context of Warring States warfare and the strategic dynamics of the period. Some have noted that the first campaign may have been intended as a reconnaissance in force rather than a full-scale conquest, and that its failure forced Qin to adopt a more committed approach. Others have emphasized the importance of logistics and supply in enabling Wang Jian's successful campaign, noting that the careful preparation of supply lines was crucial to sustaining the larger army in unfamiliar terrain.

The cultural dimensions of the Chu conquest have also attracted attention. Chu had developed a distinctive cultural tradition that differed significantly from that of Qin, and the integration of Chu into the Qin system required overcoming not only military resistance but also cultural and social barriers. The long-term consequences of this cultural tension would become apparent in the rebellions that followed the fall of Qin, when Chu cultural identity would emerge as a powerful force in opposition to Qin rule.

### Chapter Notes

The primary source for the Chu campaign is Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji*), which provides the basic narrative of both the failed first campaign and the successful second campaign. However, the *Shiji* account is relatively brief, and many details of the campaigns remain unclear.

The identity of the Chu commanders in the campaigns has been the subject of some scholarly discussion. Sima Qian mentions a commander named Xiang (項), who is likely to be Xiang Ya (項牙), a member of the powerful Xiang clan that would later play a prominent role in the rebellions against Qin.

The chronology of the campaigns is generally established, with the first campaign occurring in 224 BCE and the second in 223 BCE. However, some scholars have proposed alternative chronologies based on analysis of astronomical records and other evidence.

The size of Wang Jian's army is recorded as sixty thousand troops in the *Shiji*, though some scholars have questioned whether this figure refers to a single army or to the total Qin forces committed to the campaign. The number may also reflect the terminology of the Han period, when Sima Qian was writing, rather than the actual military organization of Qin.
