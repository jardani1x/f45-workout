# Chapter 19: The Emperor and His Title (221 BCE)

The year 221 BCE marked a watershed in Chinese political history. In that year, the king of Qin completed the conquest of the six remaining Warring States and proclaimed himself the first emperor of a unified China. This chapter examines the formal establishment of the Qin empire and the ideological significance of the new title that Ying Zheng adopted for himself. The ceremony in Xianyang, the creation of new imperial titulature, the rejection of the old title of "king" (王), and the adoption of the designation "First Emperor" (始皇帝) all reflected a conscious attempt to create a new political order fundamentally different from anything that had existed in Chinese history.

## The Ceremony at Xianyang

The formal proclamation of the empire took place at Xianyang, the capital of Qin, in 221 BCE. According to the traditional account preserved in the *Records of the Grand Historian*, the ceremony was an elaborate affair that symbolized the unprecedented nature of the new political order. The *Shiji* records that when the last of the six states—Qi—fell to Qin forces, the court ministers gathered to discuss the nature of the new political order that had been created (Sima Qian, *Shiji*, trans. Burton Watson).

The question before the court was not merely administrative but fundamentally ideological. The conquest of the six states had been completed, but what would this new political entity be called? What title would its ruler adopt? These were not trivial matters of protocol; they were questions about the nature of political authority itself.

The historical context for this moment was significant. For centuries, the Chinese world had been organized around the system of feudal lordship that had originated in the Zhou dynasty. The Zhou king had been the supreme ruler, granting titles of nobility to lords who governed territories in his name. Even as the power of the Zhou king declined and the Warring States system emerged, the ideological framework of feudal hierarchy persisted. Rulers of the various states still called themselves kings, a title that carried the implication of subordination to a higher authority—the Son of Heaven who ruled from the Zhou capital.

By 221 BCE, this framework had become obsolete. The Zhou dynasty had effectively ended, and no ruler claimed overlordship over the others. The six major states that remained after the conquest of Qi were all independent polities that recognized no superior. Yet the old terminology carried ideological baggage that did not fit the new reality. To call oneself a king, as the rulers of the Warring States had done, was to invoke a system of hierarchy that no longer existed in practice.

## The Rejection of the Kingly Title

The *Shiji* records a famous exchange between the Qin chancellor Li Si and other officials about the appropriate title for the new ruler. According to this account, someone proposed that the ruler adopt the title of "king" (王), which would have continued the nomenclature of the Warring States period. Li Si argued against this proposal, noting that the title of king had become devalued through overuse. "In ancient times, there was Yao, who gave the empire to Shun," Li Si reportedly said. "The three dynasties all followed each other, yet they did not emulate each other in their titles. Now the empire has been pacified, and the various states have been destroyed. The ruler's virtue has spread to all, and the achievements are equal to those of the ancient sages" (Sima Qian, *Shiji*, translation in Bodde, 1967).

Li Si's argument was political as much as historical. By suggesting that the new ruler's achievements were equal to those of the ancient sage-kings Yao and Shun, he was arguing for a title that would convey the unprecedented nature of the unification. The old title of king was insufficient because it had been used by many rulers of the Warring States, each of whom had governed only a portion of the Chinese world. The new ruler had accomplished something qualitatively different—he had unified the entire world under a single government.

The rejection of the kingly title was also a statement about the relationship between the ruler and his subjects. In the feudal system, kings had been at the apex of a hierarchy of lords and vassals. The new empire would have no such hierarchy; the emperor would stand alone at the summit of a centralized state. The title needed to express this radical break with the past.

## The Creation of the Imperial Title

The solution that Li Si proposed was the adoption of the title "皇帝" (huangdi), which is conventionally translated as "emperor" but which literally combines the characters for "august" (皇) and "sovereign" (帝). This title had no precedent in Chinese political history. The character 帝 had been used in ancient times to refer to the supreme deities of the Shang dynasty and later to legendary sage-rulers such as Yao and Shun. The character 皇, meaning "august" or "splendid," had been used as an honorific but never combined with 帝 in this way.

The new title was thus a creative synthesis that drew on ancient precedents while signifying something entirely new. By combining the characters for the highest divine authority (帝) and the most exalted human honorific (皇), the title "emperor" conveyed the ruler's claim to supreme authority over both the human world and the spiritual realm. It was a title that placed the Qin ruler above all previous Chinese monarchs, including the legendary figures of antiquity.

The *Shiji* records that Li Si proposed the title "huangdi" and that the king of Qin accepted this proposal. The full title adopted was "始皇帝" (shi huangdi), meaning "First Emperor." This title explicitly identified the new ruler as the first of a line of emperors who would inherit his authority. The use of the character "shi" (first, beginning) was significant—it acknowledged that future emperors would follow, while claiming for the first ruler a special status as the founder of the imperial line.

## The Ideological Foundations of Imperial Rule

The adoption of the imperial title was not merely a matter of nomenclature; it reflected a fundamental reconceptualization of political authority. The Qin empire represented a break with the feudal traditions that had dominated Chinese political life for centuries, and the imperial title was the symbolic expression of this break.

The feudal system had been based on a hierarchy of personal relationships. The Zhou king had granted lands to his lords, who in turn granted lands to their vassals. Authority flowed through these personal relationships, and the rights of each level in the hierarchy were defined by custom and tradition. The Legalist philosophy that had guided Qin's rise had always been critical of this feudal order, arguing that effective government required centralized authority rather than distributed power.

The imperial title expressed the centralization of authority in its very form. The emperor was not a king among kings, a first among equals; he was a singular figure whose authority extended directly to every corner of the empire. There were no intermediate lords between the emperor and his subjects, no feudal hierarchy to mediate the relationship between ruler and ruled.

The ideological foundation for this system drew on several intellectual traditions. The Legalist emphasis on law and centralized administration provided the practical framework. The ancient concept of the Mandate of Heaven (天命), which had originally been used to justify the rule of the Zhou dynasty over the Shang, was adapted to support the new imperial order. The emperor was seen as the recipient of Heaven's mandate to rule the unified empire, a mandate that could be withdrawn if the ruler failed to govern justly.

The doctrine of the Three Bonds (三纲) and the Five Constants (五常), which would later become central to Confucian orthodoxy, were still in the process of development during the Qin period. However, the imperial system created in 221 BCE established the political framework within which these ideas would eventually crystallize. The emperor became the pivot around which the entire social and political order revolved.

## The New Imperial Titulature

The creation of the imperial title was accompanied by a comprehensive reform of titulature throughout the empire. Just as the ruler needed a new title to reflect his unprecedented position, so too did the entire system of noble and official titles require revision to fit the new political order.

Under the feudal system, the hierarchy of noble titles had included the ranks of duke (公), marquis (侯), earl (伯), viscount (子), and baron (男). These titles had been inherited through aristocratic families and had carried with them rights to govern specific territories. The Qin conquest had abolished these feudal titles in the conquered states, and the new empire would not restore them.

Instead, the Qin empire created a new system of titles that was purely honorific. The highest ranks after the emperor were the three "Gong" (公) titles, which were granted to the most senior officials as a mark of distinction rather than as indicators of territorial authority. Below these were the ranks of Marquis (侯), which were granted for military or administrative achievements but did not carry feudal privileges.

This reform was consistent with the Legalist emphasis on merit over birth. By removing the hereditary element from noble titles, the Qin empire created a system in which advancement depended on service to the state rather than on the accidents of birth. The titles were rewards for achievement, not markers of inherited status.

The reform of titulature also extended to the language used to address the emperor. New terms of respect were created for use in official documents and court ritual. The emperor's commands were no longer simply "orders" (令) but "imperial decrees" (诏), a term that conveyed the sacred authority of the ruler. The emperor's words were recorded in special documents that were preserved as sacred texts, demonstrating the permanent authority of imperial utterances.

## The Symbolism of the Ceremony

The ceremony at Xianyang was rich with symbolic meaning. According to the *Shiji*, the new emperor ascended a special platform to receive the imperial seal, a jade symbol of authority that had been used by earlier rulers. The seal was inscribed with the words "Having received the Mandate from Heaven, may the emperor rule for ten thousand years" (受命于天，既寿永昌), a phrase that explicitly invoked the concept of the Mandate of Heaven while also expressing the hope for eternal rule.

The ceremony also included rituals that drew on the traditions of ancient Chinese kingship. The new emperor performed the suburban sacrifice (郊祭), a ritual that connected the ruler to the spiritual forces of the cosmos. This ritual, which had origins in the Shang and Zhou dynasties, was adapted to express the emperor's role as the intermediary between heaven and earth.

The location of the ceremony was itself significant. Xianyang had been the capital of Qin for generations, but the unification required a new relationship between the capital and the empire. The city was redesigned to reflect the imperial status of its ruler. According to historical accounts, construction began on a new palace complex that would dwarf all previous structures. The Epang Palace (阿房宫), whose construction would become one of the most notorious aspects of Qin's rule, was intended to symbolize the power and majesty of the new empire.

The ceremony at Xianyang thus established the template for imperial ritual that would persist throughout Chinese history. The elements introduced in 221 BCE—the new title, the imperial seal, the sacrificial rituals, the architectural program—would be adopted and adapted by every subsequent Chinese dynasty. The imperial system that Qin Shi Huang created became the standard form of Chinese government for the next two millennia.

## The Reaction of the Conquered States

The proclamation of the empire was met with a mixture of resignation and resistance among the peoples of the former Warring States. For centuries, the various states had maintained distinct identities, and the sudden imposition of unified rule was a profound disruption to established ways of life.

Some of the former ruling families of the conquered states were granted titles and stipends that allowed them to maintain a comfortable existence in the new order. This policy, which paralleled the earlier Qin practice of treating defeated rulers with a degree of magnanimity, was intended to secure the cooperation of the elite strata of the conquered societies. However, it also created a class of dispossessed nobles who had both the motive and the means to resist the new order.

Among the common people, the unification was initially received with a mixture of hope and fear. The end of decades of warfare was welcome, and the new empire promised a period of peace and stability. However, the harsh methods of Qin rule, which had been effective in conquering the states, proved difficult to maintain in peacetime. The heavy taxation, conscription for public works projects, and strict legal enforcement that characterized Qin administration generated resentment throughout the empire.

The ideological transformation required by the new imperial order was perhaps the most profound challenge. For centuries, loyalty had been to the individual state—to Qi, Chu, Yan, Wei, Zhao, or Han. The creation of the empire required the development of a new loyalty, to the emperor as an individual and to the imperial institution. This process of ideological transformation would take generations to complete and would remain incomplete throughout the Qin dynasty.

## The Legacy of the Imperial Title

The title "皇帝" that Qin Shi Huang adopted in 221 BCE became the standard designation for Chinese rulers for the next two thousand years. Although the Qin dynasty itself would collapse within years of its founder's death, the imperial title endured. Subsequent dynasties—Han, Wei, Jin, Sui, Tang, Song, Yuan, Ming, Qing—all claimed the title of emperor, acknowledging their debt to the system that Qin Shi Huang had created.

The endurance of the title is remarkable given the relatively brief duration of the Qin dynasty. It suggests that the imperial system, despite its flaws, fulfilled a deep need in Chinese political culture. The idea of a single supreme ruler, governing a unified empire from a centralized capital, became a fundamental assumption of Chinese political thought. Even when dynasties fell and the empire fragmented, the goal of reunification remained the dominant political aspiration.

The title also had implications for the relationship between ruler and ruled that extended beyond the Chinese world. The Chinese emperor claimed universal authority, not merely over the Chinese heartland but over all peoples who could be brought within the ambit of the imperial system. This claim to universal sovereignty would shape Chinese foreign relations for millennia, influencing interactions with neighboring states in East Asia and beyond.

## Conclusion

The year 221 BCE witnessed not merely the military conquest of the six Warring States but the creation of a new political order. The ceremony at Xianyang, the rejection of the old title of king, and the adoption of the unprecedented designation "皇帝" (emperor) all expressed a fundamental break with the feudal past. The new title drew on ancient concepts while signifying something entirely new—a centralized empire ruled by a single supreme authority.

The ideological foundations of this system drew on Legalist principles of centralized administration, the concept of the Mandate of Heaven, and the emerging ideas about the relationship between ruler and subject that would later be formalized in Confucian orthodoxy. The imperial title expressed the radical centralization of authority that distinguished the Qin empire from all previous Chinese polities.

The legacy of this moment extended far beyond the third century BCE. The imperial system that Qin Shi Huang created became the standard form of Chinese government, persisting in various forms until the early twentieth century. The title "皇帝" became synonymous with supreme political authority in the Chinese world. The decision made at Xianyang in 221 BCE thus shaped not only the immediate aftermath of the unification but the entire subsequent history of Chinese civilization.

### Chapter Notes

The traditional account of the adoption of the imperial title comes primarily from Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji*), written more than a century after the events described. Modern scholars recognize that this account may reflect later interpretations as much as contemporary records. The dialogue attributed to Li Si, in particular, has been analyzed as a retrospective justification for the imperial system rather than a record of actual historical statements.

The date of the proclamation has also been subject to scholarly debate. Some scholars have suggested that the ceremony may have occurred in 222 BCE rather than 221 BCE, though the latter date remains standard in most historical treatments.

The ideological significance of the imperial title has been a subject of extensive scholarly analysis. Mark Edward Lewis has argued that the title represented a fundamental reconceptualization of political authority that drew on both Legalist and ancient cosmological traditions. Other scholars have emphasized the practical political considerations that motivated the rejection of the kingly title.
