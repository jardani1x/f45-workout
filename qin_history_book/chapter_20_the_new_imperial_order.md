# Chapter 20: The New Imperial Order

The proclamation of the empire in 221 BCE was only the first step in the creation of a new political order. Having conquered the six Warring States, Qin Shi Huang faced the daunting task of governing the unified territory. The solution that emerged was a comprehensive system of centralized administration that replaced the feudal structures of the past with a rationalized hierarchy of officials appointed by the central government. This chapter examines the administrative structure of the Qin empire, the abolition of feudal enfeoffment, and the establishment of the commandery (郡) and county (县) system that became the foundation of imperial governance.

## The Abolition of Feudal Enfeoffment

The most fundamental transformation of the Qin empire was the abolition of feudal enfeoffment. For centuries, the Chinese political system had been organized around the distribution of territory to noble families who governed their domains with considerable autonomy. The feudal lords collected taxes, administered justice, and raised armies within their territories, owing only nominal allegiance to the king who had granted them their titles. This system had provided the framework for political organization since the Zhou dynasty, and its abolition represented a radical break with the past.

The Qin conquest had already destroyed the independence of the major feudal states. The rulers of Qi, Chu, Yan, Zhao, Wei, and Han had been defeated, their capitals captured, and their territories incorporated into the Qin domain. However, the mere conquest of these states did not automatically produce a centralized administration. In previous transitions of power, the conquerors had typically preserved the existing feudal structures, granting conquered territories to members of the ruling family or to loyal followers who would govern them as vassals.

Qin Shi Huang and his advisors rejected this approach. The Legalist philosophy that had guided Qin's rise was fundamentally opposed to the distribution of power to semi-independent lords. Han Fei, the Legalist philosopher whose ideas had profoundly influenced Qin Shi Huang, had argued that the only effective form of government was one in which all authority flowed from a single central source. Feudalism, with its multiple centers of power, was inherently unstable because it created rivals who might challenge the ruler's authority.

The abolition of feudal enfeoffment was therefore both a political and an ideological decision. The conquered territories would not be granted as fiefs to noble families; they would be governed directly by officials appointed by the central government. The old aristocratic families of the conquered states would be removed from power, their lands confiscated, and their roles assumed by bureaucrats accountable to the emperor alone.

This policy was implemented with characteristic Qin ruthlessness. According to historical accounts, the former rulers of the six states were relocated to the capital region, where they could be kept under surveillance. Their noble titles were abolished, and they were granted stipends that allowed them to live in comfort but denied them any political authority. The aristocratic families of the conquered states faced similar treatment, their estates broken up and their privileges revoked.

The abolition of feudal enfeoffment was not without opposition. The aristocratic families of the conquered states had deep roots in their local communities, and their removal created a power vacuum that was difficult to fill. Moreover, the policy flew in the face of centuries of tradition, and many within the empire questioned the wisdom of abandoning the old ways. However, the Qin government pressed forward with its program of centralization, confident that the benefits of direct administration outweighed the costs of disruption.

## The Commandery System

The primary administrative innovation of the Qin empire was the establishment of the commandery (郡) system. The empire was divided into administrative units called commanderies, each of which was governed by a military commander and a civil administrator appointed by the central government. This system replaced the patchwork of feudal states with a uniform grid of administrative divisions.

The number of commanderies varied over time, but the initial division established 36 commanderies covering the entire empire. These commanderies were distributed across the former territories of the six states, ensuring that no region would have sufficient local identity to challenge imperial authority. The commandery boundaries were drawn without regard to historical boundaries, breaking the connection between geography and political identity that had characterized the Warring States system.

Each commandery was further divided into counties (县), creating a hierarchical structure of administration that extended from the capital to the smallest local unit. The counties were the basic units of imperial administration, responsible for tax collection, population registration, local security, and the maintenance of public order. The county magistrates were appointed by the commandery authorities, who in turn were appointed by the central government.

The commandery system represented a fundamental transformation in the relationship between ruler and territory. Under the feudal system, the land had been owned by lords who owed allegiance to a superior. Under the imperial system, the land was administered by officials who owed their positions to the emperor. The change was not merely semantic; it altered the very nature of political authority.

The commandery governors held significant powers within their jurisdictions. They were responsible for tax collection, which provided the revenue that sustained the imperial government and its military forces. They commanded the local militia, maintaining internal order and suppressing any signs of rebellion. They administered justice, applying the uniform legal code that the central government had promulgated throughout the empire.

However, the commandery governors did not have the autonomy that feudal lords had enjoyed. Their appointments were temporary, and they were subject to constant evaluation by the central government. The system of mutual surveillance that had been developed in Qin—where officials were required to report on each other's conduct—was extended throughout the empire, creating a network of accountability that minimized the risk of independent action.

## The Appointment of Administrators

The administration of the Qin empire depended on a corps of trained officials who served at the pleasure of the emperor. The recruitment and training of these officials was a major undertaking, requiring the development of new institutions and the codification of new standards of competence.

The Qin government had long maintained a system of merit-based selection that emphasized practical skills over hereditary status. This system was extended throughout the empire, creating a bureaucracy that was unprecedented in Chinese history. The officials who served as commandery governors, county magistrates, and subordinate administrators were selected on the basis of their abilities, not their birth.

The training of these officials began in the local schools that the Qin government established throughout the empire. These schools taught the legal code, administrative procedures, and the writing system that was essential for official business. Students who demonstrated competence were eligible for appointment to administrative positions, beginning their careers as assistants to senior officials and advancing as their abilities were recognized.

The merit-based system represented a profound change from the aristocratic recruitment that had characterized earlier periods. Under the Zhou dynasty and the Warring States, government positions had been the preserve of noble families, passed down from generation to generation. The Qin system opened these positions to talented individuals regardless of their social background, creating a bureaucracy that could draw on the full range of talent within the empire.

The officials who served in the Qin empire faced demanding requirements. They were expected to master the legal code, which was complex and frequently updated. They were required to maintain detailed records of their administration, including population registers, tax assessments, and judicial proceedings. And they were subject to rigorous evaluation, with promotions and demotions depending on their performance.

The system was not without its flaws. The emphasis on strict adherence to regulations sometimes produced inflexible administration that failed to respond to local conditions. The constant evaluation created incentives for officials to prioritize easily measurable outcomes over more subtle forms of good governance. And the removal of aristocratic families from positions of authority created resentment that would contribute to the empire's instability.

## The Elimination of Noble Titles

Alongside the abolition of feudal territories, the Qin government eliminated the noble titles that had accompanied them. The complex hierarchy of dukes, marquises, earls, viscounts, and barons that had characterized the feudal order was replaced by a simplified system of ranks that carried no territorial authority.

The Qin government did not eliminate the practice of granting titles entirely. It created a new category of noble ranks—known collectively as the "twenty ranks of nobility" (二十等爵)—that could be earned through military service or administrative achievement. These ranks conferred certain privileges, including exemption from corvée labor and preferential treatment in legal proceedings, but they did not carry the right to govern territories or to collect taxes from a defined domain.

The new noble ranks were purely personal honors, tied to the individual rather than to a territory or a family. When the holder of a rank died, the rank did not automatically pass to his heir; it had to be earned anew through service to the empire. This represented a fundamental break with the hereditary principle that had characterized the feudal system.

The elimination of hereditary nobility was one of the most controversial aspects of Qin rule. The aristocratic families of the former Warring States had deep social standing and cultural influence, and the loss of their political privileges was a profound disruption to their way of life. Many of these families would play important roles in the rebellions that eventually toppled the Qin dynasty, motivated at least in part by the desire to restore their lost privileges.

The Qin government justified its policy on both ideological and practical grounds. Ideologically, the Legalist philosophy held that hereditary privilege was inherently unjust and that government should be based on merit rather than birth. Practically, the concentration of authority in the central government required the elimination of alternative centers of power, and hereditary lords represented exactly such alternative centers.

## The Centralization of Authority

The commandery-county system was the administrative expression of a broader principle of centralization that characterized the Qin empire. All significant authority was concentrated in the capital at Xianyang, where the emperor and his close advisors made decisions that affected every corner of the realm.

The central government was organized around the Three Lords and Nine Ministers (三公九卿), a hierarchical structure that divided administrative responsibilities among specialized departments. The Three Lords—the Chancellor (丞相), the Imperial Clerk (御史大夫), and the Commander-in-Chief (太尉)—were the highest-ranking officials, advising the emperor on matters of policy and overseeing the work of the Nine Ministers who managed specific areas of government.

The Nine Ministers (九卿) were responsible for various aspects of imperial administration: ritual matters, revenue, justice, the imperial household, agriculture, commerce, defense, and other essential functions. Each minister headed a department staffed by trained officials, creating a bureaucracy that could manage the complex affairs of the empire.

This structure represented a significant advance in administrative capacity. The specialization of functions allowed for greater expertise in each area of government, while the hierarchical organization ensured coordination among departments. The system was not perfect—the boundaries between departments were sometimes unclear, and the concentration of authority in the center created bottlenecks in decision-making—but it was far more effective than the fragmented administration of the feudal period.

The centralization of authority extended to the legal system as well. The Qin legal code, which had been developed over the preceding centuries, was promulgated throughout the empire, replacing the varied legal traditions of the former states. Everyone, from the highest official to the humblest peasant, was subject to the same laws, administered by the same courts, and punished according to the same standards. This uniformity was a hallmark of Qin administration and a source of both its strength and its notoriety.

## The Challenge of Governing a Unified Empire

The administrative system established in 221 BCE represented an ambitious attempt to govern a unified empire through centralized bureaucracy. However, the system faced numerous challenges that would test its viability from the beginning.

The most fundamental challenge was the sheer size of the territory to be governed. The Qin empire encompassed a vast area, from the mountainous regions of the southwest to the plains of the east and the frontiers of the north. Communication between the capital and the distant commanderies was slow, and the central government had limited ability to monitor the actions of its local officials. The infrastructure of roads and canals that the Qin government would develop was intended to address this problem, but it could never fully overcome the geographic constraints.

The cultural diversity of the empire presented another challenge. The former Warring States had developed distinct regional identities, and the imposition of uniform administration required the suppression of these identities. The standardization of writing, law, and administration was intended to create a unified culture, but it also generated resentment among peoples who valued their distinct traditions.

The economic demands of the empire were also significant. The maintenance of the bureaucracy, the construction of public works, and the defense of the frontiers required substantial resources. The tax system that the Qin government implemented was efficient but burdensome, extracting a high proportion of agricultural production to fund imperial activities. This burden would become increasingly difficult to bear as the empire aged.

Finally, the ideological challenge of legitimating the new order should not be underestimated. The feudal system had been justified by centuries of tradition and the concept of the Mandate of Heaven, but the Qin empire represented such a radical break with the past that traditional justifications were inadequate. The Legalist philosophy that justified Qin rule was controversial, and many intellectuals remained skeptical of its premises. The regime would need to develop new ideological foundations if it was to endure.

## The Legacy of the Administrative System

Despite the challenges it faced, the commandery-county system established by the Qin empire became the model for subsequent Chinese dynasties. When the Han dynasty restored imperial rule after the collapse of Qin, it retained the basic structure of centralized administration, though it modified certain features to address the problems that had contributed to Qin's downfall.

The commandery system would persist, in various forms, for over two millennia. The number of commanderies and their boundaries would change with the vicissitudes of dynasties, but the fundamental principle—that the empire should be governed through a hierarchy of appointed officials rather than feudal lords—remained constant. The system was so deeply embedded in Chinese political culture that its origins in the Qin conquest were often forgotten.

The abolition of feudal enfeoffment was also a lasting transformation. Although later dynasties would sometimes grant territories to members of the imperial family or to meritorious officials, these grants were always conditional and revocable. The hereditary feudal lords of the Zhou period never returned, replaced by a class of officials whose authority derived from appointment rather than inheritance.

The system of merit-based recruitment that Qin developed would also persist, though it would undergo significant evolution. The imperial examination system, which would not emerge until the Sui dynasty centuries later, was in many ways the fulfillment of the principle that government should be based on merit rather than birth. The Qin empire had established the foundation; later dynasties would build upon it.

## Conclusion

The administrative transformation of 221 BCE represented one of the most significant changes in Chinese political history. The abolition of feudal enfeoffment, the establishment of the commandery-county system, the appointment of administrators based on merit, and the centralization of authority all reflected a vision of government that differed fundamentally from the feudal traditions of the past.

The new imperial order was not merely a larger version of the Qin state that had conquered the Warring States; it was a qualitatively different political entity. The empire was governed through a rationalized bureaucracy, administered by officials appointed on the basis of merit rather than inherited status. The territory was divided into uniform administrative units that reported to a central government rather than to local lords. And all authority flowed from the emperor, who stood at the apex of a hierarchical structure of administration.

This system faced significant challenges, and it would prove unable to sustain the Qin dynasty indefinitely. However, the basic framework that Qin Shi Huang established would endure for over two millennia, influencing the political organization of China through successive dynasties. The imperial order created in 221 BCE thus represented not merely a particular historical configuration but a lasting transformation in the nature of Chinese government.

### Chapter Notes

The administrative system of the Qin empire has been the subject of extensive scholarly analysis. The traditional view, derived from Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian*, presents the commandery-county system as a comprehensive and well-ordered structure. Modern scholarship has emphasized the complexity and messiness of the actual administration, noting that the system was implemented unevenly and that local variations persisted.

The number of commanderies established in 221 BCE is a matter of some scholarly dispute. The traditional figure of 36 is well-attested in early sources, but some scholars have suggested that this number was achieved gradually rather than all at once.

The relationship between the central government and the local administrations has also been debated. Some scholars emphasize the capacity of the Qin state to monitor and control its officials, while others have argued that the distance from the capital limited effective oversight.
