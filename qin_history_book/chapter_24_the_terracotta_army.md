# Chapter 24: The Terracotta Army — Art and Power

In 1974, a farmer digging a well near the city of Xi'an in Shaanxi province made a discovery that would transform our understanding of the Qin dynasty and ancient Chinese civilization. Beneath the soil lay an army of life-sized clay soldiers, arranged in battle formation and buried alongside their emperor for over two millennia. The Terracotta Army—discovered at what is now known as the Qin Shi Huang Mausoleum—has become one of the archaeological wonders of the world, a testament to the ambition and capabilities of the first emperor of China. Yet the army is more than a spectacular find; it is a window into the worldview of the man who created it and the empire he built.

## The Discovery

The story of the Terracotta Army's discovery has become almost as famous as the army itself. In March 1974 farmer named Yang Zh, aifa was digging a well on his land near the village of Xiyang when he struck something hard in the earth. Investigation revealed a collection of fragmented clay figures—heads, arms, and torsos—that had been buried in pits for over two thousand years. Local officials were notified, and within months, archaeologists from Beijing had begun systematic excavations.

The initial discoveries were just the beginning. As excavations continued, it became clear that the farmer had found only a small portion of a much larger complex. The Terracotta Army, as it came to be known, consisted of thousands of figures arranged in pits covering an area of over 20,000 square meters. The scale of the find was unprecedented in Chinese archaeology, and it quickly attracted international attention.

The discovery also confirmed something that had long been suspected from historical sources. The Records of the Grand Historian, composed by the historian Sima Qian more than a century after the Qin dynasty's fall, described the burial of soldiers and horses at Qin Shi Huang's tomb. "Workers were sent to scatter coins and money around the tomb," Sima Qian wrote, "and the tomb was filled with models of palaces, pavilions, and offices, as well as vessels, commodities, and rare objects." The description also mentioned that "mercury was used to create rivers and seas" and that "a hundred kinds of mechanical devices were set up to prevent robbery."

Archaeological evidence has confirmed many of these details, though the full extent of the mausoleum complex has not yet been excavated. The burial pits containing the Terracotta Army are only part of a larger necropolis that includes numerous other pits, the main tomb mound, and various supporting structures. The entire complex covers an area of approximately 56 square kilometers, making it one of the largest burial sites in the world.

## Scale and Sophistication

The Terracotta Army is remarkable above all for its sheer scale. The excavated pits contain an estimated 8,000 soldiers, 130 chariots with 520 horses, and 150 cavalry horses, all made of clay and painted in lifelike colors. The figures are arranged in a precise military formation that mirrors the organization of the Qin army at the height of its power. Each figure is unique, with individualized facial features, hairstyles, and body types.

The soldiers are arranged in eleven corridors, separated by walls of compacted earth. The main east-facing formation includes infantry, charioteers, and cavalry, arranged in battle order. The infantry, who form the bulk of the army, stand in long rows, their weapons raised in preparation for combat. The chariots, each drawn by four horses, are positioned behind the infantry, while the cavalry are stationed on the flanks. This formation reflects the tactical organization of the Qin army, which relied on coordinated infantry and chariot forces supported by cavalry.

The individual figures are made with remarkable attention to detail. Each soldier was constructed from separate parts—head, torso, arms, and legs—that were assembled and then refined with modeling tools. The faces were hand-modeled, resulting in a remarkable diversity of features. Some figures have round faces, others angular ones; some display confident expressions, others appear more solemn. The hair and headdress vary according to the soldier's rank and role, with officers wearing more elaborate headgear than common soldiers.

The craftsmanship extends beyond the human figures to the horses and chariots. The horses, though stylized, display a convincing sense of power and movement. Their manes are carefully rendered, and their expressions suggest the energy of living animals. The chariots are detailed reproductions of the vehicles used in Qin warfare, complete with wheels, yokes, and decorative elements. The integration of figures, horses, and vehicles creates a cohesive military scene that must have been intended to convey the power of the emperor's forces.

## Individual Features

One of the most striking aspects of the Terracotta Army is the individualization of the soldiers. Rather than producing identical figures through mass production techniques, the Qin craftsmen created each soldier as a unique entity. The result is a collection of portraits that, taken together, provide a remarkable snapshot of the population that served in the Qin army.

The faces of the soldiers display considerable variety. Some have broad foreheads, others narrow chins; some have prominent noses, others more delicate features. The eyes, rendered in fine detail, show different shapes and expressions. The mouths vary from thin-lipped to full-lipped, from smiling to solemn. Scholars have identified at least a dozen different basic facial types, each modified by individual details to create unique figures.

2023 Xi'an research (Zhang et al., *Antiquity* 97) confirms individual facial molds for warriors via 3D morphometrics.

The hairstyles and headdresses are equally varied, reflecting differences in rank, role, and possibly regional origin. Common infantry soldiers typically wear their hair tied in a topknot, secured with a cap. Officers display more elaborate hairstyles, sometimes with additional hair pieces that add volume and complexity. The highest-ranking officials wear distinctive caps that mark their authority. These variations in headwear allow modern viewers to distinguish between different military roles at a glance.

The uniforms of the soldiers also vary according to their function. Infantry soldiers wear armor composed of scale plates, while charioteers have simpler garments that allow freedom of movement for driving. The cavalry wear shorter jackets that would not interfere with riding. The armor is rendered in detail, with individual scales carefully depicted. The colors originally applied to these uniforms have largely faded, though traces of pigment have been found on some figures.

The weapons carried by the soldiers are equally impressive. Each figure was originally equipped with real weapons—spears, halberds, swords, and crossbows—that have long since corroded or been looted. The empty hands of the surviving figures still grip the handles of weapons that are now missing, but the sockets and attachments for these weapons remain visible. The quality of the weapons, many of which have been found in good condition in nearby pits, demonstrates the advanced metallurgical capabilities of the Qin.

## Chariot Pits

The Terracotta Army includes not only infantry and cavalry but also a substantial number of chariots. These four-wheeled vehicles were the dominant military technology of the Warring States period, and they played a crucial role in Qin military victories. The chariot pits at the Qin Shi Huang Mausoleum provide a detailed picture of this military technology at its height.

Four chariot pits have been discovered, though only three have been excavated. The largest of these pits contains 64 chariots, arranged in eight rows of eight. Each chariot is drawn by four horses, and each is accompanied by a charioteer and, in some cases, an armed warrior. The chariots are of two types: lighter, faster vehicles for warfare, and heavier, more elaborate vehicles that may have been used for ceremonial purposes.

The chariot horses are particularly impressive, displaying a sense of animation that brings the static figures to life. Their bodies are tensed, their heads held high, their manes flowing as if in motion. The quality of the modeling suggests that the craftsmen had observed real horses carefully, capturing not just their anatomy but also their spirit. The integration of horses and vehicles creates a sense of unified purpose that conveys the power of the imperial army.

The chariots themselves are sophisticated pieces of engineering. They feature innovations such as spoke wheels, which are lighter and more durable than solid wheels, and sophisticated braking systems that allow the driver to control descent on slopes. The yokes and harnesses are designed for efficiency, distributing the pulling force evenly across the team of horses. These technical details demonstrate the advanced engineering capabilities of the Qin.

In addition to the military chariots, the mausoleum complex includes pits containing ceremonial vehicles and draft animals. These include bronze birds, apparently representing the emperor's personal menagerie, and various other objects that would have accompanied him in the afterlife. The comprehensiveness of the burial reflects the Qin belief in the continuity of life after death—a belief that motivated the creation of the Terracotta Army in the first place.

## Artistic Achievement

The Terracotta Army represents one of the great artistic achievements of the ancient world. The combination of scale, detail, and artistic sophistication places it among the most impressive examples of ancient sculpture. The army is all the more remarkable when one considers that it was produced in a relatively short period—perhaps a few decades—and that it represents the work of numerous individual craftsmen rather than a single master artist.

The technical achievement of the Terracotta Army is considerable. The production of thousands of life-sized figures required sophisticated organization and manufacturing techniques. Archaeological evidence suggests that the figures were produced in workshops, with different artisans responsible for different parts of the process. Some workshops specialized in heads, others in torsos, still others in limbs. The finished parts were assembled and refined, creating the complete figures that were then placed in the burial pits.

The painting of the figures added another dimension of complexity. Each figure was originally painted in bright colors—red, green, blue, black, and white—that would have made the army appear almost alive. The paint was applied in layers, with details added after the base coat had dried. The colors have largely faded due to exposure and the effects of time, but the skill of the original painters is evident in the traces that remain.

The artistic quality of the figures reflects the cultural context in which they were produced. The Qin dynasty was known for its emphasis on standardization and efficiency, but the Terracotta Army shows that these principles could be applied to artistic production without sacrificing individual expression. The standardization is evident in the overall organization of the army and the consistent quality of the craftsmanship. The individual expression is evident in the unique faces and details of each figure.

Scholars have noted the connections between the Terracotta Army and other Qin artistic achievements. The bronze horses and chariots found in other pits at the mausoleum display similar levels of sophistication. The bronze bells and other musical instruments demonstrate the Qin interest in music and ritual. Taken together, these objects represent a coherent artistic culture that emerged in the final decades of the Warring States period and reached its peak under the first emperor.

## Imperial Ideology

The Terracotta Army was not merely an artistic project; it was a political and ideological statement. The army was meant to serve Qin Shi Huang in the afterlife, providing him with the military force he would need to maintain his power beyond death. The creation of such an army reflected a specific understanding of death and the afterlife that was deeply embedded in Qin political culture.

The Qin believed that the dead continued to exist in a manner similar to the living. The deceased required food, shelter, servants, and soldiers just as they had in life. The burial of material substitutes—models and representations of the things needed in the afterlife—ensured that the deceased would want for nothing. This belief justified the massive expenditures on burial complexes that characterized the late Warring States and early imperial periods.

The Terracotta Army thus served as a substitute for a real army that would accompany the emperor into death. By creating thousands of clay soldiers instead of killing living ones, the Qin demonstrated a certain pragmatism—even in their pursuit of afterlife resources, they sought efficiency. Yet the creation of such an elaborate substitute army also demonstrates the importance of military power in Qin ideology. The emperor's identity was intimately connected with his army, and the army would continue to serve him in death as it had in life.

The ideological significance of the Terracotta Army extends beyond the afterlife. The army also served as a demonstration of imperial power in the present. The very existence of such an enormous burial complex—far larger than any previously constructed—announced the power and resources of the emperor to all who heard of it. The Terracotta Army was, in effect, the ultimate propaganda monument, a physical manifestation of the emperor's ability to command vast resources and achieve the impossible.

The relationship between the Terracotta Army and imperial ideology has been the subject of scholarly debate. Some scholars view the army as evidence of Qin Shi Huang's megalomania, his desire to take his power with him beyond the grave. Others have emphasized the continuity between Qin burial practices and those of earlier periods, arguing that the Terracotta Army represents an elaboration of existing traditions rather than a radical innovation. Still others have focused on the practical aspects of the project—the employment opportunities it created, the resources it mobilized, and the economic impact it had on the surrounding region.

## Archaeological Significance

The Terracotta Army has revolutionized our understanding of the Qin dynasty and early Chinese civilization. Before its discovery, our knowledge of the Qin came primarily from textual sources—historical accounts written long after the dynasty's fall. The archaeological evidence provides a tangible connection to the Qin world that complements and sometimes contradicts the written records.

The army has confirmed many details from historical sources, including the scale of the Qin military and the sophistication of its organization. The arrangement of the figures matches descriptions in ancient military texts, suggesting that the Qin army was organized in the manner that historical sources describe. The weapons found in the burial pits include types that are mentioned in historical accounts, confirming the accuracy of these sources in many details.

At the same time, the archaeological evidence has raised new questions that textual sources do not address. The individualization of the soldiers, for example, far exceeds what historical accounts describe. The artistic sophistication of the figures was not anticipated by scholars who relied primarily on written sources. The implications of these findings are still being worked out as scholars analyze the enormous quantity of data that the excavations have produced.

The Terracotta Army has also had a significant impact on Chinese cultural identity. The figures have become symbols of Chinese civilization, featured in countless publications, documentaries, and tourist materials. The museum built at the site has become one of the most visited attractions in China, drawing millions of visitors each year. The army's status as a World Heritage Site has reinforced its importance in international understanding of Chinese history.

The ongoing excavations continue to reveal new aspects of the complex. Recent discoveries have included the tomb of the emperor's concubines, the burial site of the horses that pulled the chariots, and various other features that expand our understanding of the mausoleum. The full extent of the complex may never be known—some areas remain unexcavated, and the water table makes certain portions inaccessible—making the Terracotta Army a perpetual source of new discoveries.

### Chapter Notes

The Terracotta Army has been the subject of extensive archaeological study since its discovery in 1974. The initial excavations, directed by Zhao Ruirui and other Chinese archaeologists, established the basic parameters of the find. Subsequent research has refined our understanding of the army's composition, construction, and significance. The most recent scholarship has employed advanced scientific techniques—including DNA analysis and 3D imaging—to extract new information from the figures and their context.

The dating of the Terracotta Army has been a subject of some debate. While most scholars agree that the figures were created during Qin Shi Huang's reign, the precise dates of production remain uncertain. Some archaeologists have suggested that the project may have begun before the unification of China and continued for decades afterward, though this interpretation is not universally accepted.

The ideological significance of the Terracotta Army continues to be debated. The traditional view, derived from Sima Qian's History, presents the army as evidence of Qin Shi Huang's desire for power beyond death. More recent scholarship has emphasized the continuity between Qin burial practices and those of earlier periods, as well as the practical considerations that motivated the project.
