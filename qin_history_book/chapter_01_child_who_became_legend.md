# Chapter 1: The Child Who Became a Legend

In the winter of 259 BCE, in the bustling capital of the state of Zhao, a child was born into circumstances that would one day reshape the entire Chinese world. His birth name was Ying Zheng, and he came into existence amid the political intrigue and danger that defined his father's precarious position as a royal hostage in a foreign court. The circumstances of his birth, the political machinations that surrounded his family, and the extraordinary journey that would carry him from a childhood marked by uncertainty to a throne that commanded the greatest military power of the Warring States period—these form the opening chapter of one of history's most remarkable lives.

To understand Ying Zheng's beginnings, one must first understand the world into which he was born. The China of the third century BCE was not a unified nation but a fragmented landscape of competing states, the remnants of a collapsed Zhou dynasty that had once claimed dominion over all under heaven. By the time of Ying Zheng's birth, seven major powers remained locked in an endless struggle for survival and supremacy: Qi in the east, Chu in the south, Yan in the northeast, Wei and Zhao in the central plains, Han in the southwest, and Qin in the western borderlands. This period, known as the Warring States, was characterized by constant warfare, shifting alliances, and the rise of new philosophical approaches to governance that would fundamentally transform Chinese political thought.

The state of Qin, which would become the instrument of unification under Ying Zheng's leadership, occupied a distinctive position among these powers. Descended from a lineage of frontier lords who had served as guardians of the Zhou royal house against the nomadic peoples of the western steppes, Qin had long been viewed by the more culturally sophisticated states of the central plains as a barbarous upstart. The Qin people spoke a different dialect, practiced different customs, and governed themselves through Legalist methods that emphasized strict law, harsh punishment, and absolute state control—approaches that the Confucian scholars who dominated historical writing would later condemn as morally bankrupt. Yet it was precisely these methods that had transformed Qin from a marginal frontier state into the most powerful military machine of the age.

## The Prince Who Became a Hostage

Ying Zheng's father was Zichu, a prince of the Qin royal house whose name would later be elevated to the more illustrious title of King Zhuangxiang of Qin. The circumstances that led Zichu to become a hostage in Zhao were rooted in the complex diplomatic practices of the Warring States period, when the exchange of royal hostages served as a guarantee for treaties and alliances between rival powers. In 257 BCE, as part of an agreement between Qin and Zhao during one of their recurring conflicts, Zichu was sent to the Zhao capital of Handan to live as a virtual prisoner—a guarantee that Qin would honor its diplomatic commitments.

According to the traditional account preserved in Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian*, Zichu's position in Handan was precarious and humiliating. The prince lived under constant surveillance, dependent on the goodwill of his Zhao hosts for his basic needs. Modern scholars have noted, however, that the treatment of hostages in the Warring States period varied considerably, and high-profile royal hostages often enjoyed considerable comfort and even autonomy. The Hostage Prince, as Zichu was sometimes known, was not merely a prisoner but a diplomatic asset—his welfare reflected on the state that held him.

It was in this environment of diplomatic confinement that Zichu encountered the man who would prove instrumental in changing his family's fortune: Lü Buwei. Lü Buwei was a wealthy merchant from the state of Zhao who had grown fabulously rich through trade in silk, precious metals, and other commodities that moved along the ancient trade routes connecting China to the West. According to the *Records of the Grand Historian*, Lü Buwei was a man of enormous ambition who saw in Zichu's predicament an opportunity for political advancement that no mere merchant could ordinarily aspire to.

## Lü Buwei: The Merchant Who Bet on a Prince

The traditional account of Lü Buwei's role in transforming Zichu's fortunes reads like a political thriller. Sima Qian tells us that Lü Buwei recognized Zichu as a man of unusual potential despite his reduced circumstances—a prince of a major power hidden away in foreign exile. Lü Buwei reportedly declared that Zichu was "a man worth betting on," and he proceeded to invest enormous resources in upgrading the prince's image and political prospects.

Modern historians have expressed varying degrees of skepticism about this traditional narrative. Some scholars argue that Sima Qian, writing with the benefit of hindsight and influenced by later Confucian moralizing about the dangers of merchant influence in politics, dramatically overstated Lü Buwei's role in Zichu's elevation. Others suggest that the merchant's involvement, while real, has been embellished by a tradition that loved to tell stories of cunning advisors who shaped the course of dynasties.

What can be stated with reasonable confidence is that sometime around 251 BCE, the King of Zhao released Zichu from his captivity, likely as part of a broader diplomatic reconciliation between the two states. According to the *Records of the Grand Historian*, Zichu had spent several years in Zhao and had established relationships there—including, reputedly, a relationship with a woman known historically as Zhao Jiao who would become the mother of Ying Zheng.

The traditional account holds that Zhao Jiao was originally a concubine of Lü Buwei, who gave her to Zichu after she became pregnant. This claim, which would later be used by Sima Qian to suggest that Ying Zheng might actually have been Lü Buwei's biological son, has been hotly debated by modern scholars. The *Records of the Grand Historian* itself presents contradictory accounts, with some passages suggesting that Ying Zheng was definitely Zichu's son and others hinting at the possibility of Lü Buwei's paternity. Most contemporary historians regard the claim as unprovable and possibly a later invention designed to explain the First Emperor's controversial policies through the lens of his supposedly suspect origins.

## Birth in Handan

Whatever the precise circumstances of his conception, Ying Zheng was born in Handan in 259 BCE. The child entered a world of danger and uncertainty. As the son of a Qin prince who had been held hostage in Zhao, his position was politically sensitive—useful to Zhao as a potential bargaining chip, but also potentially dangerous, as any claim he might one day have to the Qin throne could complicate the diplomatic calculations of multiple states.

According to Sima Qian, the infant was originally named Zheng, a name that would later become part of his imperial title as the First Emperor. His family name was Ying, the hereditary designation of the Qin royal house. Thus, he was known as Ying Zheng—literally, "Ying Zheng of Qin."

The first years of Ying Zheng's life were spent in Handan, surrounded by the sights and sounds of the Zhao capital. Handan was a magnificent city, one of the great urban centers of the Warring States world, renowned for its sophisticated culture, its bustling markets, and its elegant palaces. The young Ying Zheng would have been exposed to the refined ways of Zhao, a state that traced its ancestry to the ancient Zhou royal house and prided itself on its cultural accomplishments. Yet he also would have been aware, even in childhood, that his family was not truly at home—that his father was a prince of Qin, and that their presence in Zhao was the result of political necessity rather than choice.

Some scholars have suggested that this childhood as a foreign hostage's son in a rival state's capital may have shaped Ying Zheng's later worldview—instilling in him a distrust of foreign influences, a belief in the necessity of strength, and an understanding of politics as a ruthless struggle for survival. While such psychological interpretations must be offered cautiously, given the limited evidence available about the First Emperor's inner life, it is worth noting that his upbringing was fundamentally different from that of most royal heirs, who typically grew up within the protective confines of their own courts.

## A Dramatic Reversal of Fortune

The year 250 BCE brought a dramatic change in Ying Zheng's circumstances. King Zhaoxiang of Qin, who had ruled for over fifty years and had been one of the most formidable rulers in Qin's history, died. The succession that followed was complicated by the fact that King Zhaoxiang's designated heir had already predeceased him, leaving the Qin throne to pass to a series of less distinguished princes.

It was at this moment of political upheaval that Lü Buwei's investments in Zichu finally bore fruit. According to the traditional account, Lü Buwei orchestrated a complex political maneuver that involved bribing officials in the Qin court, promoting Zichu's claims to the throne, and ultimately securing his elevation as the new King of Qin. Lü Buwei's reward was appointment as Chief Minister—the highest office in the Qin state—and a level of influence that would shape the kingdom for decades to come.

Zichu, now King Zhuangxiang of Qin, dispatched urgent diplomatic missions to Zhao to secure the release of his family. In 249 BCE, the young Ying Zheng and his mother finally left Handan and traveled to the Qin capital of Xianyang. For the first time in his life, Ying Zheng set foot in the land that would one day be his to command.

The journey from Handan to Xianyang must have been a profound experience for the young boy. He was traveling from a foreign land where he had spent his entire life to a homeland he had never known—a kingdom that, according to the traditions of the time, was destined for greatness. The boy who had been born in obscurity, the son of a prince who had lived as a hostage, was now the heir apparent to the most powerful state in the Warring States world.

## King Zhuangxiang's Brief Reign

King Zhuangxiang's reign proved to be remarkably brief—lasting only three years from 249 to 247 BCE. During this period, he worked to consolidate Qin's position as the dominant military power while relying heavily on Lü Buwei to manage the day-to-day affairs of government. The traditional historical account portrays King Zhuangxiang as a somewhat weak ruler, dominated by his chief minister and more interested in pleasures than in the hard work of governance.

Modern scholars have noted, however, that this characterization may reflect later Confucian biases rather than historical reality. King Zhuangxiang inherited a kingdom that was already the most powerful in China, and his brief reign saw the continuation of Qin's aggressive expansionist policies. Under his rule, Qin captured the strategically important territory of Gong and the Chengxiao region (eastern Henan) from Zhao and also defeated Chu, extending Qin's influence further into the central plains.

According to Sima Qian, King Zhuangxiang died in 247 BCE, leaving the Qin throne to his young son. The king had reigned for only three years—a brevity that has led some scholars to speculate about the circumstances of his death, though no conclusive evidence of foul play has ever been established.

## The Boy King: Ying Zheng Ascends the Throne

In 247 BCE, at the age of thirteen, Ying Zheng ascended to the throne of Qin. He was, by any measure, an extremely young ruler to command one of the most powerful states in the Warring States world. The boy who had been born in a foreign capital to a prince who seemed destined for obscurity was now the King of Qin—the leader of a military juggernaut that would, within his lifetime, conquer all of China and establish the first imperial dynasty in Chinese history.

The traditional account in Sima Qian presents this transition as a moment of great significance—the beginning of a regency period during which Lü Buwei would effectively rule the kingdom as chief minister while the young king grew toward adulthood. According to the *Records*, the title assumed by the new king was simply "King of Qin" (秦王), though he would later adopt the grander title of "First Emperor" (始皇帝) after completing the conquest of the Warring States.

The political context of Ying Zheng's accession was complex. Qin's position as the dominant military power had made it the object of fear and suspicion among the other states, which had formed various alliances—known collectively as "vertical alliances" (合纵)—to resist Qin's expansion. Lü Buwei, as chief minister, would need to navigate this dangerous geopolitical landscape while also managing the internal affairs of the kingdom and preparing the young king for the responsibilities that awaited him.

## The Question of Historical Reliability

The early life of Ying Zheng, as presented in the traditional sources, raises important questions about historical reliability that any modern account must address. The primary source for this period is Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian*, compiled roughly a century after the events it describes. Sima Qian had access to official court records and earlier historical works that have since been lost, and his account has been enormously influential in shaping Chinese understanding of the Qin dynasty.

However, modern scholars have identified several concerns that must be kept in mind when reading Sima Qian's narrative. First, there is the matter of Sima Qian's own perspective. As a Confucian scholar writing during the Han dynasty, Sima Qian was deeply influenced by philosophical commitments that viewed Qin's Legalist governance as morally bankrupt and the First Emperor as a cautionary example of tyranny unchecked by moral constraints. This perspective may have led him to emphasize certain aspects of his narrative—particularly regarding Lü Buwei's corruption and the young king's vulnerability to manipulation—while downplaying evidence that might present Qin in a more favorable light.

Second, there is the problem of contradictory evidence. The *Records of the Grand Historian* itself contains passages that seem to contradict each other, particularly regarding the question of Ying Zheng's paternity and the precise nature of Lü Buwei's relationship with the royal family. Some passages suggest that Lü Buwei was merely a powerful minister; others hint darkly at a more intimate connection to the queen mother and possibly even to the young king himself.

Third, archaeological evidence has complicated the traditional narrative in important ways. The Shuihudi bamboo slips, discovered in 1975 and dating to the late Warring States and early Qin periods, have provided new perspectives on Qin's legal and administrative practices that sometimes differ from Sima Qian's account. While these bamboo slips do not directly address the question of Ying Zheng's early life, they have reinforced scholars' sense that the traditional narrative, written by enemies of Qin, may present a distorted picture of Qin's governance.

Despite these caveats, the basic outline of Ying Zheng's early life seems secure. He was born in Handan to Zichu, a Qin prince who had been held hostage in Zhao; he returned to Qin when his father became king; and he ascended the throne at age thirteen in 247 BCE, beginning a reign that would eventually transform the entire Chinese world. The details of this narrative—the role of Lü Buwei, the circumstances of Zichu's elevation, the nature of the regency—remain subjects of scholarly debate, but the fundamental trajectory is clear.

## The Making of a Future Emperor

As Ying Zheng assumed the throne of Qin, the stage was set for one of history's most remarkable transformations. The boy who had grown up in a foreign capital, the son of a prince who had seemed destined for obscurity, now commanded a kingdom that possessed the military power to conquer all of China. Yet the real story of his life was only beginning. The years of his minority would see the consolidation of Qin's power under Lü Buwei's guidance, the continuation of the wars that had already made Qin supreme among the Warring States, and the gradual emergence of a young king who would eventually assert his own will and vision upon the kingdom he inherited.

What kind of king would Ying Zheng become? The answer to that question would not be clear for years, as he navigated the treacherous waters of Qin court politics, surrounded by powerful ministers who had their own ambitions and agendas. But one thing was certain: the child who had been born in Handan in 259 BCE was destined to become one of history's most consequential figures—a ruler whose actions would reshape the political map of China and establish patterns of governance that would endure for millennia.

The legend of the First Emperor begins here, in the uncertain circumstances of a hostage prince's son, in the political machinations of merchants and ministers, in the ancient capitals of the Warring States world. From these humble and dangerous beginnings, Ying Zheng would rise to surpass all who had come before him—and all who would come after.

### Chapter Notes

1. **The Birth Date**: The traditional date for Ying Zheng's birth is 259 BCE, though some scholars have suggested 260 BCE based on astronomical calculations in the bamboo slip calendars. The precise year remains a matter of some debate.

2. **Lü Buwei's Historical Reputation**: Lü Buwei's reputation has undergone significant reevaluation in modern scholarship. While traditional sources portray him as a corrupt manipulator who dominated the young king, some scholars have argued that his role in Qin's governance was more positive than Sima Qian allows, and that his reforms and policies contributed to Qin's eventual success.

3. **The Hostage Prince System**: Diplomatic hostage-taking was a common practice in the Warring States period. Royal hostages were typically treated with considerable respect and often lived in relative comfort, though they were prevented from leaving their host state and were used as leverage in diplomatic negotiations.

4. **The Name "Zheng"**: The name Zheng (政) would later become part of the imperial title "First Emperor" (始皇帝), combined with the ruling house name to create 秦始皇. Some scholars have noted the irony that the character for "zheng" also means "to correct" or "to govern," suggesting that the name may have been interpreted as prophetic.
