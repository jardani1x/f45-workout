# Chapter 14: The Northern Campaign — Zhao's End (228-222 BCE)

The campaigns against Zhao represented one of the longest and most complex military struggles in Qin's campaign of unification. Unlike the swift conquests of Han and Wei, or the decisive victory over Chu, the conflict with Zhao stretched across multiple years and required the deployment of Qin's finest military commanders. The northern campaigns, which unfolded between 228 and 222 BCE, would see the destruction of the Zhao state and the elimination of one of Qin's most determined adversaries. The story of Zhao's fall is also a story of heroism and tragedy, of generals who defended their state to the last and a royal family that was ultimately scattered to the winds. The campaigns against Zhao would also test Qin's capacity for prolonged warfare and demonstrate both the strengths and limitations of the Qin military system.

The Zhao campaigns hold a particular significance in Chinese historical memory, not only for the military events themselves but also for the dramatic personalities involved. The story of Li Mu, the great general who was executed by his own government while defending the capital, became a powerful symbol of the tragedy of loyalty betrayed. Similarly, the defense of Handan, which resisted the Qin siege for so long, entered the historical imagination as an example of courage in the face of overwhelming odds.

## Zhao on the Eve of Conquest

Zhao had long been one of Qin's most formidable rivals. Situated in the northern reaches of the ancient Chinese heartland, Zhao controlled the strategic approaches to the Ordos region and the frontiers of the steppe. The state had been strengthened by the reforms of King Wuling (武靈王), who had adopted the mounted archer tactics of the nomadic peoples to create a powerful cavalry force. This military innovation had transformed Zhao into a military powerhouse capable of matching Qin in conventional warfare and exceeding it in mobile cavalry operations.

By the late third century BCE, however, Zhao had been significantly weakened by decades of conflict. The state had borne the brunt of Qin's northern campaigns, and its military had been worn down by repeated wars. The death of the great Zhao general Lian Po (廉頗) and the discrediting of another capable commander, Li Mu (李牧), had deprived Zhao of the leadership it needed to resist the Qin advance. Political instability at the Zhao court had further weakened the state's capacity for resistance.

Yet despite these difficulties, Zhao remained dangerous. The state's northern position gave it strategic depth, and its cavalry force remained a formidable weapon. The Zhao capital of Handan (邯鄲) was one of the most strongly fortified cities in the Warring States system, and the state's remaining generals were determined to defend it to the last. When Qin finally turned its attention to the final conquest of Zhao in 228 BCE, it would face a determined enemy that would fight until the very end.

## The First Campaign: Wang Jian's Northern Campaign (228 BCE)

The campaign against Zhao began in 228 BCE, following the completion of the Chu conquest. Ying Zheng assigned the campaign to Wang Jian, who had proven his strategic mastery in the southern campaigns. Wang Jian's experience in northern warfare, where he had previously fought against Zhao and other states, made him the obvious choice for this crucial operation.

The campaign began with Qin forces advancing into Zhao territory from the west. The initial phase of the campaign was a direct military offensive aimed at breaking the Zhao field army and seizing territory. According to the historical accounts, Wang Jian achieved significant victories in the early stages of the campaign, defeating Zhao forces in several engagements and advancing toward the Zhao capital.

The campaign's progress was facilitated by internal divisions within Zhao. The Zhao court had been torn by political factionalism, and the military leadership had been compromised by political interference. The general Lian Po, who had been one of Zhao's greatest assets, had been driven into exile by political enemies at court. His replacement, which some sources identify as a commander named Bian (邊), was unable to rally the same level of confidence or effectiveness.

The death of the Zhao king during this period also contributed to the instability. King You (幽繆王), who had ruled Zhao since 236 BCE, died in 228 BCE, possibly as a result of wounds sustained in battles against Qin. The succession was contested, with a prince named Jia (嘉) establishing himself as the new king. This political turmoil further weakened Zhao's capacity for resistance.

Despite these advantages, the initial Qin campaign did not achieve the complete conquest of Zhao. The siege of Handan proved to be a prolonged and difficult operation. The city was heavily fortified and well-provisioned, and the defenders put up stubborn resistance. The Zhao forces, though weakened, were still capable of mounting effective defense, and the siege dragged on through the winter months.

Wang Jian recognized that the siege could not be brought to a quick conclusion with the forces available. Accordingly, he adopted a strategy of attriting the Zhao state while maintaining pressure on the capital. Qin forces occupied large portions of Zhao territory, seizing resources and disrupting Zhao's economic base. This strategy was designed to weaken Handan's capacity for resistance over time, creating the conditions for a final assault when the city was sufficiently weakened.

## The Role of Li Mu and the Defense of Zhao

The siege of Handan continued into 228 BCE, and as the months passed, the situation for Zhao grew increasingly desperate. However, the appearance of Li Mu (李牧) as the defender of Handan changed the dynamics of the siege. Li Mu, who had previously served as a general on Zhao's northern frontier, was recalled to the capital to organize the defense. His arrival marked a turning point in the campaign.

Li Mu was one of the most capable military commanders of the Warring States period. He had earned his reputation defending Zhao's northern borders against the nomadic peoples, and he brought a level of strategic sophistication that the Qin forces had not previously encountered. Under his leadership, the defense of Handan became organized and effective. The city's walls were reinforced, supplies were carefully managed, and the defenders were coordinated into an efficient force.

The traditional accounts describe Li Mu's defense in somewhat idealized terms, emphasizing his tactical skill and his ability to inspire the defenders. According to Sima Qian, Li Mu employed defensive tactics that frustrated the Qin assault, keeping the siege stalemated despite Qin's numerical superiority (Sima Qian, *Shiji*). The Qin forces, unable to breach the city's defenses or force a surrender, found themselves locked in a prolonged campaign that threatened to drain their resources.

The stalemate at Handan presented Wang Jian with a difficult strategic choice. He could continue the siege, hoping that starvation and attrition would eventually force a surrender, or he could withdraw and regroup for a future campaign. Each option carried risks: continuing the siege would expose the Qin forces to the dangers of prolonged campaigning in difficult conditions, while withdrawal would allow Zhao to recover and strengthen its defenses.

## The Second Campaign: The Fall of Handan (228-227 BCE)

The first campaign against Zhao ended without the capture of Handan, though Qin forces had occupied substantial portions of Zhao territory. Wang Jian withdrew his main forces to reorganize and prepare for a renewed offensive. The strategic initiative, however, remained with Qin, and the following year would see the resumption of the campaign with increased force.

The second campaign, which began in 227 BCE, was assigned to a new commander: Yang Duan (楊端). Wang Jian, now elderly, had apparently withdrawn from active command, and the younger Yang Duan was given responsibility for completing the conquest of Zhao. The choice of a new commander may have reflected concerns about the stalemate under Wang Jian, or it may have been part of a broader rotation of military leadership.

Yang Duan's campaign followed a similar pattern to Wang Jian's earlier effort. The Qin forces advanced toward Handan, engaging Zhao forces in a series of battles. The key difference this time was that Li Mu, the defender who had organized Handan's resistance, was no longer in command. According to the traditional accounts, Li Mu had been recalled from the front lines and executed on false charges of treason. This was a catastrophic blow to Zhao, eliminating the general who had given the capital its best hope for survival.

Without Li Mu's leadership, the defense of Handan quickly deteriorated. The Qin forces were able to press the siege more effectively, and the city's resources began to dwindle. The defenders, lacking the coordination and inspiration that Li Mu had provided, were unable to repel the Qin assaults. In 228 BCE, the city finally fell.

The fall of Handan marked the end of Zhao as an independent state. The Zhao king, whether You (幽繆王) or Jia (嘉) depending on the precise chronology, was captured and executed. The royal family was scattered, with members either killed or taken captive. The great northern capital, which had withstood so many sieges over the centuries, was now in Qin hands.

## The Resistance of the Zhao Remnant (227-222 BCE)

The fall of Handan did not immediately end Zhao resistance. A member of the Zhao royal family, apparently a prince named Cheng (成), established a rump state in the northern region of Dai (代) and continued to resist Qin rule for several years. This remnant state, sometimes referred to as Northern Zhao, maintained a degree of independence and military capability that troubled Qin administrators.

The resistance in the north was led by the former Zhao general, Sui (隨), who rallied what remained of the Zhao military to the defense of the remnant state. For several years, this small state managed to maintain its independence, aided by the difficult terrain of the northern region and the challenges that Qin faced in projecting power so far from its base.

The existence of the Zhao remnant was a persistent irritant to Qin. It provided a focus for opposition to Qin rule and a potential base for restoration movements. The Qin court was determined to eliminate this last source of Zhao resistance, and in 222 BCE, a final campaign was launched against the northern state.

The campaign of 222 BCE, commanded by Qin forces under the general Lian (獵), achieved complete success. The Zhao remnant was destroyed, the last prince Cheng (成) was captured, and the general Sui (隨) was killed. With the elimination of this final resistance, Zhao ceased to exist as a political entity. The entire territory of the former Zhao state was now under Qin control.

## The Fate of the Zhao Royal Family

The fate of the Zhao royal family illustrated the harsh realities of conquest in the Warring States period. The rulers of Zhao were executed, their titles abolished, and their estates confiscated. The treatment of the royal family followed the pattern established in other conquered states: elimination of the old ruling elite and their replacement with Qin administrators.

However, the Zhao royal family would not entirely disappear from history. Some members of the family survived, and their descendants would later play roles in the events following the fall of Qin. The most famous of these was Zhao Tuo (趙佗), who would establish the Nanyue kingdom in the far south during the chaotic period after Qin's collapse. This ironic twist—the transformation of a captured Zhao prince into the founder of an independent kingdom beyond Qin's reach—demonstrated the unexpected ways in which conquest could shape history.

The generals who had defended Zhao met various fates. Lian Po, the veteran commander who had been driven from office before the final crisis, apparently survived to see the fall of his state. Li Mu, executed on charges of treason, became a symbol of the injustice that could attend even the most loyal servants. His death was later cited by critics of the Qin system as an example of the regime's willingness to destroy its own best defenders out of suspicion and paranoia. The tragedy of Li Mu would become a recurring theme in Chinese historical writing, serving as a warning about the dangers of political persecution of military talent.

## The Legacy of Zhao's Resistance

The resistance of Zhao, though ultimately unsuccessful, left a lasting legacy in Chinese historical memory. The defense of Handan became a symbol of courage and determination in the face of impossible odds. The story of Li Mu, in particular, resonated through subsequent Chinese history, serving as a cautionary tale about the treatment of loyal officials.

The fall of Zhao also marked a significant moment in the unification of China. The elimination of the northern state completed Qin's control over the traditional Chinese heartland, creating the territorial foundation for the new empire. The conquest of Zhao also brought Qin into direct contact with the nomadic peoples of the steppe, a relationship that would define much of Chinese history in the centuries to come.

## Strategic Consequences of Zhao's Fall

## Strategic Consequences of Zhao's Fall

The conquest of Zhao completed Qin's control over the northern heartland. With Zhao eliminated, Qin now bordered the nomadic peoples of the steppe frontier, inheriting the defensive responsibilities that Zhao had long carried. The vast territories of the north, including the strategic Ordos region, were now incorporated into the Qin state.

The fall of Zhao also changed the strategic position of the remaining states. Yan, to the northeast, was now isolated, with Qin forces on its border. Qi, to the east, faced Qin across an ever-narrowing wedge of territory. The consolidation of Qin power in the north created the conditions for the final campaigns of unification.

For Ying Zheng, the conquest of Zhao was the culmination of a long struggle. Zhao had been one of Qin's most persistent and dangerous adversaries, and its elimination removed a major obstacle to the completion of unification. The king could now turn his attention to the final campaigns against Yan and Qi, which would bring the entire Chinese world under his rule.

## Historical Perspectives on the Zhao Campaigns

The campaigns against Zhao have been the subject of considerable historical analysis, with scholars focusing on the military, political, and cultural dimensions of the conflict. The traditional Chinese historiography emphasized the role of individual commanders and the moral dimensions of the struggle. Li Mu was portrayed as a tragic hero, done to death by political enemies, while the Qin conquest was seen as an example of ruthless ambition triumphing over loyalty and virtue.

Modern scholars have adopted more analytical approaches, examining the campaigns within the context of Warring States military organization and strategy. The prolonged nature of the Zhao conflict has been attributed to the strategic difficulties of siege warfare and the challenges of projecting power into the northern regions. The role of political instability in weakening Zhao has also been emphasized, with scholars noting that the internal divisions of the Zhao court contributed as much to its defeat as any military superiority on Qin's part.

The cultural dimensions of the Zhao conquest have also attracted attention. Zhao had developed a distinctive cultural tradition, shaped by its position on the northern frontier and its interactions with the nomadic peoples. The integration of this culture into the Qin system presented challenges that would outlast the unification itself.

### Chapter Notes

The primary source for the Zhao campaigns is Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji*), which provides detailed accounts of the military operations, the commanders involved, and the fate of the Zhao royal family. However, the *Shiji* account contains some chronological difficulties, and scholars have debated the precise sequence of events.

The identity of the commanders in the Zhao campaigns has been the subject of scholarly discussion. Wang Jian's role in the initial campaign is well-established, but the details of the later campaigns and the identity of Yang Duan are less clear. Some scholars have suggested that Yang Duan may have been the same person as Lian (獵), who commanded the final campaign against the Zhao remnant.

The execution of Li Mu is one of the most controversial episodes in the campaign. Sima Qian reports that Li Mu was executed on charges of treason, but the reliability of this account has been questioned. Some scholars have suggested that Li Mu may have died in battle or that the circumstances of his death may have been different from the traditional account.

The chronology of the final campaign against the Zhao remnant is also uncertain. Most sources place the final conquest in 222 BCE, but some alternative chronologies have been proposed.
