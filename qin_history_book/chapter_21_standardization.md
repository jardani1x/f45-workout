# Chapter 21: Standardization — One Empire, One System

The unification of China under Qin Shi Huang in 221 BCE was not merely a political conquest; it was an ambitious project of cultural and social transformation. The new empire required not only a new political order but also a new set of common standards that would allow the diverse populations of the former Warring States to function as a single political community. This chapter examines the comprehensive standardization program that the Qin government implemented, encompassing weights, measures, currency, writing, roads, and law. These reforms represented a conscious attempt to create "one empire, one system," and their impact on Chinese civilization would endure for millennia.

## The Ideology of Standardization

The drive to standardize the empire drew on both practical and ideological motivations. From a practical standpoint, the unification of weights, measures, currency, and writing facilitated commerce, administration, and communication across the vast territory of the empire. A merchant traveling from the eastern coast to the western frontier could now use the same scales, measures, and coins throughout his journey. An official dispatched to a distant commandery could read the same characters and understand the same legal code as his colleagues in the capital.

However, the standardization program was more than a matter of administrative convenience. It reflected a philosophical commitment to uniformity that was central to the Legalist vision of government. The Legalist thinkers who shaped Qin policy believed that a well-ordered state required the application of uniform standards throughout its territory. Variations in practice created opportunities for corruption and confusion, while standardization ensured clarity, predictability, and control.

The ideology of standardization also served political purposes. By replacing the distinctive traditions of the former states with common standards, the Qin government was attempting to create a unified imperial culture that would supersede the parochial identities of the Warring States. The standardization of writing, in particular, was intended to create a shared medium of communication that would foster a sense of common identity among the diverse populations of the empire.

## The Unification of Writing

Perhaps the most consequential of the standardization programs was the unification of the writing system. Before the Qin unification, the various states had developed distinct scripts that, while sharing a common origin, had diverged significantly over the centuries. A document written in the state of Chu might be nearly incomprehensible to a reader from Zhao, and the differences between the scripts created serious obstacles to communication and administration.

The standardization of writing was associated with the figure of Li Si, the Qin chancellor who played a central role in shaping the imperial system. According to historical accounts, Li Si developed a standardized script—known as the small seal script (小篆)—that was adopted as the official writing system throughout the empire. This script was simpler than the variants used in the various states, facilitating both learning and writing.

The new script was promulgated throughout the empire through the establishment of writing standards and the distribution of model texts. Official documents were required to use the standardized script, and local officials were trained in its use. The penalties for using non-standard forms were severe, creating strong incentives for compliance.

The standardization of writing had profound consequences that extended far beyond the immediate administrative benefits. By creating a common medium of communication, the Qin government facilitated the spread of ideas and information throughout the empire. The standardized script also created the foundation for a shared literary culture that would develop in the Han dynasty and subsequent periods.

The script that Li Si developed eventually gave way to other forms—the clerical script (隶书) of the Han period and the regular script (楷书) of later centuries—but the basic principles of the Chinese writing system remained constant. The characters that the Qin government standardized in the third century BCE are essentially recognizable to modern Chinese readers, a testament to the enduring impact of the standardization program.

The unification of writing was also significant for the transmission of knowledge. Before the standardization, texts had to be copied by scribes who were familiar with the particular script of the original, a process that was time-consuming and prone to error. With a standardized script, texts could be copied accurately throughout the empire, facilitating the preservation and dissemination of knowledge.

## Weights and Measures

The standardization of weights and measures was another essential component of the Qin program. Before the unification, each state had maintained its own systems of measurement, creating confusion in trade and complicating the calculation of taxes. A measure of grain that was valid in one state might be significantly different from the equivalent measure in another, and the discrepancies created opportunities for fraud and dispute.

The Qin government established uniform standards for all weights and measures throughout the empire. These standards were promulgated from the capital and enforced through the official system of verification. Officials were required to ensure that the weights and measures used in their jurisdictions conformed to the imperial standards, and violations were punished as serious offenses.

The basic unit of length was the chi (尺), approximately 23 centimeters in modern measurement. This unit was subdivided into smaller units and aggregated into larger ones, creating a coherent system of linear measurement. The standard chi was reproduced in bronze and distributed throughout the empire, providing physical objects against which local measures could be checked.

Volume was measured in the dou (斗), a unit that was similarly standardized and verified. The dou was the basic unit for measuring grain and other commodities, and its standardization facilitated the collection of taxes in kind. The uniformity of measures meant that the tax burden was distributed more equitably, though the overall level of taxation remained high.

Weight was measured in the jin (斤), a unit that would persist in Chinese usage for over two millennia. The standardization of weight was essential for the proper functioning of markets, where goods were bought and sold by weight. The uniformity of standards meant that a merchant could trust that the weight indicated by his scale was accurate, facilitating commerce across the empire.

The standardization of weights and measures also had important administrative implications. The calculation of taxes, the allocation of resources, and the provisioning of the military all depended on accurate measurement. By establishing uniform standards, the Qin government created the foundation for efficient administration throughout the empire.

## Currency

The unification of currency was another essential element of the standardization program. Before the Qin conquest, the various states had minted their own coins, using different metals, different shapes, and different denominational systems. The coins of Qi, for example, were not interchangeable with the coins of Chu, and the diversity of currencies created significant obstacles to inter-state trade.

The Qin government established a uniform currency system throughout the empire. The standard coin was the ban liang (半两), a round bronze coin with a square hole in the center, weighing half a liang (approximately 8 grams). This coin was minted at the capital and distributed throughout the empire, replacing the varied currencies of the former states.

The ban liang was not the first round coin in Chinese history—the Warring States states had already begun minting round coins as a replacement for the spade money that had characterized earlier periods—but its uniform adoption throughout the empire was unprecedented. The coin's design was simple and efficient, and its weight was standardized to ensure consistency.

In addition to the standard coin, the Qin government also regulated the use of precious metals as currency. Gold, in the form of ingots known as "yi" (镒), was used for large transactions, while bronze coins served for everyday commerce. This bimetallic system provided flexibility while maintaining the uniformity that the empire required.

The standardization of currency had immediate economic benefits. Merchants could now conduct business throughout the empire without having to exchange one currency for another. The uniform coinage facilitated long-distance trade and contributed to the economic integration of the formerly separate states.

However, the currency system also faced challenges. The ban liang coin was relatively heavy, making it inconvenient for small transactions. The government attempted to address this by minting smaller denominations, but the supply of small coins was often inadequate. Moreover, the high taxes that the Qin government imposed meant that much of the available coinage flowed to the capital, creating shortages in the provinces.

## The Legal Code

The standardization of law was perhaps the most controversial aspect of the Qin program. The various Warring States had developed distinct legal traditions, and the imposition of a uniform legal code throughout the empire required the replacement of local practices with standardized regulations. The Qin legal code was rigorous, comprehensive, and notorious for its severity.

The codification of Qin law had begun before the unification, under the direction of Li Si and other Legalist reformers. The resulting code was a comprehensive system of regulations covering all aspects of social and economic life. The laws addressed matters ranging from agriculture and commerce to family relations and criminal justice, creating a comprehensive framework for the regulation of human behavior.

The fundamental principle of Qin law was the equality of all before the legal code. Unlike the legal systems of the feudal period, which had treated aristocrats differently from commoners, Qin law applied the same standards to everyone. An official who committed a crime was subject to the same punishment as a peasant who committed the same offense.

The punishments prescribed by Qin law were severe. The code included a wide range of corporal punishments, including mutilation and death, as well as punishments that affected entire families. The principle of collective punishment meant that the relatives of a criminal could be held responsible for his actions, creating strong incentives for conformity.

The harshness of Qin law has been widely criticized, both in ancient times and in the modern period. Confucian scholars, in particular, viewed the Legalist legal system as morally bankrupt, arguing that it relied on coercion rather than moral education. The rebellions that eventually toppled the Qin dynasty were motivated in part by resentment against the harsh legal system.

However, it is important to recognize the functions that the legal system served. The certainty of punishment created incentives for compliance, and the uniformity of the legal code ensured that individuals knew what was expected of them. The system was designed to maintain order and discipline, and in this it was largely successful.

The Qin legal code would not survive the dynasty, but many of its principles would persist in subsequent legal systems. The idea that law should be uniform and that everyone should be subject to the same standards was a lasting contribution to Chinese political culture, even if the specific provisions of the Qin code were modified or abandoned.

## Roads and Communication

The maintenance of communication across the vast territory of the empire was essential for effective administration. The Qin government therefore invested heavily in the construction of roads and the development of communication systems that would allow information to flow between the capital and the most distant regions.

The most famous of the Qin road projects was the straight road (直道) that connected the capital at Xianyang to the northern frontier. This road, which was constructed during the reign of Qin Shi Huang, stretched over 800 kilometers and traversed difficult terrain. According to historical accounts, the construction of the road required massive labor inputs and involved significant engineering challenges.

The straight road was designed primarily for military purposes, allowing the rapid deployment of forces to the northern frontier where the Xiongnu posed a constant threat. However, the road also facilitated civilian communication and commerce, contributing to the economic integration of the empire.

In addition to the major roads, the Qin government constructed an extensive network of smaller roads that connected the commandery capitals with the county seats and the surrounding countryside. This network facilitated the collection of taxes, the movement of officials, and the transmission of orders from the central government.

The road system was complemented by a communication system that allowed the central government to receive information from throughout the empire. Official messengers traveled the roads, carrying documents and reports between the capital and the provinces. The speed of communication was a measure of the efficiency of the administrative system, and the Qin government maintained a corps of professional messengers who could cover great distances quickly.

The construction of roads and the development of communication systems required massive labor inputs. The corvée labor system that the Qin government employed for public works projects drew on the entire adult male population, requiring individuals to spend a specified number of days each year working on infrastructure projects. This system was efficient but burdensome, and it generated significant resentment among the population.

## The Axial Standardization

One of the most distinctive aspects of the Qin standardization program was the regulation of cart axle lengths. Before the unification, the various states had used carts with different axle widths, meaning that carts from one state could not travel efficiently on the roads of another. This variation created obstacles to trade and complicated the transport of goods throughout the Chinese world.

The Qin government established a uniform standard for cart axle lengths, requiring all carts used on imperial roads to conform to a single specification. This standardization facilitated the movement of goods and people throughout the empire, contributing to economic integration.

The axle standard also had important implications for road construction. By ensuring that all carts used the same gauge, the government could construct roads that were optimized for the standard cart design. This created a positive feedback loop in which better roads led to more traffic, which in turn justified further investment in road infrastructure.

The standardization of axle lengths was a relatively minor aspect of the overall program, but it illustrates the comprehensiveness of the Qin approach to unification. Even the most technical aspects of daily life were brought under imperial regulation, creating a uniformity that was unprecedented in Chinese history.

## The Construction of the Imperial Network

The standardization program was complemented by an ambitious construction program that created the physical infrastructure for the unified empire. In addition to roads, the Qin government constructed canals, palaces, and defensive works that facilitated administration and demonstrated the power of the new regime.

The most significant canal project was the connecting of the various river systems of the empire through a network of artificial waterways. These canals facilitated the transport of grain from the productive southern regions to the capital and the northern frontier, providing the logistical foundation for the empire's military and administrative operations.

The construction of the Epang Palace (阿房宫) at the capital was intended to symbolize the power and majesty of the imperial regime. Although the palace was never completed, its planned dimensions indicate the scale of Qin imperial ambitions. The palace complex was designed to accommodate the emperor and his court, providing a setting for the elaborate rituals that expressed imperial authority.

The Qin government also constructed a network of watchtowers and defensive works along the northern frontier. These fortifications, which would eventually be connected to form the Great Wall, were designed to protect the empire from the nomadic peoples of the steppe. The construction of these works required massive labor inputs and contributed to the resentment that would eventually topple the dynasty.

## Resistance and Enforcement

The standardization program was not implemented without resistance. The populations of the former Warring States had developed distinct traditions over centuries, and the imposition of uniform standards required the abandonment of practices that were deeply ingrained. The Qin government relied on a combination of incentives and penalties to ensure compliance.

The enforcement of standards was the responsibility of the official bureaucracy. Local magistrates were required to verify that weights, measures, and coins in their jurisdictions conformed to imperial standards. Officials who failed to maintain proper standards were subject to punishment, creating incentives for rigorous enforcement.

The legal system supported the standardization program by prescribing severe penalties for violations. Merchants who used false weights or measures were subject to harsh punishments, as were officials who failed to detect or report such violations. The certainty of punishment created strong incentives for compliance.

However, the standardization program also faced more subtle forms of resistance. In many regions, the old practices persisted despite official prohibition, particularly in areas distant from the centers of administrative control. The standardization was most effective in the core regions of the former Qin state, where the administrative infrastructure was strongest, and less effective in the more distant territories.

## The Legacy of Standardization

The standardization program of the Qin empire had lasting consequences that extended far beyond the dynasty itself. The uniform systems of writing, weights, measures, currency, and law that the Qin government established became the foundation for Chinese civilization for the next two millennia.

The standardization of writing was perhaps the most enduring achievement. The script that Li Si developed in the third century BCE became the basis for the Chinese writing system, which would remain essentially unchanged despite the political upheavals that followed. The ability to read and write standardized characters became a mark of education and refinement, creating a shared literary culture that transcended regional boundaries.

The standardization of weights and measures also persisted, with the basic units of length, volume, and weight remaining in use for over two thousand years. The jin, the chi, and the dou would be used in Chinese markets until the twentieth century, a testament to the effectiveness of the Qin standardization.

The legal principles that the Qin code established—that law should be uniform, that officials should be subject to the same standards as ordinary citizens, that punishment should fit the crime—would continue to influence Chinese legal thinking even after the specific provisions of the Qin code were abandoned. The idea of a comprehensive legal code that governs all aspects of social life was a lasting contribution to Chinese political culture.

The infrastructure of roads and canals that the Qin government constructed facilitated economic integration and administrative control. The basic network of transportation routes that the Qin created would remain important throughout Chinese history, even as the specific roads were maintained, improved, or allowed to decay.

## Conclusion

The standardization program that the Qin government implemented after the unification of 221 BCE represented one of the most ambitious projects of social engineering in human history. The attempt to create "one empire, one system"—with uniform writing, weights, measures, currency, law, and infrastructure—was unprecedented in scale and in its implications for the future of Chinese civilization.

The motivations for standardization were both practical and ideological. Practically, uniform systems facilitated administration, commerce, and communication across the vast territory of the empire. Ideologically, standardization reflected the Legalist belief that order required uniformity and that variation created opportunities for confusion and corruption.

The implementation of the standardization program was not uniform in its effectiveness. Some aspects—particularly the standardization of writing and the legal code—had lasting impact, while others were more ephemeral. Moreover, the methods used to enforce standardization were often harsh, generating resentment that contributed to the instability of the regime.

Despite these limitations, the legacy of Qin standardization was profound. The systems that the Qin government established became the foundation for Chinese civilization for over two millennia. The unified writing system created a shared medium of communication that transcended regional boundaries. The standardized weights and measures facilitated commerce and administration. The uniform legal code established principles that would influence Chinese jurisprudence for centuries. And the infrastructure of roads and canals contributed to the economic integration of the empire.

The Qin standardization program thus represents one of the most consequential achievements of the first emperor's reign. The "one empire, one system" that Qin Shi Huang created shaped the political, economic, and cultural development of China for generations to come.

### Chapter Notes

The standardization program of the Qin empire has been the subject of extensive scholarly attention. The traditional view, derived from sources such as the *Records of the the Grand Historian*, presents the program as a comprehensive and well-ordered system implemented uniformly throughout the empire. Modern scholarship has emphasized the complexity and unevenness of the actual implementation, noting that local variations persisted despite official standardization.

The dating of specific standardization measures is sometimes uncertain. The unification of writing is generally attributed to the period immediately following the conquest of the six states, but the details of when each element of the standardization program was implemented are not always clear.

The assessment of the standardization program's impact has also been debated. Some scholars emphasize its practical benefits, arguing that it facilitated economic development and administrative efficiency. Others have focused on the hardships that enforcement created, noting the resentment that resulted from the harsh methods used to ensure compliance.
