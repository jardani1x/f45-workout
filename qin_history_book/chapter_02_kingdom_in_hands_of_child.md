# Chapter 2: A Kingdom in the Hands of a Child

When Ying Zheng ascended the throne of Qin in 247 BCE at the age of thirteen, the kingdom faced a challenge that was both political and practical: how should a state ruled by a child be governed? The Warring States period had seen its share of minority rulers, but few had inherited a power as vast and as precariously balanced as Qin. The young king commanded an army that had already proven itself the most formidable in China, a bureaucracy that had been refined by generations of Legalist administrators, and a treasury that flowed with the wealth of a kingdom that had been expanding for decades. Yet all of this strength was now potentially vulnerable, dependent on the wisdom and loyalty of those who would govern in the name of a boy who had not yet reached adulthood.

The regency that followed—lasting from 247 BCE until 238 BCE—was one of the most critical periods in Qin's history. It was during these years that the foundations were laid for the eventual conquest of the Warring States, but it was also a time of internal tension, political maneuvering, and the slow emergence of a young king who would eventually reject the tutelage of those who claimed to guide him. Understanding this period is essential to understanding how Qin Shi Huang, the First Emperor, became the ruler he would ultimately become.

## The Queen Mother: Zhao Jiao (Queen Dowager Zhao)

At the center of the regency government stood not merely Lü Buwei, the chief minister, but also the queen mother—Zhao Jiao (赵姬), formerly of the Zhao court, who had accompanied King Zhuangxiang from Handan to Xianyang and who now held the title of Queen Mother (太后).[^1]

[^1]: Zhao Jiao is a conventional appellation used in this text; Sima Qian records her only as "the Zhao woman" (赵姬). Her personal name is unattested in contemporaneous sources.

Modern scholars have approached this traditional portrait with considerable caution. Sima Qian wrote with a Confucian moral framework that viewed such behavior—particularly the sexual impropriety of a queen mother—as evidence of the corruption that inevitably accompanied non-Confucian governance. The portrayal of Zhao Jiao may therefore reflect as much about Sima Qian's moral concerns as about the historical reality of the queen mother's conduct.

What can be stated with greater confidence is that Zhao Jiao, as the mother of the young king, occupied a position of immense symbolic and practical importance. In the hierarchical world of Warring States politics, the queen mother was not merely a royal consort but an institution—she represented the continuity of the dynasty, the link between generations, and the guardian of royal tradition. Whatever her personal conduct, Zhao Jiao's support was essential to the legitimacy of any regency government, and Lü Buwei was careful to maintain the appearance of close coordination with the queen mother throughout his years in power.

## Lü Buwei: The Dominant Minister

Lü Buwei's position during the regency was unlike anything that had come before in Qin's history. Previous chief ministers had been powerful—Shang Yang had reshaped the entire kingdom through his Legalist reforms a century earlier, and subsequent ministers had maintained considerable authority—but none had exercised the combination of executive and symbolic power that Lü Buwei now possessed. He was not merely the chief minister; he was, in effect, the ruler of Qin in all but name.

The traditional account presents Lü Buwei as a figure of almost unbounded ambition and cunning. According to Sima Qian, he manipulated the young king, controlled the queen mother, and used his position to enrich himself and his associates. He is said to have commissioned the *Annals of Lü Buwei* (吕氏春秋), a massive encyclopedic work that he allegedly published in hopes of establishing himself as a universal teacher and sage—a claim that traditional historians viewed as evidence of his arrogance.

Modern scholars have offered more nuanced interpretations of Lü Buwei's rule. Some have emphasized the positive aspects of his governance: the continuation of Qin's administrative stability, the maintenance of its military readiness, and the careful management of foreign relations during a period when the other Warring States were attempting to form coalitions against Qin. Others have noted that Lü Buwei, unlike the strict Legalists who had previously dominated Qin, appears to have embraced a more eclectic philosophical approach—drawing on elements of Taoism, Confucianism, and Legalism in his attempted synthesis.

The archaeological evidence from Shuihudi provides some support for this more complex picture. The bamboo slips, which include legal documents, administrative records, and philosophical texts, reveal a Qin government that was more sophisticated and pragmatic than the stark Legalist stereotype might suggest. While Legalist principles remained central to Qin's self-understanding, the actual practice of governance appears to have involved a more flexible approach to law, administration, and social control than the rigid doctrine usually associated with Qin.

## The Young King's Upbringing

What do we know about Ying Zheng's childhood and education during the regency? The traditional sources are relatively sparse on details, but they suggest a young king who was carefully prepared for his eventual role. According to Sima Qian, the boy king was educated in the traditions of the Qin royal house, instructed in the Legalist philosophy that had made Qin great, and gradually exposed to the affairs of state as he approached adulthood.

The education of a Warring States prince was a serious matter, involving not merely academic instruction but also physical conditioning, military training, and the development of the character appropriate to a ruler. Young nobles learned archery, chariot driving, and military tactics; they studied history, rhetoric, and philosophy; and they were expected to develop the personal virtues—wisdom, benevolence, courage, and self-control—that would enable them to govern effectively.

For Ying Zheng, this education was complicated by the unusual circumstances of his position. He was king, but he was also a minor; he possessed ultimate authority, but he could not exercise it. This tension may have shaped his psychology in ways that would later become apparent. The experience of waiting—of being told that he was destined for greatness but being denied the power to act—may have contributed to the intensity and ambition that would characterize his later reign.

Some scholars have suggested that Ying Zheng's childhood in the Qin court was lonely and isolated—that he was surrounded by advisors and attendants who served the regency rather than the king, and that he had few genuine relationships of trust. Again, this interpretation must be offered cautiously, as it relies heavily on psychological speculation rather than direct evidence. But it is worth noting that the young king's later behavior—the secrecy, the paranoia, the difficulty trusting others—may have roots in the unusual circumstances of his youth.

## State Affairs During the Regency

Despite the political complications of minority rule, Qin's affairs of state continued with remarkable continuity during the regency period. The kingdom's bureaucracy, built up over generations of Legalist administration, maintained its efficiency; the military, under the command of skilled generals, continued its campaigns of expansion; and the economy, based on a system of strict legal controls and incentives, continued to generate the wealth that funded Qin's ambitions.

The most significant military developments during this period involved Qin's ongoing conflicts with its neighbors, particularly the states of Zhao and Chu. According to Sima Qian, Lü Buwei pursued a policy of strategic patience—continuing Qin's military pressure on its rivals while avoiding the costly full-scale invasions that would later characterize the unification wars. This approach allowed Qin to maintain its position as the dominant power without overextending itself, while the other Warring States remained too divided and suspicious of each other to form effective coalitions.

Archaeological evidence suggests that administrative reforms continued apace during the regency. The Shuihudi bamboo slips, which date from the late third century BCE, include legal documents that reveal the complexity of Qin's governance: detailed codes governing agriculture, commerce, and criminal justice; records of tax collection and labor conscription; and evidence of the elaborate system of rewards and punishments that was central to Legalist administration. These documents suggest a state that was highly organized and efficient—a machine for extracting resources and mobilizing manpower that would prove essential to the conquests to come.

One of the most important developments of the regency period was the continued influence of the philosophical school known as Legalism. Unlike Confucianism, which emphasized moral virtue and the benevolent rule of gentlemen, Legalism focused on the creation of effective systems of law, administration, and control. The Legalists believed that human beings were naturally selfish and disorderly, and that only through strict laws, severe punishments, and carefully designed incentives could a ruler maintain social order and military effectiveness.

Qin had been the testing ground for Legalist ideas for over a century, ever since Shang Yang's reforms in the fourth century BCE had transformed the kingdom. The regency period saw the continuation of this Legalist framework, but also some signs of modification and adaptation. Lü Buwei, as chief minister, appears to have been more eclectic than his predecessors—willing to draw on Taoist and Confucian ideas where they seemed useful, while maintaining the basic Legalist structure of Qin governance.

## Qin's Growing Strength

By the end of the regency period in 238 BCE, Qin had never been stronger. The kingdom controlled a vast territory in the western part of the Chinese world, with borders stretching from the modern province of Gansu in the west to the frontiers of Zhao and Chu in the east. Its population had grown through both natural increase and the absorption of conquered peoples; its treasury was full; and its army was the largest and best-trained in the Warring States world.

The other states, meanwhile, had failed to mount effective resistance. Their repeated attempts to form vertical alliances against Qin had collapsed due to mutual suspicion and self-interest; each state hoped that Qin would attack its rivals rather than itself, and none was willing to make the sacrifices necessary for collective defense. This division among Qin's enemies would prove fatal in the years to come, as the kingdom moved toward the wars of unification.

Yet Qin's strength was not merely military or economic. The regency period had also seen the development of a new imperial ideology—a sense that Qin was not merely one state among many, but was destined for something greater. This ideology would later manifest in the adoption of the title "皇帝" (Emperor), a new term that combined elements of the ancient Zhou royal title with the more recent concept of universal rule. But even before this dramatic innovation, there were signs that Qin saw itself as different from—and superior to—the other Warring States.

## The Question of Regency Governance

The regency period raises important questions about the nature of governance in early China. How did a kingdom function when its nominal ruler was a child? What mechanisms existed to ensure continuity and stability? And what does the Qin experience tell us about the relationship between personal authority and institutional power?

The answers to these questions reveal both the strengths and weaknesses of the Qin system. On one hand, the bureaucracy that Shang Yang and his successors had built was capable of functioning effectively even without strong personal leadership. The legal codes, administrative procedures, and chain of command that they created provided a framework within which government could continue regardless of who occupied the throne. This institutional continuity was a major factor in Qin's long-term success.

On the other hand, the regency also revealed the potential for abuse inherent in a system that concentrated so much power in the hands of a single minister. Lü Buwei was able to use his position to accumulate wealth, influence, and authority in ways that might have been impossible in a more balanced political system. The traditional account suggests that he became increasingly arrogant and reckless as the years of the regency passed, confident that the young king would never be able to challenge his dominance.

This confidence, as we shall see, would prove to be Lü Buwei's fatal miscalculation.

## The Emergence of a King

As Ying Zheng approached his twenty-second year, the question of his majority became increasingly urgent. In the Warring States period, the age of twenty-two was traditionally considered the age at which a ruler was sufficiently mature to take personal control of government. The political system had been designed with this transition in mind, creating a clear moment at which power would pass from regent to king.

For Lü Buwei, this transition represented a profound challenge. He had governed Qin for nearly a decade, building up an enormous network of supporters, allies, and dependents who owed their positions to his patronage. If Ying Zheng took personal control of the government, many of these figures would be vulnerable to removal—or worse. Lü Buwei's survival, and perhaps his life, depended on maintaining his influence over the young king.

The traditional account suggests that Lü Buwei attempted to maintain his control through a variety of means—including, allegedly, the provision of a young woman to the king as a concubine, in hopes of creating a new influence over the ruler through intimate relationships. Whether this story is true or not—and modern scholars have expressed skepticism—it reflects the traditional view of Lü Buwei as a manipulator who would stop at nothing to preserve his power.

What seems clear is that by 238 BCE, the regency was approaching its end. The young king had grown into a man, and the expectations of the court and the country demanded that he assume his rightful authority. The stage was set for a dramatic confrontation that would determine the future direction of the Qin state.

## Historical Status and Archaeological Perspectives

The regency period is one of the most heavily documented eras in early Chinese history, due primarily to Sima Qian's detailed account in the *Records of the Grand Historian*. Yet this very richness creates interpretive challenges, as Sima Qian's Confucian perspective may have shaped his portrayal of the events and characters involved.

Modern scholars have approached the regency narrative with a critical eye, noting the many ways in which Sima Qian's account reflects the moral concerns of later generations rather than the historical realities of the third century BCE. The portrayal of Lü Buwei as a corrupt manipulator, for example, may say as much about later Confucian attitudes toward merchant-politicians as about Lü Buwei himself. Similarly, the depiction of Zhao Jiao's alleged improprieties may reflect gender biases and moralizing tendencies that had little to do with the actual conduct of the queen mother.

Archaeological evidence has provided some corrective to these biases. The Shuihudi bamboo slips, discovered in 1975 in Hubei Province, offer a glimpse into Qin's legal and administrative practices that sometimes contradicts the traditional narrative. These documents reveal a government that was highly organized, legally sophisticated, and surprisingly pragmatic—a system that was less brutal and more complex than the stark Legalist stereotype usually associated with Qin.

The bamboo slips have also provided new perspectives on Qin's relationship with its subjects. Rather than the picture of unremitting oppression that traditional sources sometimes suggest, the legal documents reveal a system in which ordinary people had certain rights and protections, and in which the government was concerned with maintaining social order through a variety of means—not merely through harsh punishment.

Despite these archaeological insights, many questions about the regency period remain unanswered. The bamboo slips do not directly address the political drama of Lü Buwei's dominance or the relationship between the regent and the young king. For these aspects of the narrative, scholars continue to rely primarily on Sima Qian's account, while remaining aware of its limitations.

## The Path to Power

As the regency period drew to its close, Ying Zheng stood on the threshold of history. He had grown from a boy who had known only the precarious life of a hostage's son to a young man who commanded the most powerful state in the Warring States world. The kingdom he inherited was strong, its military was ready, and its bureaucracy was efficient. Yet the greatest challenges still lay ahead—the wars of unification that would reshape China, the creation of a new imperial order, and the internal struggles that would test his character and resolve.

The regency had been a period of preparation—nine years during which Qin had continued to grow stronger while the young king waited for his moment. That moment was now approaching. In 238 BCE, Ying Zheng would formally assume personal rule of Qin, and the world would see what kind of emperor he would become.

### Chapter Notes

1. **The Age of Majority**: The age at which Warring States rulers were considered mature enough to govern varied, but twenty-two was a common standard. Some states used twenty, while others followed different traditions.

2. **Lü Buwei's Philosophical Eclecticism**: The *Annals of Lü Buwei* (吕氏春秋), which Lü Buwei reportedly commissioned, represents a remarkable attempt to synthesize the various philosophical schools of the Warring States period. The work includes material from Confucian, Taoist, Legalist, and other traditions, suggesting that Lü Buwei's governance was less ideologically rigid than the traditional portrayal suggests.

3. **Shuihudi Dating**: The Shuihudi bamboo slips date primarily from the late third century BCE, with many documents from the period of the regency or shortly after. They provide invaluable evidence for Qin's legal and administrative practices during this period.

4. **Qin's Neighbors**: During the regency, Qin maintained a complex set of relationships with the other Warring States. While military campaigns continued, diplomatic maneuvers and strategic calculations also played important roles in maintaining Qin's dominant position.
