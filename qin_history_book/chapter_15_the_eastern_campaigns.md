# Chapter 15: The Eastern Campaigns — Yan and Qi (226-221 BCE)

The campaigns against Yan and Qi represented the final phase of Qin's campaign of unification. With Chu, Zhao, and Wei eliminated, only two major states remained: Yan in the northeast and Qi in the east. These campaigns, which unfolded between 226 and 221 BCE, would bring the Warring States era to its conclusion and create the first unified empire in Chinese history. The story of these final campaigns is one of contrast: Yan's desperate resistance and ultimate collapse, followed by Qin's diplomatic and military pressure on Qi, which surrendered almost without a fight. The completion of unification in 221 BCE marked the end of centuries of political fragmentation and the beginning of a new imperial age that would shape Chinese civilization for millennia.

The campaigns against Yan and Qi also had important implications for the future development of the Chinese empire. The incorporation of the northeastern territories brought Qin into contact with new peoples and cultures, while the eastern regions would become some of the most economically important parts of the new empire.

## The State of Yan on the Eve of Conquest

Yan occupied the northeastern corner of the Chinese world, controlling the territories around modern-day Beijing and Hebei Province. The state had a long history dating back to the Spring and Autumn period, and it had developed a distinctive cultural tradition shaped by its interactions with the nomadic peoples of the north and the various tribal groups of the northeast.

By the late third century BCE, Yan had been significantly weakened by its conflicts with Qi and Zhao. The state had suffered a catastrophic defeat at the hands of Qi earlier in the century, and it had never fully recovered. The territorial losses to Zhao in the west had further diminished Yan's resources and strategic position. Yet despite these weaknesses, Yan remained a significant power, and its geographic position made it a potential threat to Qin's northern flank.

The Yan court was aware of the danger posed by Qin's expansion. As Qin eliminated the other major states, Yan found itself increasingly isolated, with Qin forces on its border and no potential allies remaining. The Yan king, whose title was Xi (喜), recognized the existential threat and sought to prepare his state for the coming confrontation.

## The Campaign Against Yan (226-222 BCE)

The campaign against Yan began in 226 BCE, following the completion of the conquests of Chu and Zhao. The Qin forces were commanded by Wang Ben (王賁), the son of the veteran general Wang Jian, who had led the campaigns against Chu. Wang Ben had already proven himself in the campaign against Wei, and his selection for the Yan campaign reflected Qin's confidence in his abilities.

The campaign began with Qin forces advancing into Yan territory from the west. The initial stages of the campaign were successful, with Qin forces achieving several victories over the Yan army and advancing toward the Yan capital of Ji (薊). The speed of the Qin advance reflected the weakened state of Yan, which was unable to mount effective resistance with its diminished military.

The Yan king Xi (喜) recognized that the capital could not be held against the Qin assault. Rather than awaiting capture or execution, he chose to flee. According to the historical accounts, the king abandoned the capital and withdrew to the northeastern region of Liaodong (遼東), which was separated from the main Chinese heartland by mountainous terrain. This retreat was an attempt to preserve the Yan royal family and to maintain some base of resistance in the remote northeast.

Wang Ben pursued the fleeing Yan king, following the retreating forces across the mountains. The campaign in Liaodong was a difficult operation, as the terrain favored the defenders and the Qin forces were operating far from their base. However, the Qin army was eventually successful, and in 226 BCE, the Yan king Xi (喜) was captured and executed. The capital of Ji (薊) had already fallen, and with the death of the king, Yan ceased to exist as an independent state.

## The Suicide of the Prince of Yan at Ji

However, the elimination of the Yan king did not end all resistance. A member of the Yan royal family, known as the Prince of Yan (燕太子), escaped the initial conquest and sought to rally opposition to Qin rule. This prince, whose name is given in some sources as Tian (田) or in others simply as the Prince (太子), became the focus of resistance in the former Yan territories.

The Prince of Yan initially attempted to organize a guerrilla campaign against Qin rule. He gathered support among the local population and among nobles who remained loyal to the Yan dynasty. For a time, this resistance posed a significant challenge to Qin administrators, who struggled to maintain control over the restive population.

However, Qin forces under Wang Ben eventually tracked down the Prince of Yan at the city of Yi (衍), also identified as a location in the Liaodong region. The final engagement was decisive: the prince's forces were defeated, and the last major resistance in Yan territory was eliminated. According to some accounts, the Prince of Yan chose to commit suicide rather than be captured, ending his life in a final act of defiance (Sima Qian, *Shiji*).

The death of the Prince of Yan marked the effective end of organized resistance in the former Yan territories. The Qin now faced the task of consolidating their control over the northeastern region and integrating it into the administrative structure of the empire. This process would take time, and the region would remain somewhat unstable in the years following the conquest.

## The Strategic Position After Yan's Fall

The fall of Yan had important strategic consequences for the unification campaign. With Yan eliminated, Qin now controlled the northeastern approaches to the Chinese heartland. The former Yan territories, including the strategically important Liaodong peninsula, were incorporated into the Qin state. This expansion brought Qin into closer contact with the peoples of the Korean peninsula and the Manchurian frontier, extending the reach of Chinese civilization into these regions.

The conquest of Yan also completed Qin's encirclement of the remaining state of Qi. With Yan gone, Qi was now surrounded on three sides by Qin-controlled territory, with only the sea to the east providing any separation. The strategic position of Qi had become untenable, and the final confrontation between Qin and Qi was now only a matter of time.

## The State of Qi on the Eve of Conquest

Qi occupied the easternmost position among the Warring States, controlling the fertile Shandong Peninsula and the surrounding regions. The state had been a major power in the fourth century BCE, but it had experienced a long decline that had left it unable to resist the rising power of Qin. By the time of the unification campaigns, Qi was a shadow of its former greatness, lacking the military capacity and the political cohesion to mount effective resistance.

The internal politics of Qi had contributed to its weakness. The state had been ruled by a series of kings who had proved unable to adapt to the changing strategic environment. The Qi military, while not insignificant, was no match for the professional forces of Qin. Most importantly, the Qi court had lost the will to resist, and many influential figures advocated surrender as the only realistic option.

This decline was reflected in Qin's assessment of the campaign. Unlike the campaigns against Chu, Zhao, or even Yan, the conquest of Qi was expected to be relatively easy. The strategic and political conditions favored a rapid resolution, and Qin prepared accordingly.

## The Campaign Against Qi (221 BCE)

The final campaign of unification began in 221 BCE, when Qin forces invaded Qi from the west. The campaign was led by the general Xin (新), though the historical sources provide limited details about his identity and background. What is clear is that the campaign was conducted with overwhelming force and with the expectation of a quick victory.

The Qi forces offered minimal resistance. The state's military, demoralized and outmatched, was unable to mount effective opposition to the Qin advance. The capital of Linzi (臨淄), one of the largest and most prosperous cities in the Warring States world, fell without a significant battle. The Qi king, whose title was Jian (建), was captured and executed, and the state was annexed into the Qin empire.

The conquest of Qi was remarkable for its speed and relative bloodlessness. Unlike the campaigns against Chu and Zhao, which had involved major battles and prolonged sieges, the Qi campaign was essentially a triumphal procession. The state simply collapsed before the Qin advance, reflecting the extent of its internal weakness and the psychological impact of Qin's earlier victories.

## The Diplomatic Surrender of Qi

The ease of the Qi conquest was not merely a matter of military superiority; it also reflected a deliberate strategy of diplomatic pressure. In the years preceding the final campaign, Qin had cultivated relationships with influential figures in the Qi court, encouraging them to advocate surrender rather than resistance. This diplomatic offensive was designed to weaken Qi's will to fight and to create internal divisions that would facilitate the conquest.

The effectiveness of this strategy became apparent as the Qin forces advanced. Rather than rallying to the defense of their king, many Qi nobles and officials simply accepted the new order. The surrender was almost bloodless, with the Qin forces meeting resistance only from a few isolated garrisons that were quickly overwhelmed.

This diplomatic approach was consistent with Qin's broader strategy of minimizing the costs of conquest wherever possible. By encouraging surrender and exploiting divisions within the enemy state, Qin was able to achieve its objectives without the massive casualties that would have attended a prolonged campaign. The result was the most efficient of all the major conquests, accomplished with minimal disruption to the existing social order.

## The Completion of Unification (221 BCE)

The conquest of Qi in 221 BCE marked the completion of the unification of China under Qin rule. All of the major Warring States had now been eliminated, and the entire Chinese heartland was under the control of a single ruler. The era of division and competition that had characterized the previous two and a half centuries had come to an end.

The news of the final victory was received with great excitement at the Qin court. Ying Zheng, now the ruler of a unified empire, took the title of Shi Huangdi (始皇帝), the First Emperor, signifying the beginning of a new imperial order. The title was carefully chosen to indicate that this was not merely another dynasty but a fundamentally new form of political organization, one that would serve as a model for all future emperors.

The territorial extent of the new empire was vast, encompassing the heartland of ancient China from the eastern coast to the western frontiers. The former territories of Chu, Zhao, Wei, Yan, and Qi, as well as the smaller states that had been absorbed along the way, were now integrated into a single political entity. The administrative machinery of the Qin state, which had been refined over generations, was now applied to the governance of this unprecedented realm.

## The Fate of the Vanquished

The treatment of the conquered states followed the established Qin pattern. The rulers of Yan and Qi were executed, their royal families scattered or eliminated, and their territories placed under the administration of Qin commanderies. The noble families of both states were stripped of their privileges and either integrated into the Qin system or, in some cases, exiled to remote regions.

However, the policy of elimination was not always complete. In some cases, branches of the royal families were permitted to survive, either because they had cooperated with Qin or because their elimination was deemed unnecessary. The descendants of the conquered royal houses would persist in the population, and in the chaos following the fall of Qin, some would emerge as leaders of restoration movements.

The generals who had led the campaigns were richly rewarded. Wang Ben, who had commanded the Yan campaign, was ennobled and given extensive estates. His father Wang Jian, though elderly, received honors for his role in the conquests. The military leadership of Qin had proven decisive in the unification, and the commanders were recognized accordingly.

## Historical Perspectives on the Final Campaigns

The final campaigns against Yan and Qi have been the subject of various historical interpretations. The traditional Chinese historiography emphasized the contrast between Yan's desperate resistance and Qi's passive surrender, drawing moral lessons about the importance of courage and loyalty in the face of conquest. The suicide of the Prince of Yan was portrayed as an example of noble sacrifice, while the Qi surrender was seen as a sign of degeneracy and moral failure.

Modern scholars have adopted more analytical approaches, examining the campaigns within the context of the broader strategic and political developments of the period. The ease of the Qi conquest has been attributed to a combination of factors: the internal weaknesses of the Qi state, the psychological impact of Qin's earlier victories, and the effectiveness of Qin's diplomatic offensive. The Yan's resistance, in contrast, has been seen as a reflection of the state's greater military capacity and its determination to fight despite overwhelming odds.

The completion of unification in 221 BCE has been interpreted in various ways by historians. Some have emphasized the transformative character of the event, viewing it as a fundamental turning point in Chinese history that created the framework for two millennia of imperial government. Others have stressed the continuities between the Qin empire and the earlier Zhou system, noting that many Qin administrative practices had precedents in the Warring States period.

## Conclusion

The campaigns against Yan and Qi brought the Warring States era to its conclusion. The conquest of Yan, though it required sustained military effort, eliminated the last major resistance in the northeast. The diplomatic and military pressure on Qi achieved a virtually bloodless surrender, completing the unification of China under Qin rule. The era of division and competition that had characterized Chinese politics for centuries was over, and a new imperial order had begun.

Yet the completion of unification was not the end of the story. The challenges of governing a vast empire would prove to be immense, and the methods that had enabled Qin to conquer would prove inadequate to the task of maintaining control. Within a few short years, the empire would begin to unravel, and the forces of reaction would sweep away the Qin order. But that is a story for another chapter.

For now, it is enough to recognize the magnitude of what had been achieved. In the space of just a few years, Qin had destroyed all of the major Warring States and created the first unified empire in Chinese history. The political map of the Chinese world had been transformed beyond recognition, and a new chapter in the history of civilization had begun.

## The Legacy of the Unification

The unification of China under Qin in 221 BCE represented one of the most significant turning points in world history. The creation of a centralized empire, governed by a uniform system of laws and administration, established a model that would be emulated and adapted by subsequent Chinese dynasties for over two millennia. The Qin unification also had profound implications for the development of Chinese culture, society, and identity, creating the foundation for the emergence of a distinctive Chinese civilization.

However, the unification was also the product of a particular historical moment, shaped by the specific circumstances of the Warring States period and the character of the Qin state. The methods that had enabled Qin to conquer—the ruthless application of military force, the elimination of rival elites, and the imposition of uniform administration—would prove to be both the strengths and the weaknesses of the new empire. Within years of the unification, the empire would begin to fracture, as the forces of resistance and the contradictions of Qin rule led to its collapse. Yet the memory of the unified empire would persist, serving as an inspiration and a model for all subsequent Chinese rulers.

### Chapter Notes

The primary source for the final campaigns is Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji*), which provides the basic narrative of the Yan and Qi conquests. However, the *Shiji* accounts are relatively brief, and many details of the campaigns remain unclear.

The identity of the generals who commanded the campaigns has been the subject of scholarly discussion. Wang Ben's role in the Yan campaign is well-established, but the identity of the commander of the Qi campaign is less certain. Some sources mention a general named Xin (新), but this may be a transcription error or an alternative name for a known commander.

The chronology of the campaigns is generally clear, with the Yan campaign beginning in 226 BCE and the Qi campaign concluding in 221 BCE. However, the precise sequence of events within these years is sometimes uncertain.

The suicide of the Prince of Yan is recorded in the *Shiji*, though the details are limited. Some scholars have questioned whether the prince died by suicide or was captured and executed, but the traditional account has been widely accepted.
