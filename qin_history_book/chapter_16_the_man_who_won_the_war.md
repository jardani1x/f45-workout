# Chapter 16: The Man Who Won the War — General Wang Jian

Among the great generals of ancient China, few achieved the military success and historical prominence of Wang Jian (王翦). As the commander who led Qin to victory in the conquest of Chu, the largest and most formidable of the Warring States, Wang Jian played a decisive role in the unification of China. His career spanned decades of service to the Qin state, and his military achievements represented the culmination of Qin's long preparation for conquest. To understand how Qin achieved unification, one must understand not only the political and economic foundations of Qin's power but also the generals who translated that power into victory on the battlefield. Wang Jian was the greatest of these generals, and his campaigns against Chu and the northern campaigns against the Xiongnu established the patterns of Qin expansion that would shape Chinese history for centuries.

This chapter examines the life and career of Wang Jian, exploring his military strategies, his relationship with Ying Zheng, and his lasting legacy as the architect of Qin's greatest victories. Wang Jian's story is not merely one of military achievement; it is also a window into the nature of power and trust in the Qin empire, where even the most successful generals walked a careful path between victory and danger.

## The Making of a General

Wang Jian's origins are somewhat obscure in the historical record. According to Sima Qian's *Shiji*, he was born in the state of Qin itself, likely in the western region that had been the heartland of the Qin state for centuries. Unlike some of the great generals of the Warring States period, who came from humble backgrounds or from other states, Wang Jian was a native of Qin, and his career was intimately tied to the development of the Qin state (Sima Qian, *Shiji*).

The period of Wang Jian's emergence as a military leader corresponded with the reign of King Zhaoxiang (秦昭襄王) and the subsequent reigns that brought Qin to the brink of unification. During this period, the Qin military was transformed from a feudal levies-based force into a professional army capable of sustained campaigns far from its home territories. Wang Jian came of age within this system, rising through the ranks to become one of its most distinguished officers.

The transformation of the Qin military was closely linked to the Legalist reforms that had reshaped Qin society. Under the guidance of ministers such as Shang Yang and Li Si, Qin had developed a system that rewarded military achievement with land, titles, and official positions. This meritocratic system created powerful incentives for ambitious officers, and Wang Jian proved to be one of its greatest beneficiaries. His rise was not merely a matter of personal talent; it was also a product of the institutional structure that identified and promoted military talent.

## Early Campaigns and the Path to Prominence

Wang Jian's early military career is not well documented in the surviving sources, but it is clear that he had established himself as a significant figure in the Qin military by the middle of the third century BCE. The historical records indicate that he participated in several campaigns during the reign of King Zhaoxiang and King Xiaowen, gaining experience and reputation in the wars against the other Warring States.

One of Wang Jian's earliest documented campaigns was the campaign against Zhao in 270 BCE, during the major Qin-D Zhao war. This conflict, which lasted for several years, saw Qin make significant territorial gains at Zhao's expense. Wang Jian's role in this campaign, while not specified in detail in the sources, would have provided him with valuable experience in large-scale warfare against a formidable opponent.

The campaigns against Han and Wei in the 260s and 250s BCE also featured Wang Jian's service. These campaigns, which resulted in the elimination of Han as a significant power and the weakening of Wei, demonstrated the growing military superiority of Qin over its rivals. Wang Jian's participation in these campaigns established his reputation as one of Qin's most reliable commanders.

By the time of King Ying Zheng's accession to the throne, Wang Jian had become one of the senior generals in the Qin military. His experience, combined with his demonstrated competence, made him a natural choice for the most challenging campaigns that lay ahead. The conquest of Chu would be his greatest test.

## The Conquest of Chu (228-223 BCE)

The campaign against Chu was the largest and most ambitious military operation undertaken by Qin during the unification wars. Chu was not merely the largest of the Warring States in terms of territory; it was also a powerful state with a rich cultural tradition and significant military resources. The conquest of Chu would require not only superior force but also careful planning and strategic sophistication. Wang Jian was equal to the challenge.

The campaign began in 228 BCE, following the completion of the conquest of Zhao. The initial phases of the campaign involved the movement of massive Qin forces to the southern front, where they would confront the Chu army. According to the *Shiji*, Wang Jian led an army of 600,000 men into Chu territory, a number that has been debated by modern scholars but that clearly indicates the scale of the operation (Sima Qian, *Shiji*).

The first major engagement of the campaign was the Battle of Chen (陳), which took place in 224 BCE. The Chu forces, commanded by Xiang Yan (項燕), one of the most capable generals of the period, initially offered determined resistance. However, the Qin forces under Wang Jian were able to defeat the Chu army in a decisive engagement that shattered the main Chu field army. Xiang Yan was killed in the battle, and the Chu forces collapsed in the aftermath.

Following the victory at Chen, Wang Jian's forces advanced rapidly into Chu territory. The Qin army marched on the Chu capital of Ying (郢), which fell in 223 BCE. With the capture of the capital and the destruction of the main Chu armies, the state of Chu ceased to exist as an independent entity. The territories of Chu were incorporated into the Qin state, creating the largest commandery in the new empire.

The speed of the Chu conquest was remarkable. In just a few years, Wang Jian had accomplished what many had thought impossible: the destruction of the state that had long been considered Qin's most dangerous rival. The victory was a testament to both Wang Jian's military skill and the overwhelming power of the Qin military machine.

## Strategic Insight and Adaptability

What made Wang Jian an exceptional general was not merely his ability to win battles but his capacity for strategic thinking and adaptation to different enemies. The conquest of Chu required different methods than the campaigns against the northern states, and Wang Jian demonstrated a keen understanding of how to adjust his approach to the specific circumstances of each campaign.

In the Chu campaign, Wang Jian faced an enemy that fought differently from the Zhao or Wei forces he had encountered in earlier campaigns. Chu's military was characterized by its large numbers and its use of different tactical approaches, including the deployment of war chariots and infantry in combined arms formations. Wang Jian's response was to emphasize the strengths of the Qin infantry, which had been trained and equipped for precisely this kind of engagement.

The *Zhangu Ce* (*Strategies of the Warring States*) preserves some of Wang Jian's strategic thinking, revealing a general who was not merely a battlefield commander but also a sophisticated strategist. Wang Jian understood the importance of logistics, the need to maintain supply lines over long distances, and the value of political intelligence in planning campaigns. His success was not simply a matter of bringing more troops to the field; it was the result of careful preparation and execution.

Wang Jian also demonstrated adaptability in his approach to different types of enemies. When called upon to conduct campaigns in the north against the Xiongnu nomads, he adjusted his methods to account for the mobility and guerrilla tactics of the nomadic forces. This ability to adapt would prove crucial in establishing Qin's dominance over the northern frontier.

## The Northern Campaigns and Pacification

Following the conquest of Chu, Wang Jian was called upon to conduct another critical campaign: the pacification of the northern frontier. The Xiongnu confederation posed a significant threat to the Qin heartland, and the new empire needed to secure its northern borders before it could fully consolidate its gains. Wang Jian was given command of the forces assigned to this task.

The northern campaigns involved campaigns against the Xiongnu and other nomadic peoples in the region of Hetao (河套), the area north of the Ordos Desert. This terrain was vastly different from the plains of central China where Wang Jian had won his earlier victories, and the enemy was unlike any he had faced before. The Xiongnu were highly mobile horse-archers who could strike quickly and withdraw before the slower-moving Qin infantry could engage them.

Wang Jian's response to this challenge was characteristically pragmatic. He organized the Qin forces into mobile units capable of matching the Xiongnu's speed, and he conducted a sustained campaign of attrition against the nomadic forces. The *Shiji* records that Wang Jian's forces achieved significant victories over the Xiongnu, driving them north of the Yellow River and securing the northern frontier (Sima Qian, *Shiji*).

The northern campaigns were not merely military operations; they were also political statements. By demonstrating Qin's ability to project power into the frontier regions, Wang Jian helped establish the legitimacy of the new empire and its claim to dominion over all of China. The conquest of the northern territories expanded the empire's borders and created a buffer zone that would protect the heartland from future incursions.

## Relationship with Ying Zheng

One of the most notable aspects of Wang Jian's career was his relationship with Ying Zheng, the man who would become Qin Shi Huang. This relationship was complex, marked by both trust and the careful management of political risk. In the authoritarian atmosphere of the Qin court, even the most successful generals needed to navigate carefully between loyalty and suspicion.

The historical sources present Wang Jian as a figure who understood the political dimensions of military command. He was careful to maintain a close relationship with Ying Zheng, seeking the king's confidence and support for his campaigns. At the same time, he was aware of the dangers that came with great military success, particularly in a political system where rivals could easily turn a general's victories into accusations of treachery.

A revealing anecdote from the *Shiji* illustrates this dynamic. Before departing for the Chu campaign, Wang Jian reportedly asked Ying Zheng for substantial rewards and honors, including land and wealth, to demonstrate that he was not a threat. This request, which might seem presumptuous, was in fact a carefully calculated political gesture. By showing that he was motivated by material rewards rather than political ambitions, Wang Jian sought to allay any suspicions that he might harbor designs on the throne (Sima Qian, *Shiji*).

This episode has been interpreted by modern scholars as evidence of the precarious position of military commanders in the Qin system. The Legalist philosophy that undergirded Qin governance was deeply suspicious of individual power, and even the most loyal servants could find themselves targets of political purges if they were perceived as threats. Wang Jian's behavior suggests that he understood this reality and navigated it with skill.

## Retirement and Later Recall

Despite his enormous contributions to Qin's success, Wang Jian's career was not without its periods of retirement and recall. In the later years of his life, the general appears to have withdrawn from active service, only to be called back when circumstances required his unique talents.

The reasons for Wang Jian's retirement are not entirely clear from the historical record. Some scholars have suggested that he may have been the victim of political maneuvering at court, where rivals sought to diminish the influence of the aging general. Others have suggested that Wang Jian himself chose to step back, either because of age or because he recognized the changing nature of the empire's needs.

Whatever the cause, Wang Jian's retirement was not permanent. When the northern frontier demanded attention and the Xiongnu threats intensified, Qin Shi Huang turned to the one general he trusted to handle the challenge. Wang Jian was recalled from retirement and given command of the northern campaigns, demonstrating the emperor's continued confidence in his abilities.

This pattern of retirement and recall was not uncommon for successful generals in Chinese history, but it was particularly significant in the Qin context. The emperor's willingness to rely on Wang Jian, even in his advanced years, was a testament to the general's unparalleled reputation and proven capabilities. No other commander had demonstrated the same ability to achieve victory against the most formidable enemies.

## Legacy and Historical Significance

Wang Jian's legacy extends far beyond his individual victories. His career exemplified the transformation of Chinese warfare during the Warring States period, as the feudal levies of earlier centuries gave way to professional armies capable of sustained campaigns. The methods he employed—the emphasis on training, logistics, and strategic planning—became the model for subsequent Chinese military operations.

The conquest of Chu, in particular, had profound implications for the future of China. By eliminating the largest and most culturally distinct of the Warring States, Wang Jian paved the way for the unification of the Chinese heartland. Without his victory, the creation of the Qin empire would have been impossible, or at least would have required many more years of costly warfare.

Wang Jian also established patterns of frontier defense that would persist for centuries. His campaigns against the Xiongnu created a template for how subsequent Chinese dynasties would deal with the nomadic peoples of the north. The Great Wall, which Qin Shi Huang began constructing following Wang Jian's northern campaigns, was a physical manifestation of the defensive strategies that Wang Jian had helped develop.

The historical assessment of Wang Jian has been generally positive across the centuries. Chinese historiography has celebrated him as one of the great generals of unification, alongside figures such as Bai Qi and Lian Po. His reputation as a strategic genius who could adapt to any enemy and any terrain has endured.

Modern scholars have emphasized the institutional context of Wang Jian's achievements. His victories were not merely the product of individual genius; they were the result of the Qin military system that had been developed over generations. Wang Jian was the beneficiary of this system, but he was also its greatest champion, demonstrating its effectiveness on the battlefield.

## Conclusion

Wang Jian stands as one of the pivotal figures in Chinese history. His military achievements—the conquest of Chu, the pacification of the north, and his contributions to the overall unification of China—changed the trajectory of Chinese civilization. In him, the ambitions of the Qin state found their perfect expression: a general of unmatched skill, strategic insight, and political wisdom.

Yet Wang Jian's story is also a reminder of the complexities of power in ancient China. Even the most successful generals operated within a political system that demanded loyalty and punished ambition. Wang Jian navigated these challenges with skill, maintaining the trust of his ruler while achieving victories that seemed impossible. His career offers a window into the dynamics of command and leadership in the age of unification.

The legacy of Wang Jian endures in the military traditions of China and in the collective memory of a nation. He was the man who won the war, and his victories created the foundation upon which the Chinese empire was built. In the story of Qin's rise to dominance, no figure is more central, and no achievement more remarkable.

### Chapter Notes

The primary source for Wang Jian's career is Sima Qian's *Shiji*, which provides the main narrative of his campaigns. However, the *Shiji* account is sometimes brief and lacks detail, particularly for Wang Jian's early career. The *Zhangu Ce* (*Strategies of the Warring States*) provides additional strategic context, though its historicity is sometimes questioned.

The number of troops Wang Jian led against Chu—600,000 men as recorded in the *Shiji*—has been the subject of scholarly debate. Some modern historians argue that these numbers were exaggerated for dramatic effect, while others suggest that they reflect the massive scale of Qin military mobilization. The debate illustrates the challenges of using ancient sources for quantitative analysis.

Wang Jian's political relationship with Ying Zheng has been discussed by scholars such as Mark Edward Lewis, who has analyzed the dynamics of trust and suspicion in the Qin military system. The anecdote about Wang Jian requesting rewards before the Chu campaign is central to this discussion.
