# Chapter 12: The Destruction of Wei (225 BCE)

The conquest of Wei in 225 BCE stands as one of the most dramatic episodes in Qin's campaign of unification. Unlike the relatively straightforward annexation of Han, which had been accomplished through a combination of military pressure and diplomatic maneuvering, the destruction of Wei required a siege of extraordinary scale and an act of environmental warfare that left the Wei capital of Daliang submerged beneath the waters of the Yellow River. The fall of Wei marked a decisive turning point in the unification campaign, eliminating one of Qin's most persistent eastern rivals and demonstrating the ruthlessness with which the Qin state would pursue its imperial ambitions.

## The State of Wei on the Eve of Conquest

Wei had once been one of the most powerful states in the Warring States system. During the height of its strength in the fourth century BCE, Wei had possessed a formidable military and had even posed a serious threat to Qin in the early years of the Warring States period. The state occupied a strategically vital position in the central plains, controlling the fertile regions around the Yellow River and commanding the approaches to the Qin heartland in the Guanzhong basin.

However, by the mid-third century BCE, Wei had experienced a long decline. The state had been weakened by successive defeats at the hands of Qin, Zhao, and Chu, and it had lost substantial territory in these conflicts. The Battle of Maling in 342 BCE, in which the Qi general Tian Ji (田忌) and the strategist Sun Bin (孙膑) had defeated the Wei army through a brilliant tactical deception, had marked a turning point in Wei's fortunes. Subsequent defeats, including the catastrophic losses in the campaigns against Qin, had drained Wei's military strength and depleted its economic resources.

By the time Qin turned its full attention to Wei in the 220s BCE, the state had been reduced to a shadow of its former greatness. Wei still controlled the region around its capital Daliang, and it maintained a degree of military capability, but it was no longer able to mount effective resistance against Qin's organized might. The strategic position of Wei, which had once been an asset, had become a liability, as the state found itself caught between the expanding Qin to the west and the other major states to the east.

## The Campaign of Wang Ben

The campaign against Wei was assigned to Wang Ben (王贲), the son of the veteran Qin general Wang Jian (王翦). Wang Jian had already established himself as Qin's foremost military commander through his campaigns against Chu and other states, and his son had inherited both his tactical skills and his reputation for ruthless efficiency. The choice of Wang Ben for this campaign reflected Qin's confidence in his abilities and the importance that the Qin court attached to the destruction of Wei.

Historical sources provide relatively limited details about the campaign itself, but the basic outline can be reconstructed from the available evidence. According to Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian*, Wang Ben launched the campaign against Wei in 225 BCE, leading Qin forces to attack the Wei capital of Daliang (Sima Qian, *Shiji*). The campaign appears to have begun with a direct assault on the city, which was protected by substantial fortifications and by the natural defenses provided by the Yellow River and its tributaries.

The siege of Daliang proved to be a prolonged and difficult operation. Daliang was one of the largest and most well-fortified cities in the Warring States system, and the Wei defenders put up stubborn resistance. The city was situated on an island formed by the Yellow River and its tributary the Canal (渠), which provided both a natural moat and a means of receiving supplies and reinforcements by water. This waterborne supply route proved to be a significant challenge for the Qin forces, as they were unable to completely cut off the city's access to the outside world.

## The Destruction of the Canal System

As the siege dragged on, Wang Ben recognized that conventional methods would not be sufficient to breach the defenses of Daliang. The city's walls were strong, and the water defenses made it impossible to starve the defenders into surrender through a simple blockade. In response, the Qin general adopted a strategy that would have profound consequences for the region: he ordered the destruction of the canal system that fed the city's defenses.

The canal system around Daliang was a remarkable engineering achievement that had been developed over centuries. The canals, which connected the Yellow River with the various tributaries and waterways of the region, served multiple purposes: they provided irrigation for the fertile farmland of the Wei heartland, they facilitated transportation and trade, and they formed an integral part of the capital's defensive infrastructure. By diverting the flow of water from these canals, Wang Ben sought to not only weaken the city's defenses but also to inflict catastrophic damage on the surrounding agricultural economy.

The destruction of the canal system was a strategic decision that reflected the ruthless calculus of Qin's military planning. Rather than expending additional resources on a prolonged siege, the Qin forces opted to attack the very infrastructure that sustained the Wei state. This approach was consistent with the broader Qin strategy of targeting the economic and social foundations of enemy states, rather than simply defeating their military forces in battle.

## The Flooding of Daliang

The destruction of the canals set the stage for the most dramatic and controversial aspect of the campaign: the deliberate flooding of Daliang. According to the traditional accounts, Wang Ben ordered the diversion of the Yellow River itself, directing its waters against the Wei capital. The resulting flood overwhelmed the city's defenses, submerged the lower portions of the city, and forced the surviving defenders to retreat to higher ground.

The flooding of Daliang was an act of environmental warfare of unprecedented scale in Chinese history. The Yellow River, known for its tendency to change course and cause devastating floods, was harnessed as a weapon of destruction. The waters rushed through the canal system and into the city, destroying buildings, drowning inhabitants, and rendering the capital uninhabitable. The scale of the destruction was catastrophic, and the effects would be felt for generations.

While traditional accounts describe deliberate flooding of Daliang, 2019 Kaifeng archaeological surveys (Henan Provincial Institute) found no sediment evidence of catastrophic Yellow River flooding c. 225 BCE. Modern scholarship suggests targeted canal destruction rather than city-submerging deluge.

Whatever the precise extent of the flooding, it is clear that the waters inflicted devastating damage on the Wei capital. The city that had once been a center of culture and political power was reduced to a ruin, and the surviving population was forced to flee or submit to Qin authority. The destruction of Daliang marked the effective end of Wei as an independent state.

## The Fate of the Wei Royal Family

With the fall of Daliang, the Wei state ceased to exist as an independent political entity. The fate of the Wei royal family varied according to the circumstances of their capture and the policies of the Qin government. Some members of the Wei ruling house were executed, while others were permitted to survive under Qin jurisdiction, though stripped of their former status and power.

According to Sima Qian's account, the Wei king Jia (魏王假) was captured following the fall of Daliang. The traditional accounts suggest that he was executed, though the precise circumstances of his death are unclear. The capture and execution of the Wei king was consistent with Qin's policy of eliminating the ruling houses of conquered states, thereby preventing the possibility of restoration or rebellion.

Other members of the Wei royal family were treated differently. Some were allowed to live, though they were deprived of their titles and estates. The treatment of the Wei royal family reflected the broader Qin approach to the conquered states: the elimination of the old ruling elites and their replacement with Qin administrators. This policy was designed to prevent the continuity of local power structures that might challenge Qin authority.

The fall of Wei also had significant implications for the nobility and elite of the state. Many nobles were executed or exiled, while others were integrated into the Qin system as subordinate officials or as part of the relocated population. The destruction of the Wei aristocracy was part of the broader process of social transformation that accompanied Qin's unification of China.

## The Strategic Significance of Wei's Destruction

The destruction of Wei had important strategic consequences for Qin's ongoing campaign of unification. Wei had long served as a buffer state between Qin and the eastern states, and its elimination opened the way for further expansion into the central plains. With Wei conquered, Qin could now focus its attention on the remaining major states: Chu to the south, Zhao to the north, Yan to the northeast, and Qi to the east. The fall of Wei also marked the completion of Qin's control over the Guanzhong region and the eastern approaches to the central plains, creating a solid territorial base for the final campaigns of unification.

The campaign also demonstrated Qin's willingness to employ extreme measures when necessary. The flooding of Daliang was not merely a tactical success; it was also a statement of intent. The other Warring States now understood that Qin would not hesitate to destroy their cities and displace their populations if they resisted conquest. This demonstration of ruthlessness may have influenced the subsequent behavior of some states, contributing to the relatively quick surrender of Qi in 221 BCE. The memory of Daliang's destruction would haunt the remaining states, who now faced a conqueror willing to employ the most devastating methods available.

At the same time, the destruction of Wei created new challenges for Qin. The flooding of the Yellow River had altered the geography of the region, and the recovery of the affected areas would require considerable time and resources. The canals that had once supported the local economy were destroyed, and the agricultural land that had been irrigated by these canals was now vulnerable to drought or flooding. The environmental consequences of the campaign would linger for decades, creating administrative challenges for the Qin commanderies that would administer former Wei territory. The displacement of populations and the destruction of infrastructure created immediate difficulties in establishing effective control over the conquered region.

## Historical and Scholarly Perspectives

The destruction of Wei has been interpreted in various ways by historians and scholars. Some have seen it as evidence of Qin's brutal and destructive approach to warfare, emphasizing the cruelty of the flooding and the suffering of the civilian population. Others have contextualized the event within the broader patterns of Warring States warfare, noting that the use of water as a weapon was not unique to Qin and that similar tactics had been employed by other states in earlier periods.

The traditional Chinese historiographical perspective, shaped by Sima Qian and later Confucian scholars, tended to emphasize the negative aspects of Qin's conquest. The flooding of Daliang was cited as an example of Qin's ruthlessness and disregard for human life, and it contributed to the negative reputation that Qin Shi Huang and his state acquired in Chinese historical memory. This perspective reflected broader criticisms of Qin's Legalist philosophy and its rejection of Confucian moral values.

Modern scholars have sought to move beyond this moralistic interpretation, analyzing the destruction of Wei within the context of the strategic and political dynamics of the Warring States period. From this perspective, the flooding of Daliang was a rational military decision that served Qin's strategic objectives, even if it entailed significant human and environmental costs. The event has also been studied for what it reveals about the engineering capabilities of the Warring States period and the relationship between warfare and environmental manipulation.

The archaeological evidence for the destruction of Wei remains limited, though ongoing excavations at sites in Henan Province continue to shed light on the material culture of the Wei state and the circumstances of its conquest. The site of ancient Daliang has been identified near modern-day Kaifeng, and archaeological work has revealed evidence of occupation and destruction layers that may correspond to the Qin conquest.

## Conclusion

The destruction of Wei in 225 BCE was a pivotal moment in Qin's campaign of unification. The campaign demonstrated the willingness of the Qin state to employ extreme measures, including environmental warfare, to achieve its objectives. The flooding of Daliang eliminated the Wei state as an independent political entity and opened the way for further expansion into the central plains. The fate of the Wei royal family illustrated the harsh treatment that awaited the rulers of conquered states, reinforcing the message that resistance would be met with total destruction.

Yet the destruction of Wei was also a cautionary tale about the costs of conquest. The flooding of the Yellow River caused immense suffering and inflicted lasting damage on the region's infrastructure and economy. The canals that had sustained Wei's prosperity were destroyed, and the agricultural heartland of the state was rendered barren. These consequences would complicate Qin's administration of the conquered territory and would contribute to the challenges that the empire would face in the years ahead. The environment itself became a casualty of Qin's imperial ambitions, a pattern that would repeat itself in other regions as Qin pursued its relentless drive toward unification.

### Chapter Notes

The primary source for the destruction of Wei is Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji*), which provides the basic narrative of the campaign, the flooding of Daliang, and the fate of the Wei royal family. However, Sima Qian's account is relatively brief, and many details of the campaign remain unclear.

The traditional narrative of the flooding has been the subject of some scholarly debate. While most historians accept that water was used as a weapon against Daliang, the scale and effects of the flooding have been questioned. Some scholars have suggested that the traditional accounts may have exaggerated the destruction for moral and political reasons.

The chronology of the Wei campaign is generally well-established, with most sources placing the conquest in 225 BCE. However, some alternative chronologies have been proposed, and the precise sequence of events remains a matter of some dispute.

The identity of the Wei king at the time of the conquest is also somewhat uncertain. Sima Qian refers to the king as Jia (假), but some scholars have suggested that this name may be a posthumous title rather than the personal name of the ruler.
