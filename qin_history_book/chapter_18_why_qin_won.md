# Chapter 18: Why Qin Won — The Causes of Unification

The unification of China under Qin in 221 BCE was one of the most significant events in ancient world history. In the space of just nine years, the state of Qin destroyed all of its major rivals and created the first unified empire in Chinese history. Yet this outcome, so momentous in its consequences, was not inevitable. The Warring States period had lasted for over two and a half centuries, and many observers in the third century BCE would have found it difficult to predict which state—or whether any state—would ultimately prevail. The question of why Qin succeeded in accomplishing this monumental task has fascinated historians for over two millennia.

This chapter examines the causes of Qin's success from multiple perspectives. It considers the structural advantages that Qin's geographic position and political organization provided, the transformative impact of the Legalist reforms, the economic and military mobilization that enabled sustained warfare, the role of individual leaders in shaping Qin's destiny, the failures of the other states to mount effective resistance, and the element of contingency—the role of chance and circumstance in determining outcomes. By examining these factors, we can develop a nuanced understanding of how the unification of China came about and why it took the specific form that it did.

## Geographic Advantages

One of the most fundamental factors in Qin's success was its geographic position. The Qin state was located in the western region of the Chinese world, centered on the fertile Wei River Valley in modern-day Shaanxi Province. This position provided Qin with a number of strategic advantages that accumulated over time and contributed to its eventual dominance.

The Wei River Valley was naturally defensible, surrounded by mountains to the north, south, and west. The only vulnerable direction was the east, toward the Central Plains, but this natural barrier could be fortified and defended. Throughout the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods, Qin's western position protected it from the major conflicts that consumed the eastern states, allowing it to develop its resources in relative peace while its rivals exhausted themselves in endless warfare.

The geographic position also provided Qin with access to important natural resources. The Qin heartland was fertile and productive, capable of supporting a large population and generating significant agricultural surplus. The mountains to the north provided access to mineral resources, including iron and copper, which were essential for weapons production. The combination of agricultural and mineral wealth gave Qin an economic foundation that its rivals could not match.

Furthermore, Qin's position allowed it to expand into new territories as it grew stronger. The conquest of the Shu region to the south and the acquisition of territories in the west and north increased Qin's resource base and extended its strategic depth. Each expansion made Qin more powerful and its position more secure, creating a virtuous cycle of growth and development.

The geographic advantages were not unique to Qin—other states also possessed favorable positions—but they were particularly significant in the context of the Warring States period. The combination of defensibility, fertility, and access to resources created the foundation upon which Qin built its military and political power.

## The Legalist Reforms

The most distinctive feature of the Qin state was its commitment to Legalist philosophy and the comprehensive reforms that this commitment inspired. The Legalist tradition, developed by thinkers such as Shang Yang, Han Fei, and Li Si, provided both the ideological framework and the practical policies that transformed Qin from a relatively backward western state into the most powerful polity in the Warring States world.

The Legalist reforms began with Shang Yang's major political transformation in the mid-fourth century BCE. Shang Yang, who served as chief minister to Duke Xiao (秦孝公), implemented a series of policies that fundamentally reshaped Qin society. These policies included the abolition of the feudal nobility's privileges, the division of aristocratic estates among individual farmers, the standardization of weights and measures, and the establishment of a merit-based system of government appointments (Bodde, 1967).

The impact of these reforms was profound. By breaking the power of the aristocracy and creating a society based on individual merit rather than hereditary status, Shang Yang created a political system that could mobilize resources far more effectively than the feudal structures that persisted in the other states. The standardizations of law, currency, weights, and measures created a uniform administrative system that facilitated commerce, taxation, and military organization.

The Legalist emphasis on strict enforcement of laws and harsh punishments also played a role in Qin's success. The Qin legal system was notoriously severe, with punishments that extended to entire families for the crimes of individual members. While this harshness has been criticized by later historians and philosophers, it served the practical purpose of maintaining order and discipline within Qin territory. The certainty of punishment created a strong incentive for compliance, and the efficiency of the Qin administration was legendary throughout the Warring States world.

The reforms were continued and expanded by subsequent Qin rulers and ministers, particularly Li Si, who served as chancellor under both King Zhaoxiang and Ying Zheng. Li Si's contributions included the codification of the Legalist legal system, the standardization of the writing system, and the implementation of policies that further centralized administrative control. These refinements built upon Shang Yang's foundation and made the Qin state ever more efficient and powerful.

The Legalist character of the Qin state was both its greatest strength and its most controversial feature. The other states and later dynasties often criticized Qin for its harshness and its rejection of traditional values, but there was no denying the effectiveness of its system. The reforms had created a state that could mobilize its population, allocate its resources, and project its military power with an efficiency that its rivals could not match.

## Economic and Military Mobilization

Qin's success was built on an unprecedented capacity for economic and military mobilization. The combination of Legalist reforms, agricultural development, and technological innovation created an economic base that could sustain the massive military campaigns required for unification.

The agricultural economy of Qin was highly productive. The fertile Wei River Valley, supported by irrigation systems that had been developed over centuries, generated surpluses that could feed large standing armies. The Qin government actively promoted agricultural development, offering rewards to farmers who increased production and penalties for those who neglected their fields. This agricultural policy ensured a steady supply of food for military operations.

The Qin military was a professional force that had been trained and equipped for sustained campaigns. Unlike the feudal levies that characterized most Warring States armies—forces of citizen-soldiers who served for limited periods before returning to their farms—the Qin army was composed of career soldiers who could fight for years without relief. This professionalization gave Qin a decisive advantage in prolonged campaigns.

The organization of the Qin military was also distinctive. The basic unit was the *wu* (伍), a group of five soldiers who were held collectively responsible for each other's conduct. This system created strong incentives for discipline and mutual support, as the failure of one soldier would result in punishment for his entire unit. The military was commanded by a hierarchy of officers who were selected on the basis of merit rather than social status, ensuring that command positions were filled by competent individuals.

Qin also invested heavily in military technology and innovation. The Qin infantry was equipped with standardized weapons produced by state arsenals, ensuring quality control and allowing for mass production. The development of new tactics, including the use of massed infantry formations and coordinated attacks, gave Qin armies a tactical edge on the battlefield. The siege weapons and engineering capabilities that Qin developed allowed it to capture fortified cities that had withstood attacks for centuries.

The economic and military mobilization was not limited to the conduct of wars; it also supported the broader objectives of the Qin state. The taxation system extracted resources from the population efficiently, channeling agricultural surplus and craft production toward military and administrative purposes. The logistics system ensured that armies in the field were supplied with food, weapons, and equipment even in distant campaigns. This comprehensive mobilization gave Qin a decisive advantage in competitions with states that could not match its organizational capacity.

## The Role of Individual Leaders

While structural factors—geography, reforms, and mobilization—provided the foundation for Qin's success, the role of individual leaders was also crucial. The Qin state was fortunate in having a series of capable rulers and ministers who guided its development and made the critical decisions that led to unification.

Among the most important of these leaders was Duke Xiao (秦孝公), who ruled from 361 to 338 BCE and who hired Shang Yang to implement the Legalist reforms. Duke Xiao's willingness to adopt radical new policies set Qin on the path that would eventually lead to unification. Without his support, Shang Yang's reforms would have been impossible, and Qin would have remained a relatively minor state.

King Zhaoxiang (秦昭襄王), who ruled from 306 to 251 BCE, was another pivotal figure. His long reign saw Qin emerge as the dominant power in the Warring States system, defeating major rivals such as Qi and Zhao and significantly expanding Qin's territory. King Zhaoxiang's campaigns laid the groundwork for the final unification, weakening the other states while strengthening Qin.

However, the most consequential of Qin's leaders was undoubtedly Ying Zheng, who would become Qin Shi Huang after the unification. As king of Qin from 247 to 221 BCE and as emperor from 221 to 210 BCE, Ying Zheng was the architect of the unification and the founder of the imperial system. His vision, determination, and ruthlessness were essential to Qin's success.

Ying Zheng's leadership had several distinctive features. First, he was willing to make difficult decisions and to persist in pursuing his goals even when setbacks occurred. The campaigns of unification were not always successful, and there were moments when the outcome was uncertain, but Ying Zheng maintained his commitment to the objective of unification.

Second, Ying Zheng was skilled at selecting and managing subordinates. He surrounded himself with capable ministers such as Li Si and Li Xiang and gave commanding responsibilities to talented generals such as Wang Jian and Bai Qi. His ability to delegate authority while maintaining control was a key factor in Qin's success.

Third, Ying Zheng was willing to employ ruthless tactics when necessary. The unification campaigns were marked by brutal treatment of defeated enemies, including the execution of captured rulers and the destruction of entire noble families. While this ruthlessness has been criticized, it served the practical purpose of eliminating resistance and ensuring permanent incorporation of conquered territories.

The role of individual leaders should not be overstated; the structural advantages of Qin would have produced success even with less capable rulers. However, the specific decisions and actions of key individuals shaped the timing and character of the unification in ways that might have been different under different leadership.

## The Failures of the Other States

The explanation for Qin's success is not complete without considering the failures of the other states. The Warring States period saw the emergence of several powerful states that might have challenged Qin for dominance, yet none was able to do so. The weaknesses of Qin's rivals were as important to the outcome as Qin's own strengths.

One of the most significant failures was the inability of the states to form effective coalitions. Throughout the Warring States period, alliances of multiple states occasionally formed to counterbalance Qin, but these coalitions were always fragile and short-lived. The states were unable to overcome their mutual suspicions and historic enmities, and they consistently failed to coordinate their military operations effectively. Each state hoped that Qin would attack a neighbor rather than itself, and this individualistic approach allowed Qin to defeat them one by one.

The internal politics of the other states also contributed to their weakness. Many of the Warring States were plagued by factional disputes, court intrigues, and succession crises that distracted attention from the external threat. The states lacked the political coherence to mount sustained resistance, and their governments were often paralyzed by internal divisions.

The military weaknesses of the other states were also significant. Most of the states had not undergone the comprehensive reforms that had transformed Qin, and their military systems remained based on outdated feudal principles. Their armies were less professional, less well-equipped, and less well-organized than the Qin forces. Even when the states possessed numerical superiority, as they sometimes did, they were unable to translate this advantage into victories.

Furthermore, the other states failed to adapt to the changing strategic environment. While Qin continuously refined its military tactics and administrative methods, the other states remained stagnant. They continued to rely on strategies and approaches that had been effective in earlier periods but that were inadequate against the sophisticated Qin war machine.

The failure of the other states was not inevitable; it was the product of specific choices and circumstances. Some states, such as Qi and Chu, had opportunities to reform their systems and strengthen their positions, but they failed to do so. The combination of internal weakness and strategic stagnation created an opening that Qin was able to exploit.

## The Element of Contingency

While structural factors—geographic advantages, Legalist reforms, economic mobilization, and individual leadership—provide the main explanation for Qin's success, the element of contingency also played a role. The unification of China was not simply the inevitable result of long-term trends; it was also shaped by specific events and circumstances that could have been different.

One important contingent factor was the timing of the conquests. The states were at particular points in their development when Qin launched its campaigns, and the timing was favorable for unification. Chu had not yet recovered from earlier defeats, Zhao was weakened by natural disasters and internal conflicts, and Qi had lost the will to resist. If the conquests had been delayed by a generation, the outcome might have been different.

The deaths of key individuals also affected the course of events. The death of the Chu general Xiang Yan in the Battle of Chen, for example, deprived Chu of its ablest commander and contributed to the rapid collapse of Chu resistance. Similarly, the death or removal of key ministers and generals in other states affected their ability to respond to the Qin threat.

The outcome of specific battles could have gone differently, with consequences that might have altered the entire trajectory of the conquest. While Qin generally performed well in battle, there were moments when the outcome was uncertain. A major Qin defeat in any of the major campaigns might have delayed or even prevented unification.

The element of luck was also present. The discovery of new resources, the development of new technologies, and the occurrence of natural events all influenced the course of events in ways that could not have been predicted or controlled. The combination of these contingent factors with the structural advantages of Qin produced a particular historical outcome that was neither inevitable nor accidental.

## The Synthesis of Factors

The causes of Qin's success were multiple and interrelated. Geographic advantages provided the foundation, Legalist reforms created the political and social structure, economic and military mobilization supplied the resources, individual leadership provided the direction, the failures of the other states created the opportunity, and contingent factors shaped the specific timing and character of the outcome.

No single factor was sufficient to explain Qin's success; it was the combination of these factors that produced the unification. Geographic advantages alone would not have been enough without the reforms that allowed Qin to exploit them. Reforms alone would not have succeeded without leaders who implemented them effectively. And all of these factors would have been insufficient if the other states had been capable of mounting effective resistance.

The interaction of these factors created a historical process that was both complex and contingent. The outcome—the unification of China under Qin—was the result of choices made by individuals, structures that had developed over centuries, and events that could not have been predicted. Understanding this complexity is essential to understanding not only how the unification happened but also why it took the specific form that it did.

## Implications and Legacy

The unification of China under Qin in 221 BCE created a political entity that was unprecedented in Chinese history. The Qin empire was the first unified state to govern the entire Chinese heartland, and its creation marked the end of the Warring States period and the beginning of the imperial era that would last for over two millennia.

The causes of Qin's success have implications beyond the specific historical context of the third century BCE. The combination of geographic advantages, institutional reforms, economic mobilization, leadership, and the exploitation of enemy weaknesses that characterized Qin's rise has parallels in other historical contexts. The study of Qin's success thus offers insights into the dynamics of state formation and military competition that are relevant beyond the study of ancient China.

However, the causes of success are not necessarily the causes of longevity. The Qin empire, despite its military victories, collapsed within years of its founder's death. The very factors that had enabled Qin to conquer—the harsh Legalist system, the ruthless treatment of conquered peoples, the massive demands on the population—also contributed to its instability. The lesson of Qin's rise and fall is that the conditions that enable conquest are not always the conditions that sustain empire.

The legacy of Qin's unification, however, endured long after the empire itself had fallen. The imperial system that Qin Shi Huang created became the model for all subsequent Chinese dynasties, and the idea of a unified Chinese empire became a fundamental feature of Chinese political culture. The causes of Qin's success thus shaped not only the third century BCE but the entire subsequent history of Chinese civilization.

## Conclusion

The unification of China under Qin was one of the most consequential events in world history. Understanding why Qin succeeded in accomplishing this task requires examining multiple factors: the geographic advantages of the Qin heartland, the transformative impact of the Legalist reforms, the unprecedented capacity for economic and military mobilization, the role of individual leaders in shaping Qin's destiny, the failures of the other states to mount effective resistance, and the element of contingency that shaped the specific course of events.

These factors operated together in complex ways, creating a historical process that was neither inevitable nor accidental. The structural advantages of Qin provided the foundation, but the specific outcomes depended on choices made by individuals and events that could not have been predicted. The result was the creation of the first unified empire in Chinese history, an event that transformed the political, social, and cultural landscape of East Asia.

The causes of Qin's success continue to be debated by historians, and the debate is unlikely to be resolved definitively. What is clear is that the unification was the product of a particular historical moment, shaped by the specific circumstances of the Warring States period and the distinctive character of the Qin state. By understanding these causes, we gain insight not only into the unification of China but also into the broader dynamics of historical change.

### Chapter Notes

The analysis presented in this chapter draws on a long tradition of scholarly debate about the causes of Qin's success. Traditional Chinese historians, writing from the perspective of later Confucian orthodoxy, often emphasized the moral failings of the other states and the divine mandate that they believed had guided Qin to victory. Modern scholars have adopted more analytical approaches, focusing on the structural and institutional factors that enabled Qin's rise.

The debate about contingency versus structural determinism is particularly relevant to the study of Qin's unification. Some scholars, following the approach of Max Weber, have emphasized the rational-legal character of the Qin state as the key to its success, while others have highlighted the role of individual leadership and contingent factors.

The assessment of Qin Shi Huang's leadership has also been subject to varying interpretations. Traditional Chinese historiography often portrayed him as a tyrant whose success was made possible by the efforts of worthy ministers such as Li Si. Modern scholars have emphasized his personal contributions to the unification, while also recognizing the structural advantages that supported his efforts.
