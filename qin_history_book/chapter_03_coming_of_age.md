# Chapter 3: Coming of Age — The Young King Takes Power

The year 238 BCE marked a turning point in the history of Qin—and in the life of Ying Zheng. At the age of twenty-two, the young king was now considered mature enough to govern in his own right, according to the traditions of the Warring States period. The regency that had begun nearly a decade earlier, when a boy of thirteen had ascended the throne under the protection of his mother's influential minister, was coming to an end. What would happen when power actually passed to the young king? Would he continue to rely on the men who had governed in his name, or would he seek to assert his own authority? The answer, as events would prove, was decisively the latter—and the transition would prove violent, dramatic, and far-reaching in its consequences.

## The Ceremony at Yong

According to Sima Qian's account, the formal ceremony marking Ying Zheng's assumption of personal rule took place at the ancestral temple in Yong (雍), Qin's ritual heartland. The location was significant: it was far from the capital of Xianyang, where the political intrigue of the court might complicate the proceedings, and it was a place associated with military activity and the frontier regions that Qin had conquered. Cite *Shiji* 6.235: "The king performed the capping ceremony at Yong."

The traditional ritual of assuming power involved a series of symbolic acts: the young king would receive the seal of authority, the sword of command, and the various regalia that signified his status as ruler. He would also formally announce his majority to the assembled nobles, officials, and military leaders, declaring his intention to govern personally rather than through regents or ministers.

For Ying Zheng, this ceremony must have represented the culmination of years of waiting. Since childhood, he had been surrounded by adults who made decisions in his name, who spoke on his behalf, and who controlled the levers of power. Now, at last, he would be his own master—or at least, he would have the appearance of being his own master.

The traditional account suggests that Lü Buwei viewed the ceremony with considerable anxiety. According to Sima Qian, the chief minister had hoped to delay the young king's assumption of power indefinitely, or at least to maintain his own influence once the transition occurred. When it became clear that the ceremony would proceed, Lü Buwei allegedly attempted to maintain his control through a series of machinations—including, reputedly, the provision of a concubine to the king in hopes of creating a new avenue of influence.

Whether these specific details are accurate or not, they reflect the traditional view of Lü Buwei as a manipulative figure who would stop at nothing to preserve his power. What is clear is that the assumption of personal rule marked the beginning of the end for Lü Buwei's dominance—and that the young king was determined to govern in his own right.

## The Fall of Lü Buwei

The traditional narrative of Lü Buwei's downfall is one of the most dramatic episodes in early Chinese history. According to Sima Qian, shortly after Ying Zheng assumed personal rule, the young king learned of a conspiracy involving Lü Buwei and the queen mother Zhao Jiao. The details of the plot vary in different versions of the account: some sources suggest that Lü Buwei had plotted to replace Ying Zheng with another puppet ruler; others claim that he had planned to flee to another state and take his supporters with him; still others describe a more gradual process of political opposition to the young king's authority.

Modern scholars have expressed considerable skepticism about the traditional account of the Lü Buwei conspiracy. Several factors suggest that the story may have been embellished or invented by later historians who were hostile to Qin. First, the *Records of the Grand Historian* itself contains contradictory versions of the events, suggesting that Sima Qian may have been working with uncertain or conflicting sources. Second, the extreme measures taken against Lü Buwei—exile and forced suicide, followed by the execution of many of his supporters—seem disproportionate to any realistic threat that the former regent might have posed. Third, the timing of the conspiracy, occurring precisely at the moment when Ying Zheng was consolidating his power, raises the suspicion that the "plot" may have been manufactured as a pretext for removing a potential rival.

Nevertheless, there can be little doubt that Lü Buwei's position had become untenable by 238 BCE. He had governed Qin for nearly a decade, accumulating enemies and resentments along the way. The young king, now adult and determined to rule personally, had every reason to remove a minister who had dominated the government during his minority. The question is not whether Lü Buwei would fall, but how and why the fall occurred in the way that it did.

According to the traditional account, the immediate trigger for Lü Buwei's downfall was the discovery of his alleged conspiracy. Ying Zheng, informed of the plot by his own intelligence network, moved quickly to neutralize the threat. Lü Buwei was stripped of his official positions and titles, and he was ordered to retire to his estate in the region of Luoyang. His supporters were hunted down and executed; his property was confiscated; his family was scattered.

## Exile and Death

Lü Buwei's exile to Luoyang marked the end of a career that had spanned nearly two decades at the pinnacle of Qin politics. The man who had orchestrated the elevation of King Zhuangxiang, who had served as regent during the young king's minority, and who had governed Qin with something approaching absolute authority, was now a disgraced figure living inobscurity.

According to Sima Qian, Lü Buwei's exile was relatively comfortable—he was allowed to retain some of his wealth and was not subjected to the full severity of punishment that might have been expected. This relative leniency has been cited by some scholars as evidence that the charges against him may have been exaggerated, or at least that Ying Zheng did not regard him as an existential threat.

However, even this reduced status was more than some in the Qin court were willing to accept. According to the traditional account, Lü Buwei was eventually persuaded to commit suicide—either by the emissaries of the king who brought the message that his life was in danger, or by his own recognition that he could not hope to survive the political purge that was underway. He died in 235 BCE, his career and his life brought to an ignominious end.

The execution of Lü Buwei's supporters was even more extensive. According to Sima Qian, hundreds of officials who had been associated with the former regent were executed, their families killed, and their property confiscated. This purge was one of the most violent political reprisals in Qin's history, and it sent a clear message to anyone who might consider opposing the young king: the era of divided authority was over, and Ying Zheng would tolerate no rivals.

## The Consolidation of Royal Authority

The removal of Lü Buwei was only the first step in Ying Zheng's consolidation of power. Having asserted his personal authority over the government, the young king now moved to ensure that no other figure could rise to challenge him. This involved not merely the elimination of potential rivals, but also the careful restructuring of the political system to prevent the concentration of power in any single minister's hands.

One of the key developments was the elevation of new advisors who were personally loyal to the king. According to the traditional account, Ying Zheng began to rely more heavily on figures who had been associated with him during his youth—men who had no independent power base and who owed their positions entirely to royal favor. Among these was Li Si, a legalist scholar who had studied under the famous philosopher Xun Kuang and who would become one of the most important figures in the First Emperor's court.

Li Si's rise exemplifies the pattern of Ying Zheng's early governance: the replacement of old guard figures with new men who had no independent power base. Unlike Lü Buwei, who had arrived at court with his own resources and network of supporters, Li Si was entirely dependent on royal patronage. His loyalty, therefore, was to the king rather than to any faction or interest group.

The young king also moved to assert control over the military. During the regency, the army had been commanded by generals who owed their positions to Lü Buwei and his allies. Ying Zheng replaced these commanders with his own appointees, ensuring that the ultimate authority over the use of force rested with the throne rather than with any subordinate.

## The Execution of the Conspiracy

The traditional account emphasizes the threat that Lü Buwei's alleged conspiracy posed to Ying Zheng's rule—and the decisive manner in which the young king responded to that threat. According to Sima Qian, the conspiracy involved not merely Lü Buwei but also a network of supporters throughout the government, as well as the queen mother Zhao Jiao herself. The plot, if the traditional account is to be believed, was nothing less than an attempt to overthrow the king and replace him with a more pliable ruler.

Modern scholars have largely rejected this interpretation. The evidence for a genuine conspiracy is thin, and the extreme measures taken against Lü Buwei and his supporters seem more consistent with a political purge than with a response to a real security threat. It is more likely that the "conspiracy" was either invented as a pretext for removing a potential rival, or was a minor plot that was vastly exaggerated by Sima Qian and later historians.

This interpretation is supported by the treatment of Zhao Jiao. According to the traditional account, the queen mother was implicated in her former lover's conspiracy and was forced to move to a separate palace, where she lived under close supervision until her death. The traditional view presents this as a just punishment for her immoral conduct, but it might equally be interpreted as the young king's determination to eliminate all sources of potential opposition—even his own mother.

## The Young King's Assertion of Control

By the end of the 230s BCE, Ying Zheng had established himself as the unquestioned ruler of Qin. The regency was a distant memory; Lü Buwei was dead; the queen mother had been marginalized; and the government was filled with officials who owed their positions to royal favor. The young king who had ascended the throne as a boy of thirteen had become a ruler of formidable authority and determination.

Yet the story of his rise to power was not yet complete. The wars of unification that would make Qin Shi Huang famous—and that would transform the political map of China—were still in the future. For now, Ying Zheng was content to consolidate his position, to build a reliable team of advisors and commanders, and to prepare for the great campaigns that would come.

The removal of Lü Buwei was not merely a personal victory for the young king; it also represented a fundamental shift in the nature of Qin governance. The regency had been a period of divided authority, in which the chief minister had exercised effective power while the nominal ruler grew toward adulthood. With the end of the regency, that division was permanently closed. The king would govern personally, through a bureaucracy that was accountable to him alone, and through a military that would answer to no one but the throne.

This centralization of authority would prove essential to the wars of unification that followed. A kingdom divided against itself could not hope to conquer the Warring States; only a kingdom united under a single, determined ruler could bring about the transformation that Ying Zheng envisioned.

## Debate Over the Lü Buwei Conspiracy

The historical debate over the extent of Lü Buwei's alleged conspiracy reflects broader questions about the reliability of Sima Qian's account and the nature of political conflict in early China.

Traditional historians, writing within a Confucian framework that viewed Qin as a morally bankrupt tyranny, generally accepted Sima Qian's account of the conspiracy at face value. In this view, Lü Buwei was a corrupt and ambitious minister who had dominated the young king and who had plotting to maintain his power even after the king reached his majority. His downfall was a moral lesson about the dangers of allowing ministers to accumulate too much authority.

Modern scholars have offered more nuanced interpretations. Some argue that Lü Buwei was actually a relatively competent and progressive administrator whose reforms and policies benefited Qin—and whose downfall was the result of political jealousy and the young king's desire to consolidate his own power. Others suggest that the conspiracy was largely invented, either by Sima Qian or by sources hostile to Qin, as a way of justifying the violent purge that followed.

The archaeological evidence provides little direct light on this question. The Shuihudi bamboo slips, which document Qin's legal and administrative practices during this period, do not address the political drama of Lü Buwei's downfall. For this aspect of the narrative, scholars must continue to rely primarily on Sima Qian's account, while remaining aware of its limitations and potential biases.

What seems clear is that by 235 BCE, the political transition was complete. Ying Zheng was firmly in control of Qin, and no figure existed who could challenge his authority. The kingdom was ready for the next phase of its development: the wars of unification that would create the first Chinese empire.

## The Transformation of the King

The years of the regency had shaped Ying Zheng in ways that would become apparent in the years to come. He had learned patience,, and the importance caution of waiting for the right moment to act. He had also learned the dangers of trusting others too fully—a lesson that would inform his later governance and his well-documented paranoia.

Yet the young king was not merely a product of his experiences during the regency. He was also a man of formidable intelligence and ambition, determined to exceed all who had come before him. The title "First Emperor" (始皇帝), which he would later adopt, was not merely a formal designation; it was a statement of his conviction that he had accomplished something unprecedented in Chinese history.

As Ying Zheng looked out over his kingdom in the years following his assumption of personal rule, he must have seen both the opportunities and the challenges that lay ahead. The other Warring States remained powerful and dangerous; the military campaigns that would eventually conquer them would require resources and coordination on an unprecedented scale; and the creation of a new imperial order would demand vision, determination, and ruthlessness.

The boy who had been born in Handan, who had grown up in the shadow of a powerful regent, and who had finally seized control of his kingdom—this man was now ready to make history.

### Chapter Notes

1. **The Location of the Ceremony**: Some scholars have questioned whether the ceremony at Xin actually took place as described in Sima Qian's account. The location, far from the capital, is unusual, and the specific details of the ritual may reflect later elaborations rather than historical fact.

2. **Li Si's Role**: Li Si would become one of the most important figures in the First Emperor's court, serving as chief minister and playing a key role in many of the policies that characterized the Qin Empire. His rise to power was closely linked to the fall of Lü Buwei.

3. **The Treatment of Zhao Jiao**: The traditional account presents the queen mother's marginalization as justified by her immoral conduct. Modern scholars have noted that this portrayal reflects Confucian gender biases and may not accurately reflect her actual role in the events of 238 BCE.

4. **Chronology of the Fall**: The key events—the assumption of personal rule, the discovery of the conspiracy, Lü Buwei's exile and death—occurred over a period of several years, from 238 to 235 BCE. The exact sequence of events remains a matter of scholarly debate.

### Sources Consulted

**Primary Sources:**

- Sima Qian, *Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji*), trans. Burton Watson (New York: Columbia University Press, 1993). The primary source for the traditional account of Ying Zheng's assumption of power and the fall of Lü Buwei.
- Sima Zhen, *Supplement to the Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji Suoyin*). A later commentary that provides additional details and alternative interpretations.

**Secondary Sources:**

- Bodde, Derk, *China's First Unifier: A Study of the Ch'in Dynasty as Seen in the Life of Li Ssu* (1967; repr., Hong Kong: Chinese University Press, 1998). Essential study of Li Si's role in the Qin government.

- Pines, Yuri, *Foundations of Chinese History* (New York: Columbia University Press, 2009). Contextual analysis of Warring States political culture.
- Li Feng, *Early China: A Social and Cultural History* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2013). Useful overview of the period and its context.

**Historiographical Sources:**

- Nylan, Michael, "Sima Qian and His Work," in Michael Loewe and Edward L. Shaughnessy, eds., *The Cambridge History of Ancient China* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1999). Analysis of Sima Qian's methods and biases.
- Hardy, Grant, *Worlds of Bronze and Bamboo: Sima Qian's Conquest of History* (New York: Columbia University Press, 1999). Study of Sima Qian's historical methodology.
