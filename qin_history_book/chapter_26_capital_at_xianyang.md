# Chapter 26: The Capital at Xianyang

The imperial capital at Xianyang was the physical manifestation of Qin Shi Huang's ambition to create an empire that would endure forever. Located on the banks of the Wei River in present-day Shaanxi province, Xianyang had been the capital of the Qin state for centuries, but under the first emperor it was transformed into something unprecedented: a vast urban complex that dwarfed any city previously built in China. The construction of the new capital represented one of the most ambitious building programs in ancient history, employing hundreds of thousands of laborers and consuming resources on an almost incomprehensible scale. Yet the city that the Qin built would not survive its founders; within years of the dynasty's collapse, Xianyang would be destroyed by fire, leaving only ruins and memories.

## The Choice of Xianyang

Xianyang had served as the capital of the Qin state since the fourth century BCE, when the Qin rulers had moved their court from the western borders of their territory to the more fertile and strategically advantageous Guanzhong region. The location offered several advantages: the fertile soils of the Wei River valley provided agricultural abundance; the surrounding mountains offered natural defenses; and the central position within the Qin territory allowed for efficient administration and communication.

When Qin Shi Huang unified China in 221 BCE, he retained Xianyang as his capital rather than establishing a new city. This decision reflected both practical considerations and ideological symbolism. The existing infrastructure—the palace, the administrative buildings, the surrounding settlements—provided a foundation for expansion without the need to start from scratch. More importantly, Xianyang was already associated with the Qin dynasty and its achievements; to abandon it would have been to abandon the legacy of the Qin state that the emperor claimed to represent.

Yet the existing capital was inadequate for the needs of an empire. The palace that had served the kings of Qin was insufficient for an emperor who ruled over all of China. The city that had grown up around it was too small to accommodate the influx of officials, merchants, and laborers that the new empire required. Within months of the unification, Qin Shi Huang ordered the expansion of Xianyang into a capital worthy of the empire it was meant to embody.

The expansion of Xianyang was not merely a matter of increasing the size of the city; it was an attempt to create a new urban form that would express the ideology of the unified empire. The layout of the city was designed to reflect the cosmic order, with the emperor at the center and the territories of the empire arranged around him. The construction was guided by principles derived from earlier Chinese urban planning, but the scale was entirely new, representing a quantum leap in the ambition and capability of Chinese civilization.

## The Construction Program

The expansion of Xianyang began almost immediately after the unification and continued throughout Qin Shi Huang's reign. The construction program was enormous in scope, employing what historical sources describe as hundreds of thousands of laborers. These laborers came from across the empire—from the conquered states of the east, from the populations of the frontier regions, and from the池 of criminals and prisoners who had been sentenced to forced labor. The scale of the construction was comparable to the building of the Great Wall, and it drew on the same pool of labor and resources.

The most famous component of the construction was the Epang Palace (阿房宫), which was intended to serve as the emperor's new residence and the ceremonial center of the empire. According to historical accounts, the palace was designed on a monumental scale, with vast halls capable of accommodating thousands of people, elaborate decorations, and extensive gardens. The name "Epang" means "front of the palace," suggesting that the structure was intended as the first of several buildings in a larger complex.

The construction of Epang Palace was controversial from the beginning. According to Sima Qian's account, a court official named Li He was responsible for the initial design, which called for a structure of unprecedented size. When the design was presented to the emperor, Li Si and other ministers objected, arguing that such a building would be wasteful and could not be completed in a reasonable time. Qin Shi Huang, however, overruled their objections and ordered the construction to proceed.

The construction of Epang Palace continued for years, employing an enormous workforce. According to traditional accounts, the palace was never completed; construction was interrupted by the emperor's death in 210 BCE and was never resumed. Later sources describe the palace as covering an area of several hundred hectares, with buildings arranged in a complex pattern of halls, corridors, and courtyards. The scale of the complex was such that it was said to be visible from a great distance, its rooftops stretching to the horizon.

Archaeological excavations at the site of Epang Palace have confirmed some aspects of the traditional accounts while raising questions about others. The remains of the palace complex have been identified near present-day Xi'an, on the southern bank of the Wei River. Excavations have revealed the foundations of large buildings, evidence of elaborate decoration, and the remains of what appears to have been a massive artificial lake or reservoir. The site covers an area of approximately 15 square kilometers, confirming the traditional description of a vast complex. However, the extent of completion remains unclear; some scholars argue that the palace was never fully constructed, while others suggest that construction was more advanced than traditional accounts indicate.

## The Layout of the Capital

The expanded capital of Xianyang was organized according to principles that reflected both traditional Chinese urban planning and the ideological needs of the new empire. The city was divided into several distinct zones, each serving different functions. At the center was the palace complex, where the emperor lived and conducted government business. Surrounding the palace were the administrative districts, where the various ministries and bureaus of the imperial government were housed. Beyond these central areas were the residential districts, where officials, merchants, and ordinary citizens lived.

The layout of Xianyang was designed to express the cosmic order that the emperor was believed to embody. The palace was positioned at the center of the city, reflecting the emperor's position at the center of the universe. The streets were laid out in a grid pattern,象征 the order and rationality that the Qin sought to impose on their empire. The walls that surrounded the city were massive, symbolizing the boundaries between order and chaos, between the civilized empire and the wild frontiers beyond.

The construction techniques employed in the building of Xianyang were advanced for their time. The palaces and public buildings were constructed using timber frames, with walls of rammed earth or fired brick. The foundations were often raised on platforms of compacted earth, creating the elevated bases that characterized Qin architecture. The roofs were tiled, with decorative elements that indicated the status of the building and its occupants. The overall effect was one of monumental solidity, conveying the permanence and power of the empire.

The city was also equipped with the infrastructure necessary to support a large population. Reservoirs and canals provided water for drinking and irrigation. Granaries stored grain to feed the population in times of shortage. Workshops produced the goods needed by the court and the administration. The city was, in effect, a self-contained organism, designed to function independently of the surrounding countryside.

The population of Xianyang during the Qin period is difficult to estimate with precision. Traditional sources describe the city as containing hundreds of thousands of people, including officials, soldiers, artisans, merchants, and slaves. Some estimates suggest a population of perhaps 100,000 to 200,000, though these figures are speculative. In either case, Xianyang was far larger than any previous Chinese city and would not be exceeded in size until the Han dynasty established its capital at Chang'an.

## Palace Architecture

The palaces of Xianyang were designed to convey the overwhelming power and authority of the emperor. The structures were massive, with high foundations, wide halls, and elaborate decorations that created an impression ofawesome grandeur. Every detail was calculated to remind visitors of the emperor's supremacy and to intimidate potential challengers.

The main halls of the palace complex were built on a scale that dwarfed anything previously constructed in China. The great hall of Epang Palace, according to descriptions in historical sources, could hold 10,000 people comfortably. The roof was supported by massive pillars, each requiring several men to encircle with their arms. The floors were paved with polished bronze or stone, reflecting the light and creating an impression of almost blinding brilliance. The walls were decorated with scenes of mythical creatures, historical events, and cosmological symbols, all rendered in vivid colors.

The residential palaces where the emperor lived were somewhat smaller but no less elaborate. These structures were designed for comfort as well as display, with heated floors, private gardens, and chambers for the emperor's numerous concubines and attendants. The decoration was if anything more refined than that of the public halls, with carved screens, jade ornaments, and silk hangings creating an atmosphere of luxurious intimacy.

The palace complex also included numerous auxiliary buildings: kitchens, storehouses, armories, workshops, and quarters for the thousands of servants and guards who attended to the emperor's needs. These structures were more utilitarian in design but no less solidly constructed, reflecting the Qin emphasis on practical efficiency. The overall effect was a city within a city, a self-contained complex that met every need without requiring the emperor to venture into the public areas of the capital.

The construction of the palaces employed the most skilled artisans in the empire, many of whom had been brought to Xianyang from the conquered states. These craftsmen brought with them the artistic traditions of their home states, creating a synthesis of styles that was distinctively imperial. The result was a new architectural vocabulary that would influence Chinese palace design for centuries to come, establishing patterns that would be repeated in the capitals of later dynasties.

## The City as Imperial Symbol

Xianyang was not merely a functional capital; it was an ideological statement, a physical manifestation of the empire's power and the emperor's ambitions. Every aspect of the city was designed to convey a specific message: that the Qin empire was a new beginning in Chinese history, a unification of all under heaven that would endure forever.

The scale of the city was itself a message. By building on a scale that exceeded all previous achievements, Qin Shi Huang demonstrated that his empire was without precedent. The massive walls, the towering palaces, the vast granaries—all declared that this was an empire of unprecedented resources and capabilities. The message was directed both at the Chinese population, who were meant to be awed into submission, and at the outside world, which was meant to recognize the overwhelming power of the emperor.

The design of the city also expressed the ideological claims of the Qin regime. The grid-pattern streets represented the rational order that the Qin sought to impose on their empire. The cosmic arrangement of the palace at the center reflected the emperor's role as the pivot between heaven and earth. The standardized architecture and decoration throughout the city embodied the Qin commitment to uniformity and centralization. Xianyang was, in effect, a three-dimensional manifestation of the Legalist philosophy that guided the empire.

The construction of Xianyang also served practical purposes beyond symbolism. The city was the administrative center of the empire, housing the bureaucracy that governed the vast territories. The concentration of government functions in a single location allowed for efficient decision-making and communication. The presence of the emperor in the capital maintained the chain of command that was essential for controlling such a large and diverse empire. The city's location at the center of the empire, with access to both the northern frontiers and the eastern coastal regions, made it strategically positioned for both defense and administration.

Yet the construction also consumed enormous resources and contributed to the strains that would eventually destroy the empire. The labor required for the building program came from across the empire, draining agricultural productivity and creating resentment among the peasant population. The materials—timber, stone, bronze—had to be extracted from distant regions and transported to the capital, requiring the construction of roads and canals that themselves demanded immense labor. The cost of the construction was one factor that contributed to the economic difficulties that plagued the later years of the dynasty.

## The Destruction of Xianyang

The magnificent capital that Qin Shi Huang built would not survive its founder. Within four years of the emperor's death in 210 BCE, Xianyang would be destroyed in a fire that would become one of the most famous events in Chinese history. The destruction was carried out by Xiang Yu, the项王 (King of Xiang), who led the Chu forces that defeated the Qin in the final days of the dynasty.

The fall of Qin came after a series of rebellions that had shattered the empire's control over its territories. In 209 BCE, a rebellion led by Chen Sheng and Wu Guang sparked widespread uprising across the empire. Although this initial rebellion was suppressed, it inspired numerous other uprisings that the weakened Qin government could not contain. Among the rebel leaders was Xiang Yu, a young noble from the state of Chu who had gathered a powerful army and march west toward the Qin heartland.

In 207 BCE, Xiang Yu's forces reached Xianyang. According to historical accounts, the young king entered the capital and was overwhelmed by its magnificence. His advisor, Fan Zeng, urged him to burn the city to the ground, arguing that such a magnificent capital would inspire future generations to seek to reclaim it and would prevent any future ruler from using it as a base. Xiang Yu agreed, and the order was given to set fire to the palaces and public buildings of Xianyang.

The fire burned for what was said to be three months, consuming the palace complex and much of the city. The famous Epang Palace, which had never been fully completed, was among the structures that went up in flames. The grand halls, the elaborate decorations, the treasures accumulated over years—all were destroyed. The massive pillars and walls that remained standing would later be dismantled for their materials, leaving only foundations and废墟.

The destruction of Xianyang was more than an act of vengeance; it was a deliberate policy intended to erase the Qin from memory. By destroying the capital, Xiang Yu sought to eliminate the physical symbol of Qin rule and to prevent any future restoration of the dynasty. The fire was also a practical measure; the city had been built to serve as the administrative center of a unified empire, and without that empire, it had no purpose. The conqueror preferred to destroy rather than to preserve what he could not use.

The destruction of Xianyang marked the end of the Qin capital and the end of an era in Chinese urban history. The city that had been built at enormous cost, that had been meant to symbolize the eternal empire, was reduced to ashes within years of its completion. The grand vision of Qin Shi Huang— to create a capital that would endure forever—had been shattered by the very forces of violence and conquest that the emperor had hoped to prevent through his policies.

## Archaeological Evidence

The archaeological investigation of Xianyang has been complicated by the destruction and by subsequent rebuilding on the site. Nevertheless, excavations have revealed important information about the Qin capital and its layout. The remains of palaces, workshops, and residential areas have been identified, providing a glimpse of what the city was like in its prime.

The most significant discoveries have been at the site of Epang Palace, where excavations have revealed the foundations of the main hall and surrounding structures. The platform on which the palace was built measures approximately 1,320 meters from north to south and 420 meters from east to west, covering an area of over 550,000 square meters. The scale of the foundation confirms the traditional descriptions of a massive structure, though the full extent of the complex has not yet been determined.

Excavations have also revealed the remains of the royal palace within the city, including the foundations of administrative buildings and the remnants of workshops where craftsmen produced goods for the court. The artifacts uncovered include bronze vessels, jade ornaments, and ceramic fragments, all of which provide evidence of the wealth and sophistication of the Qin court.

The site of Xianyang has been continuously occupied since the Qin period, with subsequent dynasties building their own capitals on or near the location. This continuous occupation has disturbed the Qin remains, but it has also preserved them beneath the layers of later construction. Future excavations may reveal more about the layout and appearance of the Qin capital, adding to our understanding of this remarkable achievement.

## Legacy

The capital at Xianyang, despite its destruction, established patterns that would influence Chinese urban planning for centuries. The concept of a capital designed to express imperial authority, with the palace at the center and the city arranged according to cosmic principles, became a standard feature of subsequent Chinese empires. Later dynasties would build their own capitals—Chang'an, Luoyang, Kaifeng, Beijing—following principles derived from the Qin example.

The destruction of Xianyang also became a cautionary tale in Chinese political culture. The burning of the capital by Xiang Yu was interpreted by later generations as a judgment on the Qin dynasty, a divine punishment for the tyranny and excess of the first emperor. The story was invoked by critics of later dynasties to warn against the dangers of excessive ambition and the resentment that such ambition could provoke.

The memory of Xianyang was preserved not only in historical accounts but also in literature and art. Poets and painters evoked the ruined capital as a symbol of the transience of worldly achievement, the way in which even the greatest accomplishments could be undone by time and violence. The site itself became a place of pilgrimage, where visitors could contemplate the remains of the grand vision that had been destroyed.

### Chapter Notes

The archaeological investigation of Xianyang is ongoing, with new discoveries adding to our understanding of the Qin capital. The identification of Epang Palace and other structures has confirmed many details from the historical sources, though questions remain about the extent of construction and the timeline of the building program. The destruction of the city by Xiang Yu is well documented in historical sources, though the precise events and their motivations continue to be debated by scholars.

The ideological significance of Xianyang has also been the subject of scholarly analysis. Some scholars emphasize the continuity between Qin urban planning and that of earlier periods, arguing that the Qin capital drew on traditions that predated the unification. Others have emphasized the innovative aspects of the Qin construction, pointing to the unprecedented scale and the explicit ideological program that guided the building.
