# Chapter 28: The Conspiracy of Hu Hai

The enthronement of Hu Hai as the Second Emperor of Qin marked one of the most consequential moments in Chinese history. In the summer of 210 BCE, the young prince—barely in his twenties—ascended to the throne of the empire his father had created through decades of war and consolidation. Yet this succession was not the product of legitimate inheritance or imperial wisdom. It was the result of a conspiracy orchestrated by a palace eunuch and a chancellor who had manipulated the death of Qin Shi Huang to place their favored candidate on the throne. The consequences of this manipulation would unfold with devastating speed, as the empire that had taken generations to build began to unravel within months of the new emperor's accession.

## The Second Emperor Ascends

When the news of Qin Shi Huang's death was officially announced in Xianyang, the court was presented with a fait accompli. Hu Hai was proclaimed emperor, and the funeral arrangements for the first emperor began immediately. The new ruler was young, inexperienced, and utterly dependent on the men who had placed him on the throne. Zhao Gao, the chief eunuch who had orchestrated the conspiracy, became the de facto ruler of the empire, while Li Si, the chancellor, continued to manage the day-to-day affairs of government.

The Second Emperor's first actions were designed to eliminate potential rivals and to consolidate his position. The most pressing threat was Fusu, the elder prince who had been sent to command the northern army. Even though Fusu had been ordered to commit suicide in the forged edict, Zhao Gao and Li Si could not be certain that the order had been carried out until they received confirmation. When reports arrived confirming Fusu's death, the conspirators breathed a momentary sigh of relief—but their relief was premature, for Fusu was not the only potential rival.

Several other princes of the Qin royal house remained in the capital, and some of them had greater military experience or more support among the officials than Hu Hai. The Second Emperor moved quickly to eliminate these threats. Over the following months, he ordered the execution of twelve princes who had been sent to the eastern commanderies, as well as several princesses. The killings were carried out in secret, and the bodies were buried in a common grave—a execution method that was meant to humiliate the victims and to demonstrate the new emperor's ruthlessness.

The elimination of the royal family was followed by a purge of officials who might have been loyal to Fusu or who might oppose the new regime. Meng Tian, the famed general who had commanded the northern armies and overseen the construction of the Great Wall, was a particular target. Although Meng Tian had been a loyal servant of Qin Shi Huang, his relationship with Fusu made him a threat to the new emperor. Zhao Gao contrived to have Meng Tian arrested and imprisoned, and he was eventually forced to commit suicide along with his brother Meng Yi. The death of Meng Tian eliminated one of the most capable military leaders in the empire—a man whose skills would have been desperately needed in the months and years to come.

## The Consolidation of Zhao Gao's Power

With the elimination of potential rivals, Zhao Gao moved to consolidate his own position within the government. The eunuch had become the Second Emperor's closest advisor, and he used this position to accumulate power and to eliminate those who might challenge his authority. Li Si, who had been a key partner in the original conspiracy, soon found himself marginalized and eventually eliminated.

The conflict between Zhao Gao and Li Si began almost immediately after the enthronement. Li Si, as chancellor, held a position of great formal authority, and he had not been consulted on many of the decisions that Zhao Gao was making. The eunuch, meanwhile, resented Li Si's influence and sought to reduce it. The struggle between the two men would ultimately end with Li Si's execution, but not before the empire had suffered significant damage.

According to the historical accounts, the immediate cause of Li Si's downfall was a dispute over how to handle the rebellions that were beginning to erupt across the empire. When news of the Chen Sheng uprising reached the capital, Li Si and other officials urged the Second Emperor to take immediate action to suppress the revolt and to address the grievances that had provoked it. Zhao Gao, however, counseled delay and secrecy, arguing that the emperor should not be troubled with such matters. The historical sources describe how Zhao Gao manipulated the situation to turn the Second Emperor against Li Si, eventually convincing him that the chancellor was a traitor.

Li Si was arrested on charges of conspiracy and was executed along with his entire family. The method of execution was particularly brutal: Li Si was subjected to the five-punishments (the most severe form of corporal punishment in Qin law) and then executed. His death removed one of the last checks on Zhao Gao's power, and the eunuch now became the supreme authority in the empire, holding the title of chancellor while the Second Emperor withdrew into increasingly debauched pleasures.

## The Rule of the Second Emperor

Hu Hai, as the Second Emperor, proved to be one of the most incompetent rulers in Chinese history. The young emperor, who had never been prepared for governance, was easily dominated by Zhao Gao and spent his time in indulgence rather than in the duties of state. The historical sources describe a ruler who was obsessed with pleasure, who took delight in cruelty, and who was utterly indifferent to the suffering of his subjects.

According to Sima Qian's account, the Second Emperor's excesses knew no bounds. He continued the construction of his father's mausoleum, adding to its grandeur while ignoring the needs of the living. He expanded the imperial palaces, building new structures to house his collection of concubines and treasures. He instituted heavy taxes to fund his pleasures, extracting from the peasants the resources they needed to survive. The legal harshness that had characterized Qin rule was not only maintained but intensified, as the emperor and his minister sought to crush any expression of dissent.

The Second Emperor's cruelty extended to members of his own court. When he learned that some of the officials disagreed with his policies, he ordered their execution. When he suspected that some of the local officials were not fully loyal, he had them killed and replaced with men who would do his bidding regardless of the consequences. The terror that Zhao Gao had cultivated in the conspiracy now became a tool of governance, as the regime sought to maintain control through fear rather than through legitimacy.

The regime's paranoia reached its apex in what became known as the "slandering of the bonnets" (誣告罪), a policy that encouraged the accusation of innocent people. According to this policy, anyone could be accused of crimes, and the accusers were rewarded for their information. The result was a wave of denunciations that swept through the empire, as people accused their neighbors, their rivals, and even their family members in hopes of gaining favor or avoiding punishment. The legal system was overwhelmed, and the prisons were filled with prisoners who had been condemned without fair trial.

## The Transformation of the Empire

The combination of the Second Emperor's incompetence and Zhao Gao's ruthless consolidation of power produced a transformation in the character of the Qin regime. The first emperor, whatever his faults, had been a ruler of vision who had unified the empire and imposed a coherent system of governance. His son, by contrast, was a puppet whose only concern was his own pleasure, and the minister who controlled him was interested only in his own power. The empire that Qin Shi Huang had built began to collapse under the weight of its own contradictions.

The policies that had been implemented during the first emperor's reign were continued, but they were implemented in their most extreme form without any of the balancing considerations that Qin Shi Huang had sometimes shown. The heavy taxes that had funded the Great Wall and the imperial projects were increased to fund the Second Emperor's pleasures. The harsh laws that had maintained order were applied with increased severity, as local officials sought to demonstrate their loyalty by exceeding the quotas for arrests and punishments. The forced labor that had built the roads and palaces was expanded to include even more massive construction projects.

The result was a regime that had become a caricature of its former self. The Legalist philosophy that had guided Qin governance was not merely continued but degenerated into a form of naked oppression. The Legalists had argued that a strong state required strong laws and a capable ruler; under the Second Emperor, the laws remained strong, but the ruler was weak, and the enforcement of the laws served only to enrich and empower the officials who administered them.

The transformation was particularly evident in the treatment of the conquered peoples. During Qin Shi Huang's reign, there had been some effort to integrate the former states into a unified empire, to use the conquered elites as local administrators, and to present the unification as a positive development. Under the Second Emperor, this effort was abandoned entirely. The former Chu, Qi, Wei, Yan, and Zhao peoples were treated as subject populations to be exploited, and any expression of regional identity was suppressed with extreme prejudice.

## The Court Intrigues

The internal dynamics of the Second Emperor's court were characterized by a complex web of intrigue, manipulation, and betrayal. Zhao Gao, having eliminated Li Si and other potential rivals, was now the supreme power behind the throne, but his position was not entirely secure. He had to maintain the appearance of loyalty to the emperor while simultaneously manipulating the young ruler for his own purposes. The court became a place where anyone could become a victim of slander or accusation, where survival depended on one's ability to navigate the treacherous waters of imperial politics.

One of the most notorious episodes of this period was the execution of Li Si, which was followed by the execution of numerous other officials who had served the first emperor. The historical sources describe how Zhao Gao convinced the Second Emperor that these officials were plotting against him, and how the young emperor readily believed these accusations. The result was a series of purges that eliminated almost everyone who had experience in governing the empire.

The Second Emperor's own court was dominated by a group of young men who had been his companions in pleasure. These favorites, many of them from the same region as the emperor, were given positions of power and influence regardless of their abilities. They became the targets of Zhao Gao's manipulations, as the eunuch sought to turn the emperor against anyone who might challenge his authority.

The isolation of the Second Emperor from any honest counsel was nearly complete. No one dared to tell the emperor the truth about the situation in the empire, for fear of being accused of disloyalty. The officials who might have provided guidance were either dead, in prison, or too terrified to speak honestly. The result was a ruler who was completely out of touch with reality, who believed that the empire was prosperous and peaceful when in fact it was on the brink of collapse.

## The Empire's Transformation Completed

By the end of 210 BCE, the Qin Empire had been fundamentally transformed. The regime that Qin Shi Huang had created was still nominally in place—the institutions, the laws, the administrative structures all remained—but their character had been changed beyond recognition. The empire that had been built on the vision of a great ruler was now being destroyed by the shortsightedness of his successor and the ambition of a treacherous minister.

The transformation was not only political but also cultural and psychological. The sense of purpose that had characterized the first emperor's reign—the feeling that the unification of China was a great historical achievement, that the construction of the Great Wall was a noble endeavor, that the standardization of the empire was a beneficial reform—all of this had been lost. In its place was a sense of despair, resentment, and hopelessness. The people who had welcomed the unification as a end to the wars of the Warring States period now regretted the day that Qin had conquered their lands.

The stage was now set for the dramatic events that would unfold over the next three years. The empire that had seemed so permanent, so powerful, so destined to last forever, was about to be swept away by a wave of rebellion that would ultimately give rise to a new dynasty. But before the rebellions could begin in earnest, one more act of betrayal remained: the final consolidation of Zhao Gao's power and the complete isolation of the Second Emperor from any possibility of reform.

### Chapter Notes

The conspiracy that placed Hu Hai on the throne has been the subject of extensive historical analysis. The traditional account, based primarily on Sima Qian's Records of the Grand Historian, presents Zhao Gao as the primary villain—a cunning eunuch who manipulated the succession for his own purposes. Modern scholars have sometimes challenged this interpretation, arguing that the conspiracy was more complex and that Zhao Gao may have been acting in response to structural factors rather than purely personal ambition.

The fate of Li Si has also been debated. Some scholars view him as a tragic figure—a capable administrator who was eliminated by a treacherous colleague. Others have argued that Li Si was himself complicit in the original conspiracy and that his eventual downfall was a consequence of his own actions.

The characterization of the Second Emperor as a debauched and incompetent ruler is based primarily on the hostile sources that were written after the dynasty's fall. Some modern scholars have suggested that the sources may have exaggerated the Second Emperor's faults for political reasons, as part of the Han dynasty's effort to delegitimize the Qin.
