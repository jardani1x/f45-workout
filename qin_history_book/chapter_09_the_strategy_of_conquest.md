# Chapter 9: The Strategy of Conquest

The unification of China under Qin Shi Huang was not merely the product of military superiority; it was the result of a sophisticated and sustained strategy that combined military force with diplomatic manipulation, economic pressure, and psychological warfare. Qin systematically exploited the divisions among its rivals, playing one state against another while building its own strength for the final conquest. Understanding this strategy is essential to comprehending how a state that had been considered a marginal power on the western frontier came to dominate the entire Chinese world.

The strategy that Qin employed in its conquest of the Warring States has been studied for over two millennia, and its principles have been applied in subsequent Chinese history and beyond. The key elements of this strategy—the prioritization of diplomacy over military force when possible, the exploitation of rivalries and suspicions, the patient accumulation of advantage, and the willingness to use both overt and covert methods—reflect a sophisticated understanding of power politics that continues to resonate.

## The Strategic Environment

Before examining Qin's specific tactics, it is important to understand the strategic environment in which the conquest took place. By the mid-third century BCE, the balance of power that had characterized the Warring States system had shifted decisively in Qin's favor. The Legalist reforms had created a state with unprecedented organizational capacity, and the Qin army had demonstrated its superiority in repeated engagements with rival states.

Yet Qin still faced significant challenges. The other major states, while individually weaker than Qin, retained substantial military capabilities. The formation of a successful coalition against Qin could still pose a serious threat, as the vertical alliances of the past had demonstrated. Moreover, the geographic position of Qin, while offering protection from the west, also meant that the state had to fight wars on multiple fronts when it moved against its neighbors.

The solution that Qin developed was a comprehensive strategy that combined military and diplomatic approaches in a systematic manner. This strategy was not the creation of a single brilliant mind but rather the accumulated wisdom of generations of Qin rulers and their advisors, refined and perfected over time. Its implementation required patience, consistency, and a willingness to accept short-term costs in exchange for long-term gains.

## The Principle of "Ally with Distant States, Attack Nearby Ones"

The foundational principle of Qin's diplomatic strategy was expressed in the formula "ally with distant states, attack nearby ones" (远交近攻). This principle recognized that the most immediate threats came from neighboring states, while more distant states could be cultivated as allies or at least neutralized through diplomatic means.

The logic of this principle was both strategic and practical. Distant states posed less immediate threat because they could not easily mobilize forces for sustained campaigns across long distances. They also offered opportunities for diplomatic engagement that could isolate nearby enemies. By cultivating relationships with distant states, Qin could prevent the formation of coalitions and focus its military efforts on one front at a time.

The implementation of this principle required Qin to maintain different relationships with different states. Distant states like Qi, in the far east, and Chu, in the far south, were offered friendly relations, trade agreements, and diplomatic overtures designed to keep them neutral or supportive. Nearby states like Wei, Zhao, and Han, which bordered Qin directly, were targeted for military attack and territorial conquest.

This principle was not merely a guideline for diplomacy; it also shaped military strategy. Rather than dispersing forces across multiple fronts, Qin concentrated its attacks on specific targets while maintaining defensive positions elsewhere. This approach allowed the state to achieve local superiority and defeat its enemies in detail.

## The Policy of "Divide and Negotiate"

While "ally with distant states, attack nearby ones" described the overall framework of Qin's strategy, the more specific policy of "divide and negotiate" (连横) referred to the systematic effort to exploit divisions among the rival states. The term "divide and negotiate" originally described alliances between Qin and individual states, but it came to encompass a broader approach that included bribery, deception, and the manipulation of rivalries.

The fundamental insight behind this policy was that the other Warring States were unlikely to form lasting coalitions against Qin because of their own competing interests. Each state had its own ambitions, its own grievances, and its own relationships with the other states. Qin could exploit these divisions by offering particular states incentives to remain neutral or to collaborate with Qin at the expense of their rivals.

The tools of divide and negotiate included territorial concessions, trade benefits, diplomatic honors, and outright bribery. Qin diplomats were authorized to offer significant inducements to states that would cooperate, while also threatening those that opposed Qin's interests. The goal was to create a situation in which each state calculated that cooperation with Qin was more beneficial than opposition.

Historical records describe numerous examples of this strategy in action. In some cases, Qin offered to return territory that it had captured in exchange for neutrality or alliance. In other cases, Qin provided economic benefits or diplomatic support to states that were willing to align with Qin. The specific terms varied according to circumstances, but the underlying logic was consistent: divide the opposition and isolate specific targets for conquest.

## Bribery and Diplomatic Manipulation

A particularly controversial element of Qin's strategy was the extensive use of bribery to influence officials and decision-makers in rival states. Qin maintained a sophisticated program of bribery that targeted ministers, generals, and even rulers in the other Warring States, offering them wealth and honors in exchange for policies favorable to Qin.

The *Strategies of the Warring States* (*Zhangu Ce*), a collection of diplomatic documents from the period, contains numerous examples of this bribery. In one famous episode, Qin is said to have sent a large amount of gold to a Zhao minister, which he used to persuade his ruler to reject a proposed alliance with other states against Qin. In another example, Qin diplomats are said to have distributed bribes among the ministers of Chu, creating divisions that prevented the formation of a coalition.

The effectiveness of this bribery was based on the political conditions of the Warring States. The Warring States were not modern nation-states with strong institutions; they were personalist polities in which individual ministers and generals wielded significant influence. By corrupting key individuals, Qin could affect the decision-making of entire states without engaging in direct military confrontation.

The use of bribery has been criticized by some historians as unethical or dishonorable, but it was entirely consistent with the realist logic of Warring States politics. In an era of existential competition, states employed whatever means were available to gain advantage over their rivals. The acceptance of bribes by officials in the rival states was equally a product of this environment.

## Intelligence and Espionage

Complementing the bribery program was a sophisticated system of intelligence gathering that allowed Qin to understand the capabilities, intentions, and vulnerabilities of its rivals. The Qin state maintained networks of spies and informants that penetrated the administration and military of rival states, providing information that was crucial for planning military campaigns and diplomatic initiatives.

The *Strategies of the Warring States* contains references to the activities of Qin spies, and later sources describe the organization of intelligence operations in some detail. According to these accounts, Qin employed a variety of methods, including the insertion of agents into rival courts, the cultivation of informants among merchants and travelers, and the analysis of public information to infer developments in rival states.

One particularly important form of intelligence was the understanding of internal politics in rival states. By monitoring the relationships among ministers, the succession struggles within ruling families, and the factions within administrations, Qin could identify opportunities for diplomatic manipulation or military exploitation. The death of a ruler, the rise of a new minister, or a change in factional alliances could all create vulnerabilities that Qin could exploit.

The integration of intelligence with strategy was a hallmark of Qin's approach. Military campaigns were planned on the basis of detailed information about enemy capabilities and dispositions, and diplomatic initiatives were timed to take advantage of internal divisions in rival states. This integration of information and action gave Qin a significant advantage over rivals that lacked comparable intelligence capabilities.

## The Systematic Elimination of Rivals

With this comprehensive strategy in place, Qin proceeded to systematically eliminate its rivals one by one. The conquest proceeded in stages, with Qin targeting the weakest states first while maintaining diplomatic relationships with more distant powers. This methodical approach allowed Qin to build momentum while avoiding the formation of effective opposition.

The conquest began with Han, the smallest of the Warring States, which was annexed in 230 BCE. Han's small territory and vulnerable position made it an easy target, and its fall opened the door for further expansion. Following the conquest of Han, Qin turned against Wei, which had been weakened by earlier defeats and was unable to resist effectively. Wei was annexed in 225 BCE.

The conquest of Zhao proved more difficult, as Zhao was a larger and more powerful state with a formidable military. The campaigns against Zhao culminated in the Battle of Changping in 260 BCE, one of the bloodiest battles of the Warring States period. The Zhao forces were decisively defeated, and the subsequent execution of 400,000 surrendered soldiers marked a brutal turning point in the conquest. Zhao was annexed in 222 BCE.

Chu, the largest of the Warring States in territory, posed a significant challenge. Qin launched a major campaign against Chu in 223 BCE, but the initial effort failed. A second campaign, launched in 224 BCE under the command of the veteran general Wang Jian (王翦), succeeded in overcoming Chu's defenses, and the state was annexed in 223 BCE.

The conquest of Yan followed quickly, with the state falling in 222 BCE. Yan had been weakened by earlier conflicts and was unable to mount effective resistance. The final conquest was of Qi, which had attempted to remain neutral throughout the later conflicts but was ultimately forced to surrender in 221 BCE.

## The Role of Key Figures

The execution of Qin's strategy depended on the abilities of several key figures who served the state as diplomats, generals, and advisors. Among the most important were the ministers who formulated and implemented the diplomatic strategy, as well as the generals who led the military campaigns.

The diplomatic strategy was associated particularly with the minister Zhang Yi (张仪), who served as chancellor under King Huiwen (惠文王) in the early third century BCE. Zhang Yi was the principal advocate of the "divide and negotiate" policy and is credited with developing the diplomatic approach that isolated Qin's rivals. His skill in argumentation and persuasion was legendary, and he was able to extract significant concessions from rival states through a combination of threats and inducements.

However, the foundational principle "ally with distant states, attack nearby ones" (远交近攻) was formulated by Fan Ju (范雎) during King Zhaoxiang's reign (c. 270 BCE). Fan Ju's strategy appears in *Strategies of the Warring States* (*Zhangu Ce*), chapter 3. While Zhang Yi championed *horizontal alliances (连横)*, he did not originate this specific maxim.

Other diplomats continued this approach in later periods, maintaining the relationships with distant states while targeting nearby enemies for conquest. The consistency of this strategy over generations indicates that it was not merely the preference of individual ministers but a systematic policy of the Qin state.

The military campaigns were led by generals like Wang Jian, who commanded the campaigns against Chu, and Lian Po (廉颇) and Bai Qi (白起), who led earlier campaigns against other states. These generals combined tactical skill with the organizational capacity of the Qin military system to achieve victories that might have seemed impossible given the scale of resistance.

The integration of diplomatic and military efforts was coordinated by the Qin rulers themselves, particularly King Zhaoxiang (昭襄王) and his successors. The long-term planning and patient execution of the strategy required consistent support from the highest levels of government, and the Qin rulers provided this support over generations.

## The Conquest and the Birth of Empire

The final conquest of Qi in 221 BCE marked the end of the Warring States period and the birth of the first unified Chinese empire. Ying Zheng, who now took the title Qin Shi Huang (First Emperor of Qin), became the ruler of a territory that encompassed the entire Chinese world.

The transition from conquest to governance presented new challenges that tested the limits of Qin's organizational capacity. The administration of a unified empire required different approaches than the conquest of individual states, and the strategies that had enabled unification were not necessarily suited to the maintenance of empire. The problems that Qin would face in the years after unification would ultimately lead to the collapse of the dynasty.

Yet the strategy of conquest had succeeded in its immediate objective. The fragmented political landscape of the Warring States had been replaced by a unified state of unprecedented scale. The methods that Qin employed—diplomatic manipulation, bribery, espionage, and military force—had proven effective against each of the major powers in turn.

## Legacy and Interpretation

The strategy that Qin employed in its conquest has been studied and debated for over two millennia. Subsequent Chinese dynasties drew lessons from Qin's experience, and the strategies of alliance, division, and manipulation became standard features of Chinese diplomatic practice. The principle of "ally with distant states, attack nearby ones" became a fundamental guideline for Chinese foreign policy.

Western observers have also found relevance in Qin's approach, drawing parallels to modern theories of international relations and strategy. The combination of military power with diplomatic manipulation, the exploitation of divisions among rivals, and the patient accumulation of advantage all resonate with familiar themes in strategic studies.

Yet the strategy also raises ethical questions that continue to be debated. The use of bribery, deception, and brutal military force was characteristic of the Warring States period, but it also established patterns that would persist in Chinese imperial politics. Whether these methods can be justified as pragmatic responses to the conditions of the era, or whether they represent morally problematic approaches to power, remains a matter of interpretation.

What is clear is that Qin's strategy was extraordinarily effective in achieving its objectives. The unification of China was a monumental achievement that transformed the political landscape of East Asia and established foundations for Chinese civilization that would endure for millennia. The strategy that made this achievement possible reflected both the particular conditions of the Warring States period and timeless principles of power politics.

### Chapter Notes

The primary sources for Qin's conquest strategy include the *Strategies of the Warring States* (*Zhangu Ce*), a collection of diplomatic and military documents, and Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* (*Shiji*). These sources provide detailed accounts of diplomatic negotiations, military campaigns, and the activities of key individuals.

The interpretation of Qin's strategy has been the subject of extensive scholarly debate. Some scholars emphasize the effectiveness of Qin's approach, arguing that it represented a sophisticated and rational strategy that exploited the weaknesses of rivals. Others have been more critical, focusing on the ethical problems posed by bribery, deception, and brutality.

The role of bribery in Qin's diplomacy has been particularly debated. Some scholars accept the traditional accounts at face value, while others have suggested that they may reflect later propaganda or the biases of Confucian historians who were hostile to Qin. The evidence is fragmentary, and definitive conclusions are difficult to reach.

The chronology of the conquest is generally well-established, with the major campaigns dated to specific years in the third century BCE. However, some details remain disputed, and alternative chronologies have been proposed by various scholars.
