# Chapter 27: The Final Journey (210 BCE)

In the autumn of 210 BCE, Qin Shi Huang embarked on what would be his final tour of the empire. The first emperor was now in his late forties, though the hardships of his life and the effects of the elixirs he had consumed had aged him prematurely. The tour was intended to inspect the eastern territories, to survey the construction of the Great Wall, and to perform the rituals that would cement his connection to the empire he ruled. But fate had other plans. Before the tour was complete, the emperor would fall ill and die, setting in motion a chain of events that would reshape the course of Chinese history.

## The Eastern Tour

The tour of 210 BCE was not the first that Qin Shi Huang had undertaken since unifying the empire. The emperor had made several journeys across his domains, inspecting the territories, overseeing major construction projects, and performing the ceremonies that expressed his authority. These tours were both practical and symbolic: they allowed the emperor to verify that his orders were being carried out, and they reminded his subjects of his presence and power.

The eastern tour of 210 BCE began from the capital at Xianyang in the late summer or early autumn. The imperial procession was enormous, including not only the emperor's carriage but also thousands of attendants, officials, soldiers, and servants. The emperor traveled in one of several identical carriages, a security measure designed to prevent assassination. The procession moved eastward, following the route of the newly constructed roads that connected the capital to the eastern seacoast.

The first major destination was the region of the former Qi state, now reorganized as the Qi Commandery. This area, along the coast of the Yellow Sea, was significant for several reasons. It was the home of many of the merchants and craftsmen who had been involved in the maritime trade that flourished during the Warring States period. It was also the region where the legendary "men of the sea" had sought the immortal islands in ancient times, making it a place of particular interest to the emperor who sought longevity.

From Qi, the procession traveled south to the former Chu state, another region that had been deeply affected by the unification. The emperor inspected the local administration, received tribute from the local officials, and performed ceremonies at the sites of former Chu capitals. These ceremonies were intended to demonstrate the emperor's authority over the conquered territories and to symbolically incorporate the former states into the unified empire.

The tour also included inspections of the Great Wall construction in the north. Although the wall had been substantially completed by this time, ongoing work continued at various points, and the emperor wanted to verify the progress. The northern frontier was strategically vital, and the wall represented a massive investment of resources and labor. The emperor's presence was meant to encourage the workers and to remind the military commanders of his personal attention to their efforts.

## Illness and Death

As the imperial procession traveled through the eastern territories, Qin Shi Huang fell ill. The nature of the illness is not entirely clear from the historical sources, which describe the symptoms in general terms. The emperor experienced what was described as a fever and a feeling of great heat, symptoms that could indicate any of a number of ailments. Some scholars have suggested that he may have been suffering from the effects of the mercury-based elixirs that he had consumed in his quest for immortality, though this interpretation remains speculative.

The emperor's condition worsened as the tour continued. By the time the procession reached the area near the city of Shaqiu (沙丘) in present-day Hebei province, he was too ill to continue. The historical sources describe the emperor's final days in dramatic terms, emphasizing the tension and uncertainty that gripped the court as the ruler lay dying.

Qin Shi Huang had not named a successor, and the question of who would inherit the throne was now urgent. The emperor's eldest son, Fusu (扶苏), was the obvious heir, but he had been sent away from the capital to serve as commander of the armies in the north. Another son, Hu Hai (胡亥), was with the emperor on the tour, and he was reportedly eager to claim the throne for himself.

The historical sources describe a final, poignant moment in which the emperor attempted to dictate a letter to his eldest son. According to this account, Qin Shi Huang instructed Fusu to return to the capital and to take command of the empire upon his death. The letter was entrusted to a trusted messenger, but it was never delivered. The circumstances of this failure would become one of the great mysteries of the Qin period.

## The Conspiracy

As the emperor's condition deteriorated, a conspiracy developed among the officials and attendants who were present. The key figures in this conspiracy were the chief eunuch Zhao Gao (赵高) and the chancellor Li Si (李斯). Both men had much to lose if Fusu became emperor, and both stood to benefit if Hu Hai could be placed on the throne instead.

Zhao Gao was a palace eunuch who had served the emperor for many years. He was, according to later sources, a man of great cunning and ambition who had accumulated considerable power through his position close to the emperor. Zhao Gao had also cultivated a relationship with Hu Hai, the young prince who was traveling with the emperor, and he sought to use this relationship to advance his own interests.

Li Si, the chancellor, was one of the most powerful officials in the empire. He had been instrumental in the unification and had served as the chief architect of many of the policies that characterized Qin rule. But Li Si had reasons to fear the succession of Fusu. The historical sources suggest that Fusu had expressed criticism of some of Li Si's policies, and the chancellor feared that he would be removed from power or punished if the elder prince became emperor.

Together, Zhao Gao and Li Si devised a plan to conceal the emperor's death and to manipulate the succession. They kept the news of the emperor's illness from the outside world, allowing no one to see him except their most trusted associates. They forged letters in the emperor's name, ordering Fusu to commit suicide and naming Hu Hai as the heir. These letters were sent to the northern army, where Fusu was serving, and the prince, unaware of the deception, obeyed the supposed command of his father and took his own life.

With Fusu out of the way, the conspirators were free to proceed with their plan. They announced the emperor's death to the court and proclaimed Hu Hai as his successor. The body was placed in a carriage and prepared for transport back to the capital. The conspiracy had succeeded, but its success would prove to be the beginning of the end for the Qin dynasty.

## The Return to Xianyang

The return journey from Shaqiu to Xianyang was one of the most bizarre episodes in Chinese history. The conspirators faced a serious problem: if the news of the emperor's death became known, there was a risk of rebellion or chaos. The empire was vast and only loosely held together; the authority of the central government depended heavily on the person of the emperor. If it became known that the emperor was dead, there was no telling how the various regional authorities would respond.

To prevent the news from spreading, Zhao Gao and Li Si ordered that the emperor's body be placed in a closed carriage and treated as if the emperor were still alive. The officials who came to pay their respects were kept at a distance and were not permitted to see the body. The carriage was sealed, and the windows were covered to prevent anyone from looking inside.

The smell of the decaying fish would mask the smell of the decaying body, and anyone who approached the carriage would attribute any unpleasant odor to the fish rather than to the emperor's corpse. This macabre solution allowed the conspirators to maintain the pretense of the emperor's survival until the procession reached the capital.

The journey from Shaqiu to Xianyang took several weeks, during which time the conspirators maintained their deception. The imperial procession traveled slowly, stopping at various points where the emperor was traditionally expected to appear. At each stop, officials presented reports and received instructions that had been prepared by Zhao Gao and Li Si in the emperor's name. The deception was remarkably successful; despite the unusual circumstances, no one seems to have suspected the truth.

When the procession finally reached the capital, the conspirators announced the emperor's death and the succession of Hu Hai. The new emperor—known in history as the Second Emperor—quickly moved to consolidate his power, eliminating potential rivals and rewarding those who had supported the conspiracy. The funeral arrangements were made, and preparations began for the burial of the first emperor at the mausoleum that had been under construction for years.

## The Mausoleum and the "Crying Ghost" Strategy

Qin Shi Huang had spent enormous resources on the construction of his mausoleum, which was located near the capital at a site now known as Lintong (临潼). The tomb complex covered a vast area and included not only the burial chamber but also numerous pits containing the Terracotta Army, horses, chariots, and other objects intended to serve the emperor in the afterlife. The construction had been ongoing for decades, and it was not yet complete when the emperor died.

The burial itself was carried out in secret, following a strategy that the historical sources describe as the "crying ghost" (哭鬼) approach. According to this account, Zhao Gao advised the Second Emperor to keep the burial secret and to send a false report claiming that the emperor had gone to the eastern sea to meet the immortal gods. This report was intended to explain the emperor's absence from public view during the period of mourning and to prevent any disruption that might be caused by the announcement of his death.

The "crying ghost" strategy had another dimension as well. The historical sources describe how the burial procession was accompanied by mourners who wept and cried out, creating the impression of a supernatural presence. This display was intended to discourage any who might seek to rob the tomb or to interfere with the burial. The combination of secrecy and theatrical display ensured that the emperor was buried without incident, at least in the immediate term.

The tomb itself was constructed on an enormous scale. According to Sima Qian's account, the burial chamber was decorated to resemble the imperial palace, with models of buildings, chambers filled with treasures, and rivers of mercury representing the seas and oceans. Mechanical devices were installed to prevent robbery, and the tomb was sealed with all who had knowledge of its contents inside—literarily, according to some accounts, the craftsmen who had built the tomb were sealed inside to prevent them from revealing its secrets.

The construction of the tomb was not completed until after the emperor's burial, and even then, ongoing work continued for years. The pits containing the Terracotta Army, for example, were not yet finished when the dynasty fell, and the figures that have been excavated show signs of incomplete production. The haste of the burial and the subsequent chaos prevented the full realization of the grand vision that Qin Shi Huang had intended for his eternal resting place.

## The Fate of the Empire

The death of Qin Shi Huang and the succession of Hu Hai marked the beginning of the end for the Qin dynasty. The Second Emperor was young, inexperienced, and dominated by Zhao Gao, who had become the most powerful figure in the government. Under their rule, the policies that had contributed to the unification—centralization, standardization, harsh legalism—were continued and even intensified, provoking widespread resentment.

The consequences of the "crying ghost" deception were significant. By concealing the emperor's death and manipulating the succession, Zhao Gao and Li Si had prevented the orderly transfer of power that might have stabilized the empire. Instead, they had placed on the throne a ruler who was illequipped to govern, and they had eliminated the one person—Fusu—who might have provided an alternative. The stage was set for the collapse that would come within just a few years.

Within months of the emperor's death, rebellions broke out across the empire. Chen Sheng and Wu Guang, who had been sent to lead a group of peasants to the northern frontier, instead led them in rebellion against the government. Although this initial uprising was suppressed, it inspired numerous other revolts. Among the rebel leaders were figures who would become famous in Chinese history: Liu Bang, who would found the Han dynasty, and Xiang Yu, who would destroy the Qin but ultimately lose the contest for the throne.

The Second Emperor and Zhao Gao attempted to suppress the rebellions, but their efforts were undermined by poor judgment and internal conflict. Li Si, who had been a key figure in the conspiracy, was eventually executed by Zhao Gao, who took over the chancellery himself. The empire that Qin Shi Huang had built with such ambition was crumbling, and there was no one capable of saving it.

In 207 BCE, the rebel army led by Liu Bang approached the capital. Rather than face capture, the Second Emperor committed suicide, bringing the Qin dynasty to an end. The empire that had been created through such effort—the unification of China, the construction of the Great Wall, the standardization of weights and measures—was gone, replaced by a period of civil war that would last for several years until Liu Bang emerged victorious and established the Han dynasty.

## Historical Assessment

The final journey of Qin Shi Huang was a turning point in Chinese history. The events that followed—the death of the emperor, the conspiracy, the deceptive return to the capital—set in motion a chain of events that destroyed the first empire and created the conditions for the second. The manner of the emperor's death and burial was symbolic of the regime he had created: grandiose in conception, secret in execution, and ultimately doomed by its own internal contradictions.

The "crying ghost" strategy that was employed to conceal the emperor's death reflected the regime's fundamental weakness: its dependence on the person of the emperor rather than on institutions or laws. The Qin had built an empire on the authority of a single ruler, and when that ruler died, there was no mechanism to ensure a peaceful transition. The conspiracy that manipulated the succession only made matters worse, replacing a potentially capable heir with an incompetent one and eliminating the possibility of reform.

The burial of Qin Shi Huang in his magnificent mausoleum, surrounded by the Terracotta Army and the treasures he had accumulated, was meant to ensure his eternal rule. But the tomb itself became a symbol of the dynasty's hubris and its ultimate failure. The resources that had been spent on the tomb, like those spent on the Great Wall, the palaces, and the other grand projects of the reign, had exhausted the empire and provoked the resentment that led to its downfall.

The memory of Qin Shi Huang's final journey would persist in Chinese history and literature. The image of the emperor's corpse hidden in a carriage filled with rotten fish became a symbol of the deception and decay that characterized the end of his reign. The "crying ghost" strategy became a byword for political manipulation and secrecy. And the mausoleum, with its unopened tomb and its army of clay soldiers, became a monument to ambition exceeded only by failure.

Yet Qin Shi Huang's legacy was not entirely negative. Despite the collapse of his dynasty, the unification he had achieved became the model for all subsequent Chinese empires. The centralized administration, the standardized writing, the network of roads and canals—these innovations survived the dynasty that created them and became the foundation of Chinese civilization for the next two millennia. The first emperor had created an empire that would endure, even if his own dynasty did not.

### Chapter Notes

The events of Qin Shi Huang's final journey have been the subject of extensive historical analysis. The traditional account, based primarily on Sima Qian's Records of the Grand Historian, has been both accepted and challenged by modern scholars. Some aspects of the story—the "crying ghost" strategy, the use of rotten fish to mask the smell of the decaying body—seem almost too dramatic to be true, but the basic outline of events is supported by multiple sources and is consistent with what is known about the political situation at the time.

The identity and motivations of the key figures in the conspiracy—Zhao Gao, Li Si, and Hu Hai—have been debated by scholars. Some view Zhao Gao as the primary villain, a cunning eunuch who manipulated the succession for his own purposes. Others have emphasized the structural factors that made the conspiracy possible, including the lack of clear rules for succession and the weakness of the institutional framework for transferring power.

The "crying ghost" strategy has been interpreted in various ways. Some scholars see it as evidence of the regime's desperation, a final attempt to maintain control in the face of collapse. Others have emphasized its symbolic significance, arguing that it reflected the Qin belief in the continuing power of the emperor even after death.
