# Chapter 29: The Collapse (209-207 BCE)

In the autumn of 209 BCE, a group of exhausted soldiers trudged through the marshes of present-day Anhui province, their uniforms torn, their spirits broken. They were heading toward the northern frontier, where they were to reinforce the armies guarding the Great Wall against the Xiongnu. But these were not ordinary soldiers. They were the survivors of a band of nine hundred peasants who had been conscripted for military service, and they were about to spark a rebellion that would bring down the Qin dynasty within two years. The uprising of Chen Sheng and Wu Guang at Dazexiang was not merely a local disturbance; it was the first act of a drama that would reshape the political map of China and establish the conditions for the founding of the Han dynasty.

## The Dazexiang Uprising

The immediate cause of the Dazexiang uprising was the failure of the Qin government to fulfill its obligations to the soldiers who were conscripted for military service. According to the legal codes of the Qin, all adult males were required to serve in the army or to perform labor service for the state. The conscription system was demanding, and the conditions of service were often brutal. Soldiers were expected to report for duty on time, and those who arrived late were subject to severe punishment—even execution.

In the summer of 209 BCE, a group of nine hundred peasants from the region of Anhui (formerly the Chu state) were conscripted for military service and ordered to proceed to the northern frontier. The journey was long and difficult, and the group was led by two men named Chen Sheng and Wu Guang. As they traveled, they encountered heavy rains and flooding that slowed their progress. They soon realized that they would not be able to reach their destination on time, and according to Qin law, they would be subject to execution for their failure.

Faced with the choice between certain death and rebellion, Chen Sheng and Wu Guang chose the latter. They killed the officers who were escorting them and raised the standard of revolt. The rebellion began small—a handful of desperate men in a remote region—but it quickly grew as other groups of conscripts and oppressed peasants joined the cause. Chen Sheng first declared himself "General of Chu," *then* "King of Rising Chu" (張楚, Zhang Chu) after momentum grew—reflecting tactical legitimacy-building.

The significance of the Dazexiang uprising cannot be overstated. It was the first major rebellion against Qin rule since the unification, and it demonstrated that the empire was far more fragile than anyone had imagined. The Qin had maintained order through a combination of military force and administrative control, but they had never established legitimacy in the eyes of the conquered peoples. When the opportunity arose to resist, the resentment that had been building for years exploded into open rebellion.

## The Revival of the Six States

The Dazexiang uprising inspired rebellions across the empire. In region after region, the descendants of the former royal families emerged to claim the thrones of their ancestors. The six states that Qin had conquered—the six kingdoms that had been destroyed in the wars of unification—were reborn, as local leaders and noble families rallied to restore the old order. The map of China, which had been simplified by Qin unification, became complex again, as dozens of small kingdoms and warlord states vied for power.

In the east, the former Qi state was restored, with a descendant of the Qi royal family placed on the throne. In the south, the former Chu state was reestablished, with Chen Sheng claiming the title of king. In the north, the former Zhao and Yan states were revived, with local leaders declaring independence from Qin rule. In the central plains, the former Wei and Han states were also restored, as the Qin administrative system collapsed under the pressure of rebellion.

The revival of the six states was not merely a symbolic gesture. Each of the new kingdoms raised its own army, appointed its own officials, and governed its own territory. The Qin government, which had struggled to maintain control even in peacetime, was completely overwhelmed by the scope of the rebellion. The imperial armies were stretched thin, and the local officials either joined the rebellions or were killed by the insurgents.

The rapidity of the collapse was breathtaking. Within months of the Dazexiang uprising, the Qin Empire had ceased to function as a unified state. The central government at Xianyang still existed, and the Second Emperor still held the title of emperor, but their authority extended only to the territory immediately around the capital. Everywhere else, independent kingdoms had been established, and the Qin commanderies had been either overthrown or forced to negotiate with the rebels.

## The Campaigns of Xiang Yu and Liu Bang

Among the many rebel leaders who emerged two would during this period, come for power to dominate the struggle Yu and: Xiang Liu Bang. These two men, who would become the great rivals of the early Han period, represented different approaches to the rebellion and different visions for the future of China.

Xiang Yu was a young nobleman from the former Chu state, a descendant of the Chu royal family who had fled during the Qin conquest. He was a military leader of extraordinary ability, renowned for his courage, his charisma, and his skill in battle. When the rebellion against Qin rule began, Xiang Yu raised an army of his own and quickly established himself as one of the most powerful rebel leaders. His uncle, Xiang Li, served as his advisor and helped to coordinate his campaigns.

Xiang Yu's military campaigns were characterized by boldness and brutality. He led his armies in a series of devastating attacks against Qin forces, inflicting crushing defeats on the imperial armies wherever he encountered them. His most famous victory was the Battle of Julu (巨鹿之戰) in 207 BCE, where he defeated a Qin army of several hundred thousand men, capturing or killing almost the entire force. The battle was a turning point in the war, as it shattered the Qin army's ability to resist the rebels and opened the road to the capital.

Liu Bang, by contrast, was a man of humble origins. He had been a peasant or a minor official in the former Qin territory, and he had risen to leadership through a combination of military skill and political acumen. When the rebellion began, Liu Bang led his own small band of followers and gradually expanded his territory through a series of campaigns in the east. He was less brilliant than Xiang Yu as a military commander, but he was more patient and more skilled at building alliances.

Liu Bang's approach to the rebellion was different from Xiang Yu's. While Xiang Yu sought to destroy the Qin military through decisive battles, Liu Bang sought to undermine the Qin administration through political maneuvering. He made alliances with other rebel leaders, promised land and titles to those who joined him, and cultivated the support of the local populations in the territories he conquered. His campaigns were slower but more sustainable, and he was able to establish a stable base of operations in the east.

The relationship between Xiang Yu and Liu Bang was complex. Initially, they were allies against the common enemy, and they fought together in some campaigns. But as the rebellion progressed, tensions developed between them. Xiang Yu, as the senior nobleman, expected to be recognized as the leader of the rebellion, while Liu Bang, with his growing power, had ambitions of his own. The stage was set for the conflict that would determine the future of China.

## The Fall of the Second Emperor

While the rebel armies were gaining strength in the east, the situation in the Qin capital was deteriorating rapidly. The Second Emperor, isolated in his palace and dominated by Zhao Gao, was completely out of touch with reality. He continued to indulge in pleasure while the empire crumbled around him, and he refused to acknowledge the gravity of the situation until it was too late.

Zhao Gao, who had consolidated his power through the elimination of rivals, now faced a crisis that he could not control. The rebel armies were advancing, the administrative system was collapsing, and the imperial forces were failing. The eunuch attempted to maintain the pretense of normalcy, but it was clear to everyone that the dynasty was on the brink of collapse. Zhao Gao's response was to become even more ruthless, executing anyone who suggested that the situation was hopeless and demanding ever-greater efforts from the remaining loyal officials.

The final crisis came in 207 BCE, when the rebel army under Liu Bang approached the capital. The Qin forces that had been sent to stop the rebels had been defeated, and there were no more armies to defend the city. The Second Emperor, confronted with the reality of his situation, realized that he could not escape. According to the historical sources, he asked to be allowed to retreat to the western region and to rule as a king, but this request was rejected. On the first day of the twelfth month of 207 BCE, the Second Emperor committed suicide, ending the Qin dynasty.

The death of the Second Emperor marked the end of the Qin dynasty, but it did not immediately end the conflict. Zhao Gao, who had orchestrated so much of the tragedy, tried to maintain power by placing a puppet emperor on the throne, but his regime lasted only a few months. In 206 BCE, the rebel armies under Xiang Yu entered Xianyang, and the capital was sacked. The magnificent palaces that Qin Shi Huang had built were burned, and the treasures that had been accumulated over decades were looted. The empire that had been created through such effort was destroyed, and China entered a period of civil war that would last for several years.

## The Aftermath and the Rise of Han

The fall of the Qin dynasty was followed by a period of intense conflict known as the Chu-Han Contention (楚漢之爭), during which Xiang Yu and Liu Bang fought for control of China. This period, which lasted from 206 to 202 BCE, was characterized by shifting alliances, military campaigns, and political maneuvering. Both sides claimed to be the legitimate successors to the Qin, and both had support among the population.

Xiang Yu, as the more powerful military leader, initially seemed likely to win. He had defeated the Qin armies, destroyed the dynasty, and partitioned the empire among his followers. He established himself as the "Hegemon-King of Western Chu" (西楚霸王) and awarded the various former territories to his allies and generals. However, Xiang Yu's political judgment was poor, and he made enemies quickly. He alienated many of the former Qin territories by treating them as conquered lands rather than as partners in the new order.

Liu Bang, by contrast, proved to be the more effective political leader. He had established his base in the eastern region, and he had cultivated the support of the local populations. He promised land reform, reduced taxes, and governed in a relatively benign manner. He built alliances with other rebel leaders and with the former Qin officials who were willing to serve the new regime. His patience and political skill gradually gave him the advantage.

The decisive moment came in 202 BCE, when Liu Bang's forces defeated Xiang Yu at the Battle of Gaixia (垓下之戰). Xiang Yu, surrounded and outnumbered, committed suicide, and Liu Bang became the supreme ruler of China. He established the Han dynasty, with himself as Emperor Gaozu (漢高祖), and began the task of rebuilding the empire that had been destroyed by the collapse of Qin.

## Historical Assessment

The collapse of the Qin dynasty was one of the most dramatic political transformations in Chinese history. In the span of just three years, an empire that had seemed permanent was destroyed, and a new order emerged from the chaos. The events of 209-207 BCE demonstrated the fragility of the Qin regime and the depth of the resentment that had accumulated during its rule.

The causes of the collapse were multiple and complex. The Qin regime had never established legitimacy in the eyes of the conquered peoples, and it had relied on force rather than consent to maintain order. The policies of the Second Emperor and Zhao Gao had accelerated the regime's decline, alienating even those who might have supported the dynasty. The rebellions that broke out were not merely local disturbances but expressions of deep-seated resentment against Qin rule.

The role of individual actors in the collapse was also significant. The Second Emperor's incompetence and Zhao Gao's treachery removed any possibility of reform or recovery. The rebel leaders—Chen Sheng, Xiang Yu, Liu Bang, and others—exploited the weaknesses of the regime to their advantage. The collapse was not inevitable, but it was made much more likely by the decisions of the individuals who shaped the events of the period.

Yet the collapse of Qin also created opportunities. The Han dynasty that emerged from the chaos inherited many of the institutions and practices of the Qin, including the centralized administration, the commandery system, and the standardized writing. The unification that Qin had achieved was preserved, even though the dynasty itself had fallen. The legacy of Qin would continue to shape Chinese civilization for millennia.

### Chapter Notes

The collapse of the Qin dynasty has been the subject of extensive historical analysis. The traditional account, which emphasizes the role of the Second Emperor's incompetence and Zhao Gao's treachery, has been both accepted and challenged by modern scholars. Some historians have argued that the structural weaknesses of the Qin regime were more important than individual failures, while others have emphasized the role of the rebel leaders in shaping the outcome.

The Dazexiang uprising has been the subject of particular interest, as it is often seen as the beginning of the end for Qin. The historical sources describe the events in some detail, but there are gaps and inconsistencies that have been debated by scholars. Some have questioned whether the traditional account gives too much emphasis to the role of Chen Sheng and Wu Guang, arguing that the uprising was part of a broader pattern of resistance.

The Chu-Han Contention is also complex, and the historical sources present conflicting accounts of the relationship between Xiang Yu and Liu Bang. The traditional portrayal of Xiang Yu as a heroic but ultimately tragic figure and Liu Bang as a wise and benevolent founder has been challenged by modern scholars, who have emphasized the political and military dimensions of their conflict.
