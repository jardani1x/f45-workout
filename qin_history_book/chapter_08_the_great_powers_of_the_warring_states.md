# Chapter 8: The Great Powers of the Warring States

The period known as the Warring States (战国时代) represents the final and most intense phase of the political fragmentation that characterized the Eastern Zhou dynasty. Beginning around 475 BCE with the collapse of the Spring and Autumn order, this era witnessed the emergence of seven major powers that dominated the political landscape of the Chinese world until the unification under Qin in 221 BCE. Understanding these states—their distinct political cultures, military traditions, diplomatic strategies, and internal dynamics—is essential to comprehending how Qin was able to achieve supremacy and what obstacles it overcame in the process of unification.

The seven Warring States—Qi (齐), Chu (楚), Yan (燕), Wei (魏), Zhao (赵), Han (韩), and Qin (秦)—represented dramatically different approaches to governance, warfare, and diplomacy. Each had developed its own strengths and weaknesses, its own cultural identity and political traditions. The interactions among these states, shaped by the competing strategies of alliance and confrontation, defined the diplomatic landscape that Qin would navigate on its path to empire.

## The Evolution of the Warring States System

The Warring States period emerged from the collapse of the Spring and春秋 order, in which a weakened Zhou king was supported by a system of vassal states led by a hegemonic power. As the Zhou authority declined further, the vassal states that had formerly acknowledged the king's supremacy began to pursue their own interests with increasing independence. By the fifth century BCE, the political map of China had been redrawn around a handful of large states that competed for dominance without any effective central authority.

The early Warring States period was characterized by a process of consolidation and expansion, as larger states absorbed smaller ones and extended their control over new territories. This process reduced the number of independent states from dozens to seven major powers plus several smaller states that survived as satellites or buffer zones. Each of the seven major states had its own capital, its own administration, its own army, and its own diplomatic relationships.

The period from roughly 350 BCE to 221 BCE saw the emergence of a distinctive balance of power, in which the seven states engaged in complex diplomatic maneuvers designed to prevent any single state from achieving overwhelming dominance. This balance was maintained through a combination of military deterrence, strategic alliances, and the careful manipulation of relationships among the major powers.

The geopolitical center of the Warring States world was the Central Plains (中原), the fertile river valleys of the Yellow River region that had been the heartland of Zhou civilization. The states that controlled this region—Wei, Zhao, Han, and later Qi—were often considered more culturally advanced than the peripheral states, though this assessment was contested. The peripheral states—Chu in the south, Yan in the north, and Qin in the west—were often dismissed as cultural inferiors by the Central Plains states, though they possessed significant military and economic strength.

## Qi: The Eastern Power

Qi (齐) occupied the Shandong Peninsula in eastern China, a region of fertile farmland, developed commerce, and access to the sea. The state had been a major power since the Spring and Autumn period, when it served as one of the hegemonic states that dominated the Zhou political system. By the Warring States period, Qi had developed a distinctive political culture that combined elements of Confucian tradition with practical governance.

The Qi rulers adopted the title of king (王) early, asserting their status as independent sovereigns rather than vassals of the Zhou. The Qi capital at Linzi (临淄) was one of the largest and most prosperous cities of the Warring States world, renowned for its markets and cultural life. The *Guoyu* and *Zuo Zhuan* records describe Qi as a center of philosophical debate and artistic refinement.

The military tradition of Qi emphasized the use of infantry and chariots, reflecting the terrain of the Central Plains where the state primarily operated. Qi generals were known for their tactical skill, and the Qi army was generally considered one of the strongest in the Warring States period. However, Qi was also vulnerable to naval attack from the states of the eastern coast and faced pressure from Qi on its western border.

Qi's diplomatic strategy was often characterized by a degree of caution and pragmatism. The state was slow to engage in the most intense phases of conflict and sometimes preferred to negotiate from a position of neutrality rather than commit to decisive action. This tendency toward caution, while it preserved Qi from the most destructive conflicts, also limited its ability to expand and eventually left it vulnerable to Qin's diplomatic manipulation.

A significant development in Qi's history was the reforms of King Wei of Qi (齐威王), who ruled from 356 to 320 BCE. King Wei implemented administrative reforms that strengthened central control and improved the efficiency of governance. He was also famous for his willingness to listen to honest counsel, including the famous episode in which he rewarded the minister Zou Ji (邹忌) for a candid assessment of the king's weaknesses. These reforms made Qi a more effective state, though they did not transform it as fundamentally as the Legalist reforms transformed Qin.

## Chu: The Southern Giant

Chu (楚) was the largest of the Warring States in terms of territory, controlling the vast region south of the Yangtze River. This territory included rich agricultural lands, extensive forests, and access to the mineral resources of the south. Chu's position on the southern periphery of the Chinese world gave it a distinctive cultural identity that set it apart from the Central Plains states.

The Chu state traced its origins to a confederation of tribal groups in the south, and its rulers claimed descent from the mythical Yan Emperor (炎帝) rather than the Yellow Emperor (黄帝) who was the ancestor of the Central Plains ruling houses. This different genealogical claim was one expression of Chu's distinct identity, which was also reflected in its artistic traditions, religious practices, and political institutions.

The political culture of Chu was characterized by a degree of pluralism that distinguished it from the more centralized states of the Central Plains. The Chu ruler governed through a network of local lords and tribal leaders who retained significant autonomy, and the central government was often weaker than in states like Qin. This decentralized structure gave Chu a degree of resilience—revolts or losses in one region did not immediately threaten the whole state—but it also limited the state's ability to mobilize resources for sustained military campaigns.

Chu's military forces reflected its geographic and cultural context. The armies of Chu included large numbers of infantry, and the state was known for its use of archers and its navy, which operated on the Yangtze River and its tributaries. The Chu military was generally capable but sometimes suffered from the same organizational weaknesses that affected the state's administration.

Chu was a persistent challenger for hegemony in the Warring States period, engaging in frequent conflicts with the Central Plains states. However, Chu also faced challenges from within, including the problem of integrating conquered populations into the Chu political system. The state's size and diversity, while a source of potential strength, also created management challenges that limited its effectiveness.

## Yan: The Northern Frontier

Yan (燕) occupied the northern region around modern-day Beijing and Hebei, controlling a territory that extended to the borders of the nomadic peoples of the steppes. This frontier position gave Yan a distinctive character and shaped its political priorities, which often focused on defense rather than expansion.

The Yan state traced its origins to the early Zhou period, when a member of the Zhou royal house was granted a fief in the north. However, Yan was long considered a marginal state, peripheral to the major centers of Chinese civilization. The *Zuo Zhuan* and other early sources describe Yan as a relatively weak state that was often threatened by more powerful neighbors.

The political culture of Yan reflected its frontier position. The state was known for its military preparedness and its vigilance against the nomadic peoples who threatened the northern border. Yan rulers invested heavily in fortifications and maintained a standing army that was prepared for both external attack and internal defense.

The relationship between Yan and the nomadic peoples of the northern frontier was complex and sometimes involved periods of conflict as well as cooperation. Some scholars have suggested that the Yan developed techniques of cavalry warfare that were influenced by contact with nomadic traditions, though the evidence for this is contested. Whatever the precise nature of this influence, Yan's frontier position clearly shaped its military practices.

Yan's diplomatic position in the Warring States system was often difficult. The state was surrounded by more powerful neighbors—Qi to the south, Zhao to the west, and various nomadic groups to the north—and had to navigate carefully to maintain its independence. Yan's typical strategy was to seek alliances with one powerful neighbor against another, playing off the competing interests of the major states.

One notable episode in Yan history was the alliance with Zhao against Qi, which resulted in the recovery of territory that Yan had lost. Another significant event was the failed attempt by the Yan general Qin Kai (秦开) to invade Qi in the early third century BCE, which ended in disaster and weakened Yan significantly. These episodes illustrate both Yan's ambitions and its limitations as a major power.

## Wei: The Central Power

Wei (魏) was one of the most important states of the Warring States period, occupying a central position in the fertile Central Plains. Wei controlled the region around the Yellow River, including the old Zhou capitals, and was considered one of the most culturally advanced states. Its capital at Daliang (大梁) was a major center of commerce and intellectual life.

The Wei state emerged from the division of the former Jin state in the fifth century BCE and quickly established itself as a major power. The early Wei rulers, particularly King Hui (惠王), who ruled from 370 to 319 BCE, pursued aggressive policies of expansion that brought Wei into conflict with its neighbors. At its height, Wei was arguably the most powerful of the Warring States.

The military tradition of Wei was sophisticated and innovative. The state was known for the quality of its infantry, organized in formations that emphasized discipline and coordination. Wei also developed the "flexible tactics" (太极拳) that would later be adopted by other states, reflecting a sophisticated approach to warfare that sought to combine offensive and defensive capabilities.

The political culture of Wei was characterized by a tradition of strong central government and effective administration. The state employed a system of merit-based advancement that attracted talented officials from throughout the Chinese world, and its bureaucracy was generally considered efficient. The *Wenzi* and other texts describe Wei as a model of good governance.

However, Wei's central position also made it vulnerable to attack from multiple directions. The state was surrounded by powerful neighbors—Qin to the west, Zhao to the north, Han to the south, and Qi to the east—and had to fight wars on multiple fronts. This strategic vulnerability was a persistent problem for Wei and ultimately contributed to its decline.

The decline of Wei began in the mid-fourth century BCE, when Qin defeated Wei in a series of battles that culminated in the Battle of Maling (马陵之战) in 341 BCE. This defeat, which involved the famous stratagem of feigning retreat, cost Wei heavily and marked the beginning of Qin's ascendancy. After this point, Wei was increasingly forced onto the defensive and eventually became a subordinate of Qin.

## Zhao: The Northern Warrior

Zhao (赵) emerged from the same division of Jin as Wei but developed a distinct character shaped by its northern position and its frequent conflicts with nomadic peoples. The Zhao state occupied the territory around modern Shanxi, extending to the borders of the steppes. This frontier position gave Zhao a martial character that distinguished it from the more culturally oriented Central Plains states.

The military forces of Zhao were among the most formidable in the Warring States period. The state was famous for its cavalry, which was developed through contact with the nomadic peoples of the northern frontier. The Zhao cavalry, organized and trained along lines influenced by steppe practices, gave Zhao a military capability that few other states could match. The legendary general Lian Po (廉颇) and the even more famous Zhao Mu (赵武) were products of this martial tradition.

The political culture of Zhao was characterized by a degree of pragmatism and adaptability. The Zhao ruler King Wuling (武灵王), who ruled from 326 to 295 BCE, implemented reforms that adopted nomadic clothing and cavalry tactics, setting aside the ceremonial traditions of the Central Plains in favor of practical military effectiveness. This reform, which was controversial at the time, demonstrated Zhao's willingness to learn from other traditions.

Despite its military strength, Zhao faced significant challenges in the Warring States system. The state's position on the northern frontier meant that it had to maintain expensive defenses against nomadic incursions while also engaging in competition with its human neighbors. The famous general Bai Qi (白起), who would later become one of Qin's greatest commanders, was originally from Zhao but served Qin instead—a reflection of the talent flows that shaped the period.

Zhao's most famous military episode was the defense against Qin in the late third century BCE, when the state resisted Qin's attempts at conquest with remarkable determination. The Battle of Changping (长平之战) in 260 BCE, which resulted in a catastrophic defeat for Zhao and the death of 400,000 Zhao soldiers, marked the effective end of Zhao as a major power. The aftermath of this battle, in which Qin executed the surrendered Zhao soldiers, became one of the most infamous episodes of the Warring States period.

## Han: The Smallest of the Great Powers

Han (韩) was the smallest of the seven Warring States, occupying a relatively small territory in the Central Plains around modern-day Henan. Despite its limited size, Han was a significant power in the Warring States period, in part because of its strategic position at the crossroads of the major states.

The Han state emerged from the division of Jin alongside Wei and Zhao. Its capital at Xianyang (新郑) was a prosperous city, though not as large or influential as the capitals of the major states. Han's territory was fertile and well-developed, but it lacked the resources to compete with the larger states on equal terms.

The military forces of Han were limited by the state's small size, but Han compensated for this weakness through technological innovation and diplomatic skill. Han was known for the production of high-quality weapons, and the state was a center of metallurgical expertise. Han's crossbowmakers were particularly famous, and the state's weapons were sought after by other powers.

The diplomatic strategy of Han was typically characterized by flexibility and adaptation. As the smallest of the major states, Han had to be careful not to provoke stronger neighbors and often had to choose between alignment with one power or another. Han's position at the center of the Warring States system made it a valuable ally and a tempting target.

Han was the first of the Warring States to fall to Qin, in 230 BCE. The state's small size and strategic vulnerability made it difficult to defend, and Qin was able to conquer it relatively quickly once the time was right. The fall of Han marked the beginning of the final phase of Qin's conquests.

## The Balance of Power: Vertical and Horizontal Alliances

The diplomatic system of the Warring States was characterized by the competing strategies known as "vertical alliance" (合纵) and "horizontal alliance" (连横). These terms, which derive from the directional orientation of the alliances on maps of the period, described the two main diplomatic approaches that shaped the interactions among the major states.

The vertical alliance (合纵) referred to the strategy of forming a coalition of states to resist the dominance of a single powerful state. Typically, this meant the alliance of the eastern states—Qi, Zhao, Yan, and sometimes Chu—against Qin, which was the most powerful state in the west. The goal of the vertical alliance was to balance the power of Qin and prevent any single state from achieving hegemony.

The horizontal alliance (连横), by contrast, referred to the strategy of aligning with Qin, typically in exchange for territorial gains or other benefits. States that adopted this strategy hoped to benefit from Qin's strength while avoiding its attacks. The horizontal strategy was often associated with the diplomacy of individual states seeking to improve their position at the expense of their rivals.

These two strategies were not mutually exclusive, and states shifted between them depending on changing circumstances. The same state might pursue vertical alliances at one moment and horizontal alignments at another, depending on the threat environment and the opportunities available. The fluidity of the diplomatic system meant that alliances were constantly being formed, broken, and reformed.

The most famous practitioners of these diplomatic strategies were the itinerant diplomats known as "strategists" (策士), who traveled from state to state offering advice and arranging alliances. The most famous of these was probably Su Qin (苏秦), who advocated the vertical alliance against Qin and succeeded for a time in forming a coalition that constrained Qin's expansion. His counterpart Zhang Yi (张仪) advocated the horizontal strategy and worked to break up coalitions against Qin.

The balance of power that these alliances produced was inherently unstable, and it shifted over time as the relative strength of the major states changed. In the early Warring States period, Qi and Chu were among the most powerful states; by the later period, Qin had emerged as the dominant power. The collapse of the balance of power as Qin grew stronger eventually led to the unification of China under Qin rule.

## The Competition Among the Powers

The Warring States period was characterized by a constant competition among the major powers, driven by the logic of survival in a system where only the strongest states could expect to endure. This competition took many forms—military, diplomatic, economic, and cultural—and shaped the development of each state.

Military competition was the most visible form of the rivalry, as the Warring States engaged in almost constant warfare. The scale of these wars was often enormous, involving tens or even hundreds of thousands of soldiers. Battles could decide the fate of states, as the defeat at Changping demonstrated. The military competition drove innovations in tactics, weapons, and organization, as each state sought to gain an advantage over its rivals.

Diplomatic competition was equally important. The formation and breaking of alliances, the negotiation of treaties, and the manipulation of relationships among the major powers were all crucial to survival. The great diplomats of the period, like Su Qin and Zhang Yi, were among the most influential figures of the age, and their successes and failures shaped the course of history.

Economic competition was a quieter but nonetheless important dimension of the rivalry. States sought to develop their agricultural production, promote commerce, and accumulate wealth that could be used to support military efforts. The Legalist reforms in Qin were partly motivated by the desire to create a more efficient economic system that could support sustained warfare.

Cultural competition was also significant, as states sought to attract talented officials and scholars who could contribute to their strength. The great philosophical schools of the period—Confucian, Mohist, Daoist, Legalist, and others—traveled from state to state, offering their services to rulers who would employ them. The competition for talent was intense, and states that could attract and retain skilled officials gained significant advantages.

## The Road to Unification

The Warring States system was ultimately unsustainable, and the competition among the major powers was resolved by the emergence of Qin as the dominant state. This outcome was not inevitable; it resulted from a combination of Qin's distinctive strengths and the particular circumstances of the late Warring States period.

Qin's Legalist reforms gave it a level of organizational capacity that other states could not match. The centralized administration, the meritocratic bureaucracy, and the discipline of the Qin army gave it advantages that accumulated over time. While other states also implemented reforms, none did so as thoroughly as Qin.

The geographic position of Qin also contributed to its success. The state was protected by natural barriers in the west, which allowed it to focus its military efforts on the east. The fertile Guanzhong region provided the economic base to support sustained warfare, and the state had access to the resources of the frontier.

The diplomatic skill of Qin also played a crucial role. The policy of "allying with distant states while attacking nearby ones" (远交近攻) allowed Qin to isolate its immediate neighbors while building coalitions with more distant powers. This strategy systematically undermined the balance of power and paved the way for conquest.

When the time came for final conquest, Qin was able to defeat each of its rivals in turn, culminating in the unification of China in 221 BCE. The Warring States system had produced one survivor, and the era of fragmentation gave way to the age of empire.

### Chapter Notes

The traditional account of the Warring States period is derived primarily from Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* and from earlier historical works like the *Zuo Zhuan* and the *Guoyu*. These sources provide detailed narratives of the major events and personalities of the period, though modern scholars have noted that they were written from particular perspectives and may not always be accurate.

The concept of "vertical" and "horizontal" alliances is central to the understanding of Warring States diplomacy, but scholars have debated the precise meaning of these terms. Some have argued that the terms referred primarily to the geographic orientation of alliances, while others have suggested that they had more complex meanings related to the goals and strategies of the states involved.

The exact chronology of the Warring States period is sometimes debated, particularly regarding the transition from the Spring and Autumn period to the Warring States. The traditional date of 475 BCE, marking the beginning of the Warring States, corresponds to the death of the Zhou king and the effective end of the Spring and Autumn order, but some scholars have suggested alternative dating systems.

The relative strength of the Warring States shifted over time, and the balance of power was constantly in flux. The traditional narrative often emphasizes the rise of Qin and the decline of the other states, but this perspective can obscure the complexity of the period and the genuine strengths of the other powers.
