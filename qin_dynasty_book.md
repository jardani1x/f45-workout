# The First Emperor: A History of the Qin Dynasty

## Book One: The Rise of Ying Zheng and the Unification of China

---

## Prologue: The Age of Warring States

In the twilight of the Zhou Dynasty (c. 256 BCE), China was fractured into seven major warring states: Qi, Chu, Yan, Han, Zhao, Wei, and Qin. For over two centuries, these kingdoms fought endlessly for supremacy, leaving the land exhausted and the people weary of constant warfare.

It was from this chaos that one man would emerge to forge an empire that would shape Chinese civilization for over two millennia.

---

## Chapter 1: The Qin Kingdom

### Origins of the Qin

The Qin state traced its origins to the western borders of ancient China, among the Rong and Di peoples—non-Chinese tribes that the Zhou dynasties had long considered barbarians. In 897 BCE, a Zhou prince named Feizi was granted the title of "Earl of Qin" and given the task of guarding the western frontier.

Over centuries, the Qin gradually assimilated Zhou culture while developing a reputation for military prowess and administrative efficiency. Located in the fertile Guanzhong region (modern-day Shaanxi), Qin possessed natural defenses—the Yellow River to the east, the Long Mountains to the west, and the Qinling Mountains to the south.

### Legalism: The Philosophy of Power

What distinguished Qin from other states was its embrace of Legalism (法家, Fajia), a philosophy that advocated strict laws, harsh punishments, and centralized state control. Unlike Confucianism, which emphasized moral virtue and hierarchical harmony, Legalism focused on practical governance.

The three primary Legalist thinkers were:

1. **Shang Yang** (c. 390–338 BCE) — Known as the "Father of Qin," he implemented sweeping reforms that transformed Qin into a militarized state
2. **Han Fei** (c. 280–233 BCE) — Theorist who synthesized Legalist thought into a comprehensive system
3. **Li Si** (c. 280–208 BCE) — Philosopher and chancellor who would later serve the First Emperor

Shang Yang's reforms established the foundation upon which Qin would build its empire.

## Chapter 2: The Making of an Emperor

### The Birth of Ying Zheng

In 259 BCE, in the royal palace of Qin, a child named Ying Zheng (嬴政) was born to King Zichu of Qin and a concubine named Zhao Jiao (赵姬). Few could have imagined that this infant would become the first emperor of a unified China.

Ying Zheng's early years were marked by political intrigue and danger. His father, Zichu, was sent to Zhao as a hostage to secure an alliance. When Zichu eventually succeeded as king (r. 250–247 BCE), he was already terminally ill. Within days of taking the throne, King Zichu died, leaving the crown to the young Ying Zheng.

### The Regencies: Lü Buwei and Zhao Gao

At just thirteen years old, Ying Zheng inherited the throne, but real power rested with his chancellor, Lü Buwei (吕不韦). Lü Buwei had been instrumental in placing Zichu on the throne and had even arranged for Zhao Jiao to become the king's concubine—some historians suggest he may have been Ying Zheng's biological father.

Lü Buwei ruled as regent until Ying Zheng came of age, effectively controlling the state for over a decade. During this period, Lü Buwei continued the expansion of Qin's territory and maintained stability.

However, Lü Buwei made a critical mistake. He had an affair with Zhao Jiao, the queen mother, and when rumors spread, he sought to distance himself by finding a new consort for the young king—a woman named Zhao Ji. To further secure his position, Lü Buwei arranged for Zhao Jiao's current lover, Lao Ai, to be castrated and appointed as her bodyguard, allowing them to continue their affair secretly.

This web of intrigue would unravel catastrophically.

### The Rise to Power

In 238 BCE, at the age of twenty-two, Ying Zheng formally took control of the government. His first act was to execute Lao Ai and purge his supporters. The scandal ensnared Lü Buwei, who was stripped of his titles and later forced to commit suicide in 237 BCE.

With Lü Buwei gone, Ying Zheng now ruled Qin's destiny himself. But he was not alone—two figures would shape his reign: Li Si, a Legalist scholar who became his chief advisor, and Zhao Gao, a cunning eunuch who served as his personal attendant.

---

## Chapter 3: The Unification Wars

### The Strategy of Conquest

With full power in his hands, Ying Zheng pursued the ultimate goal: unifying all of China. His generals and advisors developed a systematic approach:

1. **Weaken the alliances** — Use diplomacy and bribery to fracture the coalitions between other states
2. **Isolate and conquer** — Attack states one by one, preventing them from uniting against Qin
3. **Prioritize the weak** — Eliminate the smaller, weaker states first before facing the powerful ones

### The Fall of Han (230 BCE)

The first state to fall was Han, the smallest of the seven warring states. In 230 BCE, Qin forces captured the Han capital of Xianyang, ending the Han dynasty's 400-year rule. The territory was reorganized as the Qin commandery of Sanchong.

### The Fall of Wei (225 BCE)

Next came Wei. In 225 BCE, Qin general Wang Xin led an army to attack Wei's capital of Daliang, which was protected by a massive water defense system. Wang Xin employed a cunning strategy: he dammed the Yellow River and flooded the city. After three months of flooding, Daliang collapsed, and Wei fell.

Legend claims that Wei's king, Jia, committed suicide rather than surrender.

### The Fall of Chu (223–222 BCE)

Chu, the largest and most powerful southern state, proved more challenging. In 223 BCE, Qin forces under the command of Li Tai and Wang Jian decisively defeated Chu at the Battle of Feng. The Chu king was captured, and the state ceased to exist.

### The Fall of Yan (222 BCE)

Yan, located in modern-day Beijing and Hebei, was next. In 222 BCE, Qin forces captured Yan's capital of Ji (near modern Beijing). The Yan prince, Jia, fled to the Liaodong Peninsula but was eventually captured.

### The Fall of Zhao (228 BCE)

Zhao, Qin's most formidable military rival, had long resisted Qin expansion. In 230 BCE, Qin invaded, and after years of warfare, the Zhao capital of Handan fell in 228 BCE. The Zhao king was captured and later died in Qin captivity.

### The Fall of Qi (221 BCE)

Qi, the easternmost state, attempted to remain neutral as its neighbors fell. In 221 BCE, Qin turned its attention to Qi. With no allies to call upon, Qi surrendered without a fight. The Qi king was exiled, and the state was absorbed into Qin.

### The Birth of an Empire

By 221 BCE, the seven warring states had been conquered. For the first time in Chinese history, the land was unified under a single ruler.

In 221 BCE, Ying Zheng proclaimed himself **Qin Shi Huang** (秦始皇帝)—"First Emperor of Qin"—a title that declared he would be the first of a line of emperors that would last "eternally" (万世, wan shi).

## Chapter 4: The Emperor's Reforms

### Standardization

One of Qin Shi Huang's most lasting contributions was standardization. He decreed:

- **Script** — A unified written script replaced the various scripts used by different states
- **Weights and Measures** — Standard units of weight, length, and volume across the empire
- **Currency** — The ban liang (半两) coin became the universal currency
- **Road Widths** — Standard widths for roads to ensure carriage compatibility

These reforms, though implemented through brutal enforcement, created the foundation for a unified Chinese civilization.

### The Great Wall

To protect against northern invasions from the Xiongnu, Qin Shi Huang ordered the connection and extension of existing defensive walls built by the warring states. This became the precursor to the Great Wall of China.

The construction was brutal. Hundreds of thousands of workers—soldiers, peasants, and prisoners—died from exhaustion, malnutrition, and abuse. The dead were reportedly buried in the wall itself.

### The Road Network

Qin Shi Huang ordered the construction of a national road system to facilitate military movement and trade. The Five Great Roads (五大道) connected the empire's major cities, allowing rapid troop deployment and communication.

### Administrative Divisions

The empire was divided into **36 commanderies** (郡, jun), each ruled by a governor, assistant, and military commander. Below the commanderies were **counties** (县, xian), creating a hierarchical administrative structure that would persist for centuries.

## Chapter 5: The Dark Side of the Emperor

### Legalist Brutality

Qin Shi Huang's Legalist philosophy, while efficient, was merciless. The legal code was harsh:

- **Collective punishment** — Families and neighbors could be held responsible for an individual's crimes
- **Public executions** — Dissidents were executed publicly to deter opposition
- **Thought control** — Criticism of the government was punishable by death

### The Burning of Books

In 213 BCE, Qin Shi Huang ordered the burning of all historical records and philosophical texts, with the exception of works on agriculture, medicine, and divination. The goal was to eliminate dissenting ideas and establish the Qin legal code as the sole source of knowledge.

Only the imperial archives were permitted to keep copies. According to historical accounts, approximately 460 scholars were buried alive for opposing this decree—though modern historians debate the accuracy of this figure.

### The Assassin's Blade

In 227 BCE, before unification was complete, an event occurred that would haunt the emperor. Crown Prince Dan of Yan sent an assassin named Jing Ke to murder Qin Shi Huang. Posing as a traitor offering territory maps, Jing Ke attempted to stab the emperor with a poisoned dagger.

The assassination failed. Qin Shi Huang survived, and in retaliation, he devastated the state of Yan. This attempt on his life intensified the emperor's paranoia and contributed to his increasingly repressive policies.

---

## Chapter 6: The Search for Immortality

### The Obsession with Eternal Life

As Qin Shi Huang aged, he became increasingly obsessed with achieving immortality. This obsession was fueled by:

1. **Fear of death** — The first emperor, who had conquered all of China, could not accept that he would die
2. **Magicians and Alchemists** — Court alchemists promised elixirs of life made from mercury and other substances
3. **The Legend of the Immortals** — Myths spoke of islands in the eastern sea where immortals lived

### The Imperial Tours

Qin Shi Huang embarked on five grand tours of his empire, supposedly to inspect his territories but also to seek immortality. He traveled extensively throughout China, erecting inscribed stone tablets celebrating his achievements.

### The Terracotta Army

To protect himself in the afterlife, Qin Shi Huang ordered the creation of the Terracotta Army (兵马俑)—a massive collection of life-sized pottery soldiers, horses, and chariots buried near his mausoleum.

Discovered in 1974 by farmers digging a well, the Terracotta Army is one of archaeology's greatest finds. Over 8,000 soldiers, 130 chariots, and 670 horses have been excavated, each with unique facial features.

The army was meant to serve the emperor in the afterlife, continuing his conquest and protecting his realm.

---

## Chapter 7: The Decline

### The Emperor's paranoia

In his later years, Qin Shi Huang became increasingly paranoid. He feared assassination and rarely slept in the same palace two nights in a row. He surrounded himself with guards and took elaborate security precautions.

### The Court Intrigues

The emperor's inner circle was consumed by factional struggles. Two main factions competed for influence:

1. **The Legalists** — Led by Li Si, they advocated continued harsh rule and strict Legalist policies
2. **The Confucianists** — A small group who urged more moderate policies

The struggle between these factions would have consequences after the emperor's death.

### Zhao Gao's Rise

Zhao Gao, the eunuch who had served the emperor for years, cunningly maneuvered himself into a position of immense power. He became the emperor's closest advisor and used his position to eliminate rivals and accumulate authority.

---

## Chapter 8: The Death of the First Emperor

### The Final Tour

In 210 BCE, Qin Shi Huang embarked on his fifth and final tour of the empire, accompanied by his favorite son, Huhai, and a large entourage. The party traveled through the eastern provinces, descending to the coast where the emperor made sacrifices to the sea.

### The Poison Takes Its Toll

The emperor had been consuming mercury-based "immortality elixirs" for years. The accumulated mercury in his system was slowly killing him. By the time of this tour, his health was failing severely.

### The Death at Shaqiu

On September 10, 210 BCE (by the Gregorian calendar), Qin Shi Huang died at the age of 49 at the palace in Shaqiu (沙丘), located in modern-day Hebei Province.

The emperor's death was kept secret. His inner circle—led by Zhao Gao and Li Si—feared that news of his death would trigger rebellion. They sealed the emperor's body in a carriage and continued as if nothing had happened, bringing provisions and appearing to attend to the emperor's needs.

### The Secret Burial

According to historical accounts, the emperor's body was placed in a carriage sealed with fish to mask the smell of decomposition. For days, the court continued its pretense while the emperor's body began to decay.

The truth was concealed for over a month until the imperial procession returned to the capital at Xianyang.

---

## Chapter 9: The Succession Crisis

### The Forged Edict

Before his death, Qin Shi Huang had written a letter to his eldest son, Fusu (扶苏), naming him successor. However, the letter was never sent. Zhao Gao, who had allied with Huhai, conspired to place Huhai on the throne.

Zhao Gao and Li Si forged an edict in the emperor's name:

1. **Accusing Fusu of disobedience** — The forged edict claimed Fusu had failed in his duties
2. **Ordering Fusu to commit suicide** — The letter commanded Fusu to kill himself
3. **Appointing Huhai as heir** — Huhai was named successor

The edict was sent with the imperial seal. Fusu, believing it to be genuine, obeyed and committed suicide. His general, Meng Tian, was also ordered to die.

### Huhai Takes the Throne

With Fusu out of the way, Huhai became the second emperor of Qin, known as **Er Shi Huang** (Second Emperor). In reality, Zhao Gao held the power, manipulating the young emperor as a puppet.

---

## Chapter 10: The Collapse

### The Weight of Rule

Er Shi Huang (Huhai) was ill-prepared for the responsibilities of empire. Without Zhao Gao's manipulation, he might have been a competent ruler. Instead, he became a tool of the eunuch's ambitions.

### The Rebellions Begin

The brutal policies of Qin Shi Huang had created deep resentment among the population. Now, with the strong hand of the First Emperor removed, that resentment exploded.

**Chen Sheng and Wu Guang** — In 209 BCE, two army officers named Chen Sheng and Wu Guang led a rebellion of conscripted peasants. Though the rebellion was quickly suppressed, it sparked a chain reaction.

**The Xiang Yu and Liu Bang Uprisings** — Two powerful warlords emerged: Xiang Yu, a heroic figure from Chu, and Liu Bang, a peasant-turned-warlord from沛. Their armies grew rapidly, threatening Qin rule.

### The Fall of Xianyang

In 207 BCE, rebel forces reached the Qin capital of Xianyang. Zhao Gao, now terrified, ordered Er Shi Huang to commit suicide and attempted to negotiate with the rebels.

The second emperor was killed, and Zhao Gao installed a puppet ruler. But the facade could not last.

### The End of Qin

In late 207 BCE, the last Qin emperor surrendered to Liu Bang. The Qin dynasty, which had unified China and ruled with absolute power, had fallen in just **14 years**.

Zhao Gao was killed by his own allies. The Qin imperial family was exterminated.

---

## Epilogue: The Legacy of Qin Shi Huang

### The Han Succession

The collapse of Qin led to a new power struggle between Xiang Yu and Liu Bang. After four years of war, Liu Bang emerged victorious and founded the Han Dynasty (206 BCE), which would rule China for over 400 years.

The Han Dynasty inherited much from Qin:

- The administrative system of commanderies and counties
- The unified script
- The concept of the emperor as supreme ruler

### The Verdict on Qin Shi Huang

History has judged Qin Shi Huang harshly. Confucian scholars, who dominated Chinese historiography, depicted him as a tyrant—a brutal conqueror who destroyed culture and enslaved his people.

Yet his achievements were undeniable:

1. **Unification** — He united China for the first time
2. **Standardization** — His reforms created a cohesive civilization
3. **Infrastructure** — Roads, canals, and the Great Wall transformed the empire
4. **The Terracotta Army** — A testament to his ambition and artistic vision

Modern scholars offer more nuanced assessments. Qin Shi Huang was a product of his time—a ruthless leader who employed brutal methods but accomplished what no one else could. His Legalist policies were severe, but they were also remarkably effective at creating order from chaos.

### The Eternal Emperor

Qin Shi Huang's ambition was to rule for "ten thousand generations" (万世). Though his dynasty lasted less than two decades, his legacy endures. The imperial system he created would persist in China for over 2,000 years.

His tomb, still unopened in the mound at Lintong, remains one of history's greatest mysteries. Some believe it contains treasures beyond imagination; others fear the deadly traps that may await. For now, the First Emperor rests, as he intended, with his terracotta army, guarding his eternal sleep.

## Glossary

- **Ban liang** (半两): Qin Dynasty coinage
- **Commandery** (郡, jun): Administrative division in imperial China
- **Er Shi Huang** (二世皇帝): Second Emperor of Qin (Huhai)
- **Fusu** (扶苏): Eldest son of Qin Shi Huang, named heir
- **Han Fei** (韩非): Legalist philosopher
- **Huhai** (胡亥): Second son of Qin Shi Huang, became Second Emperor
- **Legalism** (法家, Fajia): School of thought emphasizing strict laws
- **Li Si** (李斯): Chancellor under Qin Shi Huang
- **Lü Buwei** (吕不韦): Chancellor and regent
- **Qin Shi Huang** (秦始皇帝): First Emperor of Qin
- **Shang Yang** (商鞅): Legalist reformer, "Father of Qin"
- **Xianyang** (咸阳): Capital of Qin Dynasty
- **Zhao Gao** (赵高): Eunuch who manipulated the court
- **Zhao Jiao** (赵姬): Mother of Qin Shi Huang
